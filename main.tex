\begin{abstract}
Sorghum is a globally important cereal crop whose genetic diversity underlies its ability to adapt to nutrient deficiency and temperature stress, traits that are increasingly relevant under climate change. Lipids play a major role combating these stresses. However, population-scale lipidomic datasets remain scarce. Here, we describe the Sorghum Lipid Database (SoLD), a curated lipidomics resource derived from the Sorghum Association Panel (SAP; ~380 accessions) profiled at an early developmental stage in two contrasting field conditions: a conventional, nutrient-sufficient control and a low-input, early-planted treatment imposing combined nitrogen and phosphorus limitation together with temperature-related stress. Using high-resolution liquid chromatography–mass spectrometry, we quantified 244 lipid species across both environments and detected a reproducible lipidome reprogramming under low-input conditions. This remodeling was characterized by marked depletion of sulfoquinovosyldiacylglycerols (SQDG), enrichment of triacylglycerols (TG), redistribution within major phospholipid pools which includes a phosphatidylserine (PS)-centered remodeling, and a coordinated lysophospholipid adjustments, reflected in shifts in lysophosphatidylcholine (LPC)/lysophosphatidylethanolamine (LPE) ratios. Genome-wide association studies (GWAS) conducted on low-input individual lipid species as well as on class-level sums and their ratios highlighted hundreds of candidate loci. These included regulatory modules in glycerol-backbone and lipid biosynthesis, diacylglycerol (DG)–TG partitioning, phospholipid turnover, and regulatory pathways associated with nutrient and cold stress responses. Furthermore, gradient-boosted decision tree models coupled with SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP)-based interpretation revealed that TG-enriched lipid modules provide robust predictive power for plant height, whereas flowering time is only weakly predictable from single-stage lipidomic profiles. All the information regarding the database and the experiment can be freely accessible through a Shiny application: https://github.com/sawers-rellan-labs/SAP-Lipidomics-Database.
\end{abstract}

\lettrine[lines=2]{\color{color2}S}orghum (\textit{Sorghum bicolor} L.) is a major C\textsubscript{4} cereal and bioenergy crop, grown for its resilience in environmentally harsh conditions. Its ability to sustain productivity under conditions of cold stress and nutrient limitation highlights its growing significance for global food and energy security (\cite{Paterson2009}; \cite{Ngara2014} ; \cite{George2022}). Furthermore, sorghum contains substantial standing genetic variation shaped by its domestication history and wide geographic distribution. Diversity panels, such as the Sorghum Association Panel (SAP) offer a robust experimental framework for linking genotype with agronomic phenotypes via genome-wide association studies and multi-environment analyses (\cite{Boatwright2022}).

Plants are routinely exposed to adverse environmental conditions that constrain crop productivity, with nutrient deficiencies, particularly of nitrogen (N) and phosphorus (P), and low-temperature stress representing some of the most prevalent limitations (\cite{Schluter2012}; \cite{Silveira2018}; \cite{Emendack2021}). N limitation profoundly modifies root system architecture and N acquisition strategies, whereas P scarcity constitutes an escalating challenge to global agriculture due to finite mineral P reserves and the inherently low efficiency of fertilizer use (\cite{Nacry2013}; \cite{LopezValdivia2025}; \cite{Medici2019}). Cold stress, including chilling (0–15\textbackslash{}textdegree C) and freezing (<0<0<0\textbackslash{}textdegree C) temperatures, disrupts cell membrane fluidity, slows metabolism, and can cause physiological damage that reduces yield (\cite{Ding2019}; \cite{Guo2025}). Together, these abiotic stressors trigger acclimation programs that involve substantial membrane reorganization and broad remodeling of lipid metabolism. (\cite{Bohn2007}; \cite{Siebers2015}; \cite{Xu2025}).

Lipids occupy a central position in plant stress physiology, functioning as fundamental structural constituents of cellular membranes, reservoirs of metabolic energy, and precursors for diverse signaling molecules. Stress-induced lipid remodeling helps stabilize membranes, adjust photosynthetic performance, and reshape cellular signaling, and it is a conserved response to both nutrient limitation and low-temperature stress (\cite{Hou2016}; \cite{Rawat2021}; \cite{Li2020}; \cite{Bhattacharya2022}). For instance, phosphate  starvation responses are coordinated by conserved transcriptional regulatory networks centered on Phosphate Starvation Response1 (\textit{PHR1}) and related transcription factors (\cite{Rubio2001};\cite{Bustos2010}). This regulatory framework is tightly integrated with alterations in membrane lipid composition and the reprogramming of lipid metabolic pathways. Despite their importance, population-scale lipidomics resources in plants remain limited compared to transcriptomic datasets, particularly concerning datasets that are quantitatively rigorous, computationally interoperable, and explicitly connected to natural genetic variation and agronomically relevant phenotypes.

Two challenges constrain interpretability and reusability in large-scale lipidomics investigations. First, liquid chromatography–mass spectrometry (LC–MS) measurements are prone to systematic signal drift, batch effects, and spatial artifacts arising from field layout. Quality control (QC)-based normalization strategies, such as systematic error removal using random forests (SERRF), can substantially attenuate technical variance when QC sample design is appropriate (\cite{Fan2019}). In parallel, spatial mixed-modeling frameworks such as SpATS accommodate field-position effects by modeling spatial trends with two-dimensional penalized splines (\cite{RodriguezAlvarez2018}). Second, lipid annotation practices and nomenclature remain heterogeneous across studies. Community platforms such as the Global Natural Products Social Molecular Networking (GNPS) environment facilitate spectral library matching and collaborative curation (\cite{Wang2016}), whereas ontology-based resources such as LION/web support enrichment analyses over lipid classes and physicochemical attributes (\cite{Molenaar2019}). These methodological advances provide a foundation for lipidomics datasets that are analytically robust and readily comparable across experiments.

Here, we present the Sorghum Lipid Database (SoLD), a curated resource that links annotated lipid species and class-level lipid programs to genetic variation and agronomic traits in the SAP. Using leaf samples from [developmental stage], we quantified lipid profiles across two contrasting field environments: an optimal control condition with sufficient nutrients and the usual planting date, and a combined low-N, low-P, and cold-stress treatment. At the population level, the low-input regime elicited robust and reproducible lipid remodeling signatures, characterized by sulfoquinovosyldiacylglycerol (SQDG) depletion, triacylglycerol (TG) enrichment, redistribution of phosphatidylserine (PS) within the phospholipid network, and coordinated remodeling of lysophospholipids (LPC/LPE). These patterns indicate coordinated redistribution across interconnected lipid pools rather than isolated changes within single lipid classes. We further integrated these biochemical profiles with genome-wide association analyses to prioritize candidate loci spanning phosphate-starvation regulators, N metabolism, key steps in lipid biosynthesis, and enzymes involved in lipid partitioning and phospholipid turnover. This framework yields genetically informed hypotheses for the regulation of lipid remodeling under combined nutrient and temperature stress. SoLD delivers standardized outputs that include: (i) compositional and ratio-based values of lipid classes and molecular species; (ii) genome-wide association results derived from linear mixed models (\cite{Zhou2012}); and (iii) predictive models of agronomic traits using gradient-boosted trees (\cite{Chen2016}) with SHAP-based model interpretation (\cite{Lundberg2017}). Finally, we show that triglyceride-enriched lipid modules function as reproducible predictors of plant height across independent datasets, supporting the utility of lipids as  biomarkers.

\section{Results}

\subsection*{Quality control and normalization of LC-MS features}

[NOTE: Should this go in supplementary?]

We profiled early-stage lipids from SAP grown under two contrasting field conditions: (i) a control field with sufficient nutrients and a normal planting date (CTR), and (ii) a low-input field (LIN), defined by reduced N and P fertilizers and an earlier planting date to mimic cold stress. The experimental workflow is outlined in Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1_lipidomics_landscape}A, including tissue collection and extraction, high-resolution LC-MS-based lipidomic profiling, GNPS-assisted lipid annotation, and post-acquisition data correction procedures through SERRF and SpATS (\cite{Fan2019}; \cite{RodriguezAlvarez2018}), as well as dissemination via the SoLD Shiny application available through https://github.com/sawers-rellan-labs/SAP-Lipidomics-Database.

Run-order quality control indicated stable analytical performance in both experimental conditions (Supplementary Figure~\ref{fig:suppfig_s1_qc} A,B). Throughout the injection sequence, sample TICs conformed to expectations based on system suitability checks, internal standards, and blanks. TIC variability among QC samples also remained low (CTL QC TIC CV = 7.5\%; LIN QC TIC CV = 6.0\%; Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s1_qc} A,B). SERRF normalization enhanced technical precision in both environments, reducing the median QC-RSD from 3.6\% to 2.0\% in the CTL, and from 1.6\% to 0.3\% in the LIN condition ( Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s1_qc} C,D). Consistently, the proportion of features satisfying a QC-RSD $<30\%$ threshold increased from 75.7\% to 94.1\% in CTL and from 77.3\% to 89.3\% in LIN (Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s1_qc}.~C,D). Furthermore, PCA of normalized data revealed a more compact clustering of samples relative to the unprocessed signal (Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s1_qc}~E,F). Given that samples were derived from field-grown material, we further accounted for field-position effects using spatial modeling (SpATS) in R. Residual maps indicated that broad-scale row and column structures were largely mitigated following correction (Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s1_qc}~G,H). Altogether, these procedures generated a normalized, spatially corrected data that was used for all downstream analyses.

\subsection*{Lipid variation across genotypes}

To determine whether the observed lipid differences between CTL and LIN reflected systematic, population-wide lipidomic reprogramming rather than being driven by a small number of outlier genotypes, we conducted two-sample Wilcoxon rank-sum tests coupled with a leave-one-out jackknife stability analysis. For lipid class-level comparisons, we analyzed pairwise log-ratios computed from class-level summaries. All 33 evaluated class log-ratios exhibited statistically significant differences between conditions (BH-adjusted p<0.05, \cite{Benjamini1995}), each with maximal jackknife stability (1.0; Supplementary TableS1). At the individual lipid species level, we applied a centered log-ratio (CLR) transformation prior to inferential testing to appropriately handle compositional constraints (\cite{Aitchison1982}). Among the 172 lipid species shared between conditions, 170 (99\%) differed significantly (Supplementary Table\~S2).. Among the 172 lipid species shared between conditions, 170 (99\%) differed significantly (Supplementary Table S2). All 172 species displayed perfect jackknife stability (1.0), demonstrating that the direction of each species-level change was invariant to the exclusion of any single genotype. This pattern was consistent across lipid classes (Supplementary Table\~S3), indicating that the lipidomic remodeling observed under LIN conditions represents a robust and uniform response across the SAP genotypes examined, rather than being driven by a limited subset of outlier accessions.

Given this robustness, we next evaluated which lipids exhibited the highest genetic variance. MGDG(18:3/18:3) was the highest-variance lipid in both environments (Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s2_topvar} A,B; Supplementary Table~S4). Seven of the ten highest-variance lipids were shared between CTL and LIN (Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s2_topvar} C), suggesting that a conserved subset of lipid traits exhibits strong genotype sensitivity across environments. Within this high-variance subset, PC species were the most frequent class in both conditions (CTL: 7/10; LIN: 6/10; Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s2_topvar} S2D; Supplementary Table S4), supporting their prioritization for subsequent genetic mapping and predictive modeling analyses. 

\subsection{Lipidome overview for control and low-input SAP}

Across both environments, we quantified 244 distinct lipid species, with substantial overlap between conditions (153 shared species) and smaller environment-specific subsets (49 CTL-exclusive; 42 LIN-exclusive; Supplementary Fig.~S4A; Supplementary Table~S6 A,B,C). In terms of species richness, TG and PC were the most diverse classes in each environment (TG: 71 in CTL, 69 in LIN; PC: 39 in CTL, 38 in LIN; Supplementary Table~S6 A,B,C), and most annotated species fell within the broader glycerolipid and glycerophospholipid superclasses (Supplementary Fig.~S4B,D). PCA further indicated that the multivariate structure of the dataset was strongly driven by lipid class, with TGs separating from membrane lipid classes and galactolipids forming distinct clusters under both conditions (Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s5_pca} A,B).

In contrast, when considering abundance at the class level (\%TIC), the lipidome was dominated by galactolipids and phospholipids, with DG and TG comprising smaller but responsive fractions (Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s3_comp} A). Under CTL conditions, MGDG accounted for 34.5\% of mean TIC and declined to 32.1\% under LIN, whereas PC remained comparatively stable (25.6\% in CTL versus 24.8\% in LIN). TG increased from 2.7\% (CTL) to 5.4\% (LIN), while SQDG decreased from 2.9\% (CTL) to 1.4\% (LIN) (Supplementary Fig.~S3A). Compositional contrast analyses corroborated these trends and further revealed coordinated headgroup rebalancing within the phospholipid network. CLR contrasts highlighted PS as one of the strongest positively shifted classes (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1_lipidomics_landscape}B; Supplementary Table~S5 A), and TG-referenced ALR contrasts showed PS increasing relative to multiple phospholipid pools (e.g., PC, PE, and PG) even when absolute \%TIC changes were modest (Supplementary Table~S5 B, Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s3_comp}B). Moreover, lysophospholipids exhibited a reproducible remodeling signal, with LPC/LPE behavior shifting under LIN in a manner consistent with stress-responsive phospholipid turnover ( \cite{Hou2016}; \cite{Rawat2021}) rather than uniform scaling of total lyso-lipid abundance (Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s3_comp}B; Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1_lipidomics_landscape}; Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s7_ratios}). Together, these results support four LI-associated lipid signatures: (i) SQDG depletion, (ii) TG enrichment, (iii) PS enrichment relative to other phospholipid headgroups, and (iv) lysophospholipid remodeling (LPC/LPE).

To characterize structural remodeling beyond total class abundance, we summarized each lipid class in a reduced chemical space defined by the abundance-weighted mean total carbon number and abundance-weighted mean double-bond count across its constituent molecular species (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1_lipidomics_landscape}C). In this representation, a displacement of a class centroid reflects a shift in the within-class mixture of species (e.g., relative enrichment of longer- versus shorter-chain species), rather than a change in absolute signal alone. TG occupied the high-carbon region of this space and exhibited a clear shift toward higher weighted mean total carbon under LIN, with comparatively modest changes in average unsaturation (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1_lipidomics_landscape}C), indicating the preferential enrichment of longer-chain TG species in the stress condition. Moreover, PE showed the most pronounced upward shift along the unsaturation axis among membrane-associated classes (approximately 2.68 to 3.38 weighted mean double bonds, with only a small carbon shift), consistent with remodeling toward relatively more unsaturated PE species under LIN. We interpret these centroid shifts as descriptive evidence of coordinated remodeling of acyl-chain composition accompanying the class-level abundance changes (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1_lipidomics_landscape}B), without implying specific enzymatic fluxes. In contrast, most remaining classes retained distinct, class-specific carbon/unsaturation signatures with limited centroid movement, consistent with tighter compositional constraints on major membrane-associated lipid pools.

Consistent with these shifts, LION enrichment analysis highlighted ontology terms that increased under LIN, reflecting neutral-lipid accumulation and storage biology, including \textit{triacylglycerols [GL0301]}, \textit{glycerolipids [GL]}, \textit{diacylglycerols [GL0201]}, \textit{lipid storage}, and \textit{lipid droplet} (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1_lipidomics_landscape}D) (\cite{Molenaar2019}). In contrast, ontology terms enriched in CTL were dominated by membrane phospholipid categories and membrane descriptors, including \textit{glycerophospholipids [GP]}, \textit{glycerophosphocholines [GP01]/diacylglycerophosphocholines [GP0101]}, \textit{glycerophosphoethanolamines [GP02]/diacylglycerophosphoethanolamines [GP0201]}, and \textit{membrane component} (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1_lipidomics_landscape}D). Together, LION corroborates that LIN is associated with a coordinated shift toward TG alongside phospholipid/membrane remodeling captured by both abundance contrasts and acyl-chain chemical-space movement.

\subsection*{Ratio-based multivariate analysis reveals coordinated lipid class transitions under low-input}

To characterize coordinated reallocations among major lipid pools, rather than just absolute changes in individual lipid species, we aggregated them into broader lipid classes (e.g., galactolipids, phospholipids, lysophospholipids, and neutral glycerolipids) and analyzed all pairwise log-ratios between them. This log-ratio–based framework is motivated by three considerations. First, LC–MS signal intensities are frequently affected by sample-wide multiplicative factors (e.g., variation in injection volume or ionization efficiency) (Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s1_qc} A,B) (\cite{Broadhurst2018}; \cite{Fan2019}). Ratios are invariant to such global scaling and are therefore less sensitive to these shared effects (Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s6_pcor} A,B) (\cite{Barnes2022}). Second, log-ratios directly capture compositional trade-offs among lipid pools (e.g., TG relative to membrane lipids or PS relative to other phospholipids), which are often more interpretable than analyzing each class in isolation (\cite{Greenacre2020}). Third, many lipidomic responses to nutrient or abiotic stress are expected to involve coordinated shifts  (\cite{Pandey2025}; \cite{Barnes2022}). Class-level log-ratios provide a concise and interpretable summary of these relative changes without imposing assumptions about the directionality or causality of specific biochemical conversions .

An OPLS-DA model (\cite{Trygg2002}) constructed from these ratios, as expected, discriminated CTL from LIN samples along the predictive component (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig2_opls_da} A), indicating that the LIN lipidome is characterized by a rebalancing among lipid classes. Model diagnostics indicated robust classification performance (1 predictive and 1 orthogonal component; $Q^2_{\mathrm{cum}}=0.977$, $R^2Y_{\mathrm{cum}}=0.977$, $R^2X_{\mathrm{cum}}=0.643$; Fig.~\ref{fig:fig2_opls_da}D), and a label-permutation test (n=200) located the observed model performance at the extreme tail of the corresponding null distributions (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig2_opls_da}C), consistent with a stable, non-overfitted multivariate pattern. VIP-ranked ratios (VIP $>$ 1) revealed a highly structured pattern delineating which lipid pools most strongly discriminate CTL from LIN samples (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig2_opls_da}B; Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s7_ratios}A; Supplementary Table~S7). First, many of the highest-ranking ratios were anchored on SQDG (e.g., MG/SQDG, PS/SQDG, PG/SQDG, PE/SQDG, PC/SQDG, LPE/SQDG), and these ratios were consistently elevated in LIN (Supplementary Fig.~\ref{fig:suppfig_s7_ratios}A), indicating a depletion of SQDG relative to multiple membrane-associated and neutral lipid pools. This observation is concordant with the class-level profile, in which SQDG abundance decreases under LIN conditions, whereas several other lipid classes (such as TG and, to a lesser extent, PS) exhibit relative enrichment (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1_lipidomics_landscape}B; Supplementary Fig.~\ref{fig:suppfig_s3_comp}). Second, PS emerged as a central node in the remodeling landscape as multiple ratios with PS in the denominator (PC/PS, PE/PS, PG/PS, PA/PS, DG/PS, DGDG/PS, LPC/PS) were elevated in CTL (Supplementary ~\ref{fig:suppfig_s7_ratios}A), implying that the LIN condition is characterized by increased PS abundance relative to a broad spectrum of other lipid classes, especially phospholipids. Third, lysophospholipid ratios provided an additional, reproducible discriminator of CTL versus LIN. In particular, the LPC/LPE ratio was consistently lower under LIN (Supplementary Fig.\ref{fig:suppfig_s7_ratios}A), indicating a shift in lysophospholipid balance toward higher LPE relative to LPC. This pattern is consistent with condition-dependent remodeling within phospholipid turnover rather than uniform scaling of total lysophospholipid abundance. It also complements the PS-centered headgroup redistribution by indicating that LIN effects extend beyond bulk phospholipid classes to include their lysophospholipid intermediates. Together with the SQDG- and PS-anchored ratios, these lysophospholipid contrasts support a LIN-associated remodeling axis spanning sulfolipid depletion, phospholipid headgroup rebalancing, and altered lysophospholipid partitioning.

While ratio data do not directly measure metabolic flux, the direction-consistent behavior of these ratios supports a coherent LIN program in which plastid-associated glycolipids/sulfolipids are reduced relative to neutral storage and selected membrane pools.

To further delve into how LIN reshapes lipids beyond the VIP-ranked ratios, we computed pairwise Pearson correlation coefficients in CLR-transformed space (Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s6_pcor} A,B). Relative to CTL, the LIN-derived network displayed pronounced rewiring of lipid-class coordination, with several of the most substantial alterations involving phospholipid, lysophospholipid, and neutral-lipid interactions. Notably, three lipid-class pairs underwent complete sign reversals while remaining statistically significant in both environments. PC--PS shifted from a positive association in CTL to a negative association in LIN (\(r:\,+0.45\rightarrow-0.34\)), PS--TG shifted from negative to positive (\(r:\,-0.26\rightarrow+0.43\)), and LPC--PC shifted from negative to positive (\(r:\,-0.41\rightarrow+0.18\); Supplementary  \ref{fig:suppfig_s6_pcor}). These reversals are not minor perturbations around zero, rather, they correspond to some of the largest \(\Delta r\) values, indicating that LIN changes the direction of coordinated variation among these lipid pools rather than simply weakening existing associations.

These rewiring patterns corroborate the results previously inferred from the OPLS-DA analysis and the lipid ratio statistics. The PC--PS reversal is concordant with the VIP structure, in which multiple PS-denominator ratios (PC/PS, PE/PS, PG/PS, PA/PS, DG/PS, DGDG/PS, LPC/PS) are elevated in CTL. This pattern reiterates that the LIN condition is characterized by an increase in PS abundance relative to a broad membrane-lipid background (Supplementary Fig.~\ref{fig:suppfig_s7_ratios}A). Similarly, the PS–TG relationship differs between environments. In CTL, PS and TG are inversely associated, whereas in LIN they vary in the same direction relative to the rest of the lipidome. This behavior is consistent with the observed elevation in TG abundance and the enrichment of PS-centered ratios under LIN (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1_lipidomics_landscape}; Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s3_comp}). The LPC--PC reversal is likewise consistent with the lysophospholipid-related features observed elsewhere in the dataset. Under CTL, LPC and PC are negatively correlated, whereas under LIN they become positively correlated, in agreement with altered phospholipid turnover under stress (\cite{Hou2016}) and with the shift in lysolipid balance captured by LPC/LPE-, LPC/PS-, and LPE-centered ratios (Supplementary ~\ref{fig:suppfig_s7_ratios}A).  These correlation rewiring independently recapitulates the same LI-associated program of PS-centered headgroup redistribution, TG expansion, and altered PC/LPC dynamics. Thus, LIN does not induce a uniform, proportional scaling of lipid classes, rather, LIN restructures the covariance architecture of the lipidome such that TG, PS, and specific phospholipid/lyso-phospholipid pools are redistributed relative to SQDG- and galactolipid-enriched membrane classes, thereby recapitulating the same coordinated remodeling pattern identified by OPLS-DA.

\subsection*{Genome-wide Association Studies of Lipid Traits Under LowInput}

We performed GWAS using three representations of the same lipidomics dataset: (i) individual lipid species, (ii) summed abundances at the level of lipid classes, and (iii) all possible pairwise ratios among these summed classes. Statistical significance was defined as $-\log_{10}(p) \ge 7$, and SNPs were mapped to genes using a symmetric 25~kb flanking window around each SNP. To prioritize genes exhibiting robust pleiotropic effects across multiple lipid-related traits, we implemented additional filters based on the number of associated phenotypes. Only Genes associated with at least one-third of the highest number of occurrences, i.e.,  $\ge 4$ individual lipid species or $\ge 23$ class-level sum/ratio phenotypes (corresponding to upper-quartile cutoffs), were retained for downstream functional annotation (Supp Table).  

Under the two filters, we detected 538 distinct genes for individual lipid species and 656 genes significantly associated with summed-class or ratio lipid phenotypes. We categorized the candidate genes into four functional groups based on Gene Ontology (GO) annotations, gene family membership, gene names, and known functions from the literature: (i) lipid metabolism, (ii) cold stress response, (iii) N metabolism, and (iv) P homeostasis. Comprehensive GWAS summary statistics and corresponding gene annotations are presented in Supplementary Tables~S8--S10, as well as the Shiny app.

\paragraph{Lipid metabolism genes.}                                                                
One of the most highly connected genes across all GWAS was \texttt{SORBI\_3007G223801}, encoding a glycerol-3-phosphate dehydrogenase (GPDH), which was associated with 48 distinct sum/ratio phenotypes ($p_{\min} = 1.2 \times 10^{-18}$). GPDH catalyzes the reduction of dihydroxyacetone phosphate to glycerol-3-phosphate, a key precursor for glycerolipid biosynthesis, positioning this gene at a critical metabolic branch point (\cite{Driver2017}) . The Kennedy pathway enzyme diacylglycerol O-acyltransferase 1 (\texttt{SORBI\_3010G170000}, DGAT1) (\cite{Zhang2009}) showed associations with 5 individual lipid species ($p_{\min} = 3.2 \times 10^{-15}$) and 17 sum/ratio phenotypes ($p = 2.8 \times 10^{-10}$), consistent with its established role in TG biosynthesis (ref). Additionally, \texttt{SORBI\_3001G283700}, encoding a chloroplastic glycerol-3-phosphate acyltransferase (GPAT), is associated with 47 sum/ratio phenotypes ($p = 2.1 \times 10^{-13}$), implicating plastidial lipid biosynthesis in the LIN response (\cite{Kang2020}). Two lecithin-cholesterol acyltransferase-like genes were also identified: \texttt{SORBI\_3001G103800} (LCAT-like 1) (\cite{Chen2012}) was associated with 4 individual lipids and 15 sum/ratio phenotypes ($p = 3.1 \times 10^{-12}$), while \texttt{SORBI\_3006G214500} (LCAT like 4) showed associations with 4 individual lipids ($p = 4.1 \times 10^{-10}$).          

\paragraph{Nitrogen metabolism genes.}    

Among the nitrogen-associated candidate genes, \texttt{SORBI\_3001G541900}, which encodes a NRT1/PTR FAMILY 6.3–type transporter homolog, was significantly associated with 37 sum and ratio phenotypes ($p = 3.8 \times 10^{-11}$) (ref). NRT1/PTR transporters are known to mediate nitrate uptake and redistribution, thereby coupling nitrogen acquisition to downstream metabolic reprogramming (\cite{Maghiaoui2020}; \cite{OBrien2016}). In addition, three tandemly arranged tryptophan synthase genes (\texttt{SORBI\_3001G056400}, \texttt{SORBI\_3001G056500}, \texttt{SORBI\_3001G056601}) were each associated with 5–7 individual lipid species ($p_{\min} = 1.6 \times 10^{-10}$) and 16–17 sum/ratio phenotypes, indicative of coordinated regulation of amino acid and lipid metabolism under nutrient limitation (\cite{Obata2012}). The amino acid transporter gene (\cite{Ogasawara2021}) \texttt{SORBI\_3010G116400} exhibited robust associations with both individual lipid traits ($p = 2.5 \times 10^{-12}$, 3 traits) and ratio phenotypes ($p = 1.9 \times 10^{-12}$, 26 traits).  

\paragraph{Phosphorus homeostasis genes.}

The transcription factor PHOSPHATE STARVATION RESPONSE 1 (\texttt{SORBI\_3001G384300}, \textit{PHR1}) exhibited significant associations with nine individual lipid species ($p_{\min} = 6.6 \times 10^{-11}$), consistent with its established function in coordinating phospholipid remodeling under phosphate deficiency (\cite{Pant2015}; \cite{Rubio2001}). Purple acid phosphatase 18 (\texttt{SORBI\_3001G186800}, PAP18) was associated with five individual lipid species ($p = 5.7 \times 10^{-9}$). PAP enzymes catalyze the mobilization of organic phosphorus and are transcriptionally upregulated in response to phosphate deprivation (\cite{Wang2014PAP}; \cite{Li2002PAP}).  Additionally, the MYB-CC transcription factor PHOSPHATE STARVATION RESPONSE 1-LIKE (\texttt{SORBI\_3002G161700}, \textit{PHL}) was associated with 29 sum/ratio phenotypes ($p = 1.4 \times 10^{-9}$), reinforcing the central role of the PHR1/PHL regulatory network in lipid remodeling under nutrient limitation (\cite{Wang2023PHR}).

\paragraph{Cold stress response genes.}    

Two genes with previously characterized functions in cold acclimation were significantly associated with variation in lipid-related phenotypes. The bZIP transcription factor \texttt{SORBI\_3003G363400}, a homolog of ABA-INSENSITIVE 5 (\textit{ABI5}), was associated with 28 aggregate or ratio-based lipid phenotypes ($p = 6.4 \times 10^{-10}$); \textit{ABI5} is known to integrate abscisic acid (ABA) and low-temperature signaling to modulate membrane lipid composition (\cite{BarreroSicilia2017}). The AP2/ERF family member \texttt{SORBI\_3003G324400} (AP37) was associated with five individual lipid species ($p = 1.3 \times 10^{-8}$) and has been reported to confer enhanced drought and cold tolerance in cereal crops (\cite{Oh2009}).

\subsection*{LINEX-based lipid reaction network analysis}

To place the lipid associations into a biochemical framework, we queried LINEX2 (\cite{Morgat2016Rhea}) to retrieve RHEA-annotated reactions connecting the glycerolipids and glycerophospholipids. The resulting subnetwork (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig4_linex_network}, Supplementary Table 11) highlights a small set of plausible acyl-transfer and lipase transformations connecting DG/MG, TG, and lysophospholipid pools. These links represent annotation-based hypothetical conversions rather than direct evidence of \textit{in vivo} enzymatic reactions. Importantly, two highlighted transformations coincide with our GWAS results for LIN and are therefore prioritized as candidate biochemical connections.

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{RHEA-annotated PC + DG $\leftrightarrow$ LPC + TG transacylation (LCAT-like activity):}
LINEX identifies an acyl-transfer reaction linking PC and DG to LPC and TG (PC + DG $\leftrightarrow$ LPC + TG; RHEA annotation in Supplementary Table~S12) (\cite{Morgat2016Rhea}; \cite{Chen2012}). Although LINEX assigns this reaction to a canonical enzyme class in its reference database, the specific catalytic gene product operating in sorghum cannot be inferred from network topology alone. Two LCAT-like loci show strong GWAS signals: SORBI\_3001G103800 (best $p = 3.1 \times 10^{-12}$) and SORBI\_3006G214500 (best $p = 4.1 \times 10^{-10}$). These associations are consistent with genetically mediated variation in phospholipid-to-TG remodeling in which LPC is produced as a coproduct.

\item \textbf{Bidirectional TG$\leftrightarrow$DG flux (DGAT-mediated synthesis and lipase-mediated hydrolysis):}
LINEX links TG and DG as an interconvertible lipid pair. Our GWAS results support the genetic regulation for both directions (Table~\ref{tab:linex_gwas_support}). A putative acyl-CoA:diacylglycerol acyltransferase (\textit{DGAT1}) locus, SORBI\_3010G170000, exhibits strong association signals with multiple TG molecular species, consistent with a role in DG-to-TG biosynthesis (DG $\rightarrow$ TG) (\cite{Zhang2009}). Conversely, TG mobilization (TG $\rightarrow$ DG + FA) is supported by a triacylglycerol lipase locus (\cite{Eastmond2006}), SORBI\_3001G041900 (minimum $p = 1.13 \times 10^{-9}$), and by SORBI\_3010G270700, annotated as participating in triacylglycerol catabolism and identified in the sum/ratio GWAS (minimum $p = 3.07 \times 10^{-8}$), including associations with LPC/TG-related phenotypes. Together, these loci support coordinated genetic control of TG synthesis and degradation that can modulate DG availability within this module.

\item \textbf{PE$\rightarrow$LPE transacylation (LRO1/PDAT-type activity):}
The LINEX subnetwork incorporates a phospholipid:diacylglycerol acyltransferase reaction consistent with PDAT/LRO1-like activity, in which an acyl chain is transferred from  PE to DG to generate TG and LPE (PE + DG $\rightarrow$ TG + LPE; Supplementary Table~S12) (\cite{Dahlqvist2000}). In our GWAS gene-level summaries at the reporting threshold, we did not detect a discrete candidate locus that provides specific support for this PE$\rightarrow$LPE branch.

\item \textbf{DG + MG $\leftrightarrow$ TG interconversion (PNPLA3-labeled step):}
LINEX also includes a DG--MG--TG interconversion reaction (DG + MG $\leftrightarrow$ TG), annotated as PNPLA3 within the subnetwork. (\cite{Ryu2004}) In the two GWAS gene-summary tables examined here, we did not identify an additional locus that directly corresponds to this reaction.
\end{enumerate}

Across all four reactions, DG occupies a central position, functioning as both a substrate and a product, thereby acting as a metabolic branch point. The strongest GWAS support maps to (i) the PC + DG $\leftrightarrow$ LPC + TG transacylation module (LCAT-like loci) and (ii) the TG$\leftrightarrow$DG module, including DGAT-like DG$\rightarrow$TG synthesis and lipase/catabolism-associated TG$\rightarrow$DG turnover (Table~S12). Within this subnetwork, lysophospholipids (LPC, LPE) arise as coproducts of the PC- and PE-linked acyl-transfer reactions (items 1 and 3), linking membrane-lipid remodeling to neutral-lipid dynamics.


\subsection*{Lipid modules predict plant height with high accuracy and cross-dataset reproducibility}

We also applied a machine learning framework to assess whether lipid profiles can predict agronomic traits and serve as potential biomarkers. We applied XGBoost regression with SHAP-based interpretation (\cite{Chen2016}; \cite{Lundberg2017}). Given the high correlation among lipid species within biochemical classes, we first grouped the 226 individual lipids into 27 correlation-derived modules using hierarchical clustering based on Spearman correlation distances (Supplementary Table 12, Supplementary Table 13). This module-level strategy mitigates the limitation that feature attributions (including SHAP) can be sensitive to multicollinearity / feature dependence, which can redistribute or dilute importance across correlated predictors (\cite{Aas2021}).

\paragraph{Plant height prediction.}                                                              
For plant height, the XGBoost model exhibited robust predictive performance on our dataset ($R^2 = 0.752 \pm 0.027$, 5-fold CV; $n = 388$; Fig.~\ref{fig:fig5_xgb_shap_plantheight}A, Supplementary Table 14). Module-level SHAP analysis indicated that a single TG-dominated module (M1; 22 lipids) contributed the majority of the predictive signal, with a mean absolute module SHAP approximately 10-fold higher than the second-ranked module (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig5_xgb_shap_plantheight} B). Lipids assigned to M1 consisted primarily of short- and medium-chain TG, including TG(10:0/10:0/10:0), TG(12:0/12:0/14:0), and TG(10:0/12:0/16:0)(Supplementary Table 13).

To further substantiate these findings, we replicated the analysis using plant height phenotypes obtained from an independent study (\cite{Boyles2016}). Although the overall predictive performance was reduced in this dataset ($R^2 = 0.36 \pm 0.154$; $n = 264$), the ranking of module importance was highly concordant, i.e., 8 of the top 10 modules were shared between datasets, and the correlation of module importance scores was effectively perfect ($r = 0.99$; Fig.\ref{fig:fig5_xgb_shap_plantheight}C,D, Supplementary Table 15). Block M1 remained the top-ranked module in both datasets, indicating that short- and medium-chain TG represent robust and reproducible biomarkers of plant height across independent studies.

\paragraph{M1 module score stratifies tall and short genotypes.}
To assess whether the TG-enriched module M1 could be distilled into a practical lipid marker, we computed a per-sample M1 score as the mean of the (z)-scored abundances of the 22 lipids in M1 (Supplementary Table 12). The M1 score exhibited a strong positive association with plant height (Spearman’s $\rho = 0.559$, $p = 2.7\times10^{-33}$; Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s8_m1_tall_short}.~A, Supplementary Table S16), indicating that individuals with higher M1 abundance tended to be taller. In line with this association, stratification of genotypes into short and tall groups (bottom and top quartiles of plant height, respectively) revealed a marked shift in the distribution of M1 scores (Wilcoxon $p = 3.6\times10^{-18}$; Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s8_m1_tall_short}B, Supplementary Table 17). This separation might be of practical relevance. The M1 score yielded an AUC of 0.861 for discriminating tall from short genotypes, reflecting a high probability that a randomly selected tall genotype exhibits a higher M1 score than a randomly selected short genotype (Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s8_m1_tall_short}B,D). Within M1, the lipids most strongly correlated with plant height were primarily short- and medium-chain TG species (Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s8_m1_tall_short}C), consistent with the module’s SHAP dominance in the XGBoost models. Thus, these findings support M1 as a compact, biologically interpretable lipid marker that captures plant height–associated variation in the SAP and could be used for other sorghum populations.

\paragraph{Flowering time shows no lipid-based predictive signal but reveals consistent module patterns.}                                                                                         
In contrast to plant height, lipid modules exhibited negligible predictive power for flowering time(days to anthesis). Across all five environments evaluated, including our DTA measurements and the flowering time phenotype in four different environments reported in \cite{Wei2025} (MI20, NE20, IA21\_1, IA21\_2), the cross-validated $R^2$ values were negative or close to zero (range: $-0.39$ to $-0.05$; Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s9_ft_shap}B; Supplementary Table 18), indicating that lipid profiles do not explain appreciable variation in flowering time.

Despite the absence of a predictive signal, we evaluated whether any lipid modules exhibited consistent importance across environments. Four modules were recurrently ranked within the top 10 in all five environments: M1 (22 lipids, TG-dominated), M2 (22 lipids, PC-dominated), M5 (15 lipids, PC-dominated), and M6 (15 lipids, TG-dominated) (Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s9_ft_shap}A,D; Supplementary Table 19 and 20). Two additional modules (M3, M13) were among the top 10 in four of the five environments. This cross-environment consistency indicates that, although these lipid programs do not predict flowering time with sufficient accuracy for practical applications, they likely exhibit weak yet reproducible associations with developmental timing and could be used as markers for flowering time.

\section{Discussion}

Our lipidomics analysis indicates that the SAP undergoes substantial lipid remodeling under LIN conditions, with TG, PS, LPC/LPE, and SQDG showing the most pronounced changes. We identified candidate genes associated with these lipid traits and with broader stress-response pathways, but functional validation will be needed to assess their roles. We also found that lipidomic features can predict some phenotypes, such as plant height, whereas the prediction of flowering time was weaker. Finally, we developed an interactive Shiny application to support the visualization and exploration of the dataset.

\subsection{Phosphorus homeostasis regulators implicate phosphate-starvation control of phospholipid remodeling.}

The recurrent identification of the sorghum MYB-like DNA-binding gene \texttt{SORBI\_3001G384300} (a \textit{PHR1} homolog, Phosphate Starvation Response1) across phospholipid-associated traits is consistent with naturally occurring variation in phosphate-starvation regulatory pathways, contributing to lipidomic diversity (\cite{Pant2015}). This locus exhibited significant associations with multiple PCs and a PE molecular species, including PC(16:0/20:3), PC(16:0/22:5), PC(16:0/22:6), PC(18:1/20:1), PE(16:0/18:1), PC(18:1/24:1), PC(18:2/20:0), and PC(18:3/0:0) ($p_{\min}=6.6\times10^{-11}$), indicating that phospholipid composition may serve as a sensitive downstream readout of the phosphate-starvation regulatory status (Supp.~Table). In plants, the MYB-CC transcription factor \textit{PHR1} is widely recognized as a central regulator of transcriptional responses to phosphate limitation (\cite{Rubio2001}). \textit{PHR1} binds the P1BS cis-regulatory element (GNATATNC) located in the promoters of phosphate starvation–induced (PSI) genes and activates phosphate transport, signal transduction, and metabolic adaptation to phosphate scarcity (\cite{Bustos2010}). Genetic perturbation analyses further highlight its global regulatory function, as \textit{phr1} loss-of-function mutants exhibit impaired induction of PSI genes and disruption of characteristic phosphate-starvation phenotypes. In contrast, elevated PHR1 expression enhances phosphate uptake capacity and strengthens physiological and molecular responses to phosphate deprivation (\cite{Rubio2001}; \cite{Bustos2010};  \cite{Nilsson2007}). Moreover, PHR1 integrates phosphate status with other nutrient pathways. Under phosphate-deficient conditions, it upregulates the sulfate transporter \textit{SULTR1;3} and modulates shoot-to-root sulfate allocation, thereby contributing to the coordinated regulation of anion homeostasis under nutrient stress (\cite{Rouached2011}). 

Consistent with this, we also identified a broad sum/ratio association signal at the PHR1-like locus \texttt{SORBI\_3002G161700} (PHL; $p=1.4\times10^{-9}$; 29 sum/ratio phenotypes).This pattern suggests that natural variation in upstream phosphate-starvation regulators can propagate through the regulatory network to produce coordinated shifts in lipid ratios, rather than isolated changes in individual lipid species. PHR1-LIKE 1 (PHL1), like \textit{PHR1} gene, is well-established as a central regulator of transcriptional responses to phosphate starvation and is a member of a 15-gene MYB-CC transcription factor family in \textit{Arabidopsis thaliana} (\
\cite{Rubio2001}; \cite{Bustos2010}). The loss of function of \textit{PHR1}, even in combination with its close homolog \textit{PHL1}, only partially compromises PSI gene expression (\cite{Bustos2010}). This observation indicates that additional, functionally redundant transcription factors participate in orchestrating the full transcriptional response to phosphate deficiency (\cite{Sun2016}). \textit{PHR1} and \textit{PHL1} have been shown to form homo- and heterodimers, thereby modulating a broad suite of genes involved in phosphate homeostasis (\cite{Bustos2010}).

In addition to transcriptional regulators, our GWAS analysis identified an enzyme involved in P mobilization, corresponding to \texttt{SORBI\_3001G186800} (PAP18; $p=5.7\times10^{-9}$; five individual lipid species). Purple acid phosphatases (PAPs) constitute a large family of non-specific acid phosphatases that hydrolyze orthophosphate from a broad spectrum of P-monoesters and phosphoanhydrides and are particularly important under  phosphate deprivation (\cite{DelVecchio2014}; \cite{Mehra2019}). This family comprises intracellular isoforms implicated in the remobilization of internal phosphate reserves, as well as extracellular or secreted isoforms that scavenge phosphate from organic-P pools in the apoplast and rhizosphere. Extracellular PAP activity is widely regarded as a key adaptive mechanism under low-P conditions (\cite{DelVecchio2014}; \cite{Mehra2019}). Studies in \textit{Arabidopsis} indicate that phosphate starvation–inducible PAPs are functionally specialized, including the surface-associated PAP10, the secreted enzymes PAP12 and PAP26, and the cell wall–localized PAP25, which has been proposed to play a signaling-related role.  In rice, secretory PAPs such as OsPAP21b enhance the utilization of external organic P, resulting in increased biomass accumulation and P content when organic P is limiting ( \cite{Mehra2017OsPAP21b}; \cite{Wang2014PAP}; \cite{DelVecchio2014}; \cite{Mehra2019}). Taken together, the co-localization of PHR/PHL regulatory signals and a PAP locus in our lipid GWAS is consistent with a model in which natural genetic variation in phosphate-starvation sensing and phosphate acquisition/remobilization capacity is coupled with phospholipid remodeling phenotypes under nutrient limitation. 


\subsection{Glycerolipid buffering and DG--TG control under low-input stress.}

A recurrent theme among the lipid-GWAS candidates is that multiple loci map to the DG–TG branch point and to upstream glycerol-backbone supply, together influencing whether acyl flux remains in membrane lipids or is redirected into neutral-lipid storage. This biochemical junction has been repeatedly implicated as stress-relevant in vegetative tissues, where TAG can accumulate as a transient sink during nutrient limitation (including N deficiency) (\cite{Yang2011}) and during low-temperature/freezing stress (\cite{Tan2018}), rather than functioning exclusively as a seed storage reserve. Consistent with a broader role for TG metabolism outside seeds, leaves possess measurable TG synthetic capacity, and \textit{DGAT1} is a major contributor to TAG synthesis in young Arabidopsis leaves (\cite{Tjellstrom2015}), while vegetative TG has been widely discussed as part of plant stress responses (\cite{Lu2020}). In contrast, seed oil phenotypes emphasize that \textit{DGAT1} and \textit{PDAT1} occupy a quantitatively important position in TAG assembly during development (\cite{Zhang2009}).

In our dataset, the strongest evidence for DG$\rightarrow$TG transition is provided by the \textit{DGAT1} locus \texttt{SORBI\_3010G170000}, which was reproducibly associated with five distinct TG molecular species (TG(18:1/18:3/22:0), TG(18:1/20:3/22:0), TG(18:2/18:2/18:4), TG(18:2/20:3/22:0), TG(18:3/18:3/18:3)) and 17 sum/ratio phenotypes (Table~1). \textit{DGAT1} catalyzes the terminal acyl-CoA--dependent acylation step that converts DG to TG. Prior work provides a rationale for how standing genetic variation at \textit{DGAT1} could manifest as lipidomic differences under stress. Under N limitation, \textit{Arabidopsis} leaves accumulate TG alongside increased expression of \textit{DGAT1} and \textit{OLEOSIN1}. Abscisic acid signaling can also activate \textit{DGAT1} transcription through ABI4 binding to CE1 elements (CACCG) in the \textit{DGAT1} promoter. Accordingly, \textit{abi4} mutants show reduced \textit{DGAT1} induction and lower TG accumulation under low-N conditions (\cite{Yang2011}).

Cold-stress responsiveness provides an additional, independent line of evidence that \textit{DGAT1} represents a strong candidate gene. \textit{DGAT1} expression is induced at low temperature (4$^\circ$C), and \emph{dgat1} loss-of-function mutants exhibit chlorosis and enhanced cell death under cold stress, phenotypes that are accompanied by reduced TG levels but increased DG and PA. The model proposed by \cite{Tan2018} et al. further suggests that elevated PA enhances RbohD-dependent reactive oxygen species production and that increased diacylglycerol kinase (\textit{DGK}) activity can further channel DG into PA, with loss of specific \emph{dgk} genes restoring cold tolerance (\cite{Tan2018}).Seed phenotypes further indicate that \textit{DGAT1} is an important control point for lipid flux. The \textit{dgat1} mutant has reduced seed oil content, and combined disruption with \textit{pdat1} or strong \textit{DGAT1} knockdown leads to severe TAG depletion and developmental abnormalities (\cite{Zhang2009}). Within this framework, our DGAT1--TG associations suggest that natural allelic variation may influence how strongly DG is directed into TAG under LI, while recognizing that \textit{DGAT1} is unlikely to be the only factor shaping the lipidome changes.

Furthermore, the GWAS does not exclusively implicate TG biosynthesis, rather, the associated loci include genes whose annotated functions are consistent with roles in both TG formation and TG catabolism. We identify a TG lipase locus, \texttt{SORBI\_3001G041900}, associated with variation in multiple TG molecular species (minimum $p = 1.13 \times 10^{-9}$), supporting genetically encoded variation in TG mobilization (TG $\rightarrow$ DG + FA) (\cite{Eastmond2006,Kelly2013,Fan2017}). In addition, \texttt{SORBI\_3010G270700} is annotated in SorghumBase as participating in TG degradation (\cite{Gladman2022SorghumBase}) and emerges as a significant locus in the sum/ratio GWAS (minimum $p = 3.07 \times 10^{-8}$), including phenotypes based on LPC/TG (Table~1). Rather than treating these associations as redundant with \textit{DGAT1}, they fit a coherent model in which DGAT1-driven TG synthesis buffers acyl flux and limits the buildup of potentially bioactive or cytotoxic intermediates (e.g., DG and PA) during stress, while lipases and related catabolic processes subsequently remobilize DG and fatty acids to support membrane lipid remodeling/repair and lipid-derived signaling as conditions change (\cite{Fan2017,Yu2021}). Importantly, this interpretation stays within what genetic association can support. Resolving flux direction and timing (storage versus mobilization) will require complementary evidence from expression profiling or enzymatic assays.

Upstream of DG $\rightarrow$ TG interconversion, the detection of a strong association signal at a glycerol-3-phosphate dehydrogenase locus (\texttt{SORBI\_3007G223801}; \textit{GPDH}) provides a plausible explanation for the coordinated shifts observed across numerous ratio traits. Plastidial glycerol-3-phosphate production can constrain the supply of glycerol backbones available for bulk membrane glycerolipid synthesis. In \textit{Arabidopsis}, SFD1/GLY1 encodes a plastid-localized glycerol-3-phosphate dehydrogenase required for maintaining normal plastid lipid composition, and overexpression of \textit{SFD1} in rice results in a gain-of-function phenotype characterized by increased total fatty acid content and elevated levels of plastidic membrane lipids (MGDG, DGDG, PG) (\cite{Singh2016SFD1}). Studies in rice further indicate that elevated plastidial glycerol-3-phosphate can be exported to the endoplasmic reticulum and influence the eukaryotic pathway of glycerolipid synthesis, thereby altering galactolipid molecular-species distributions in patterns consistent with modified DG supply (\cite{Singh2016SFD1}). In our sorghum dataset, the broad spectrum of ratio-level associations at this GPDH/G3P-supply locus is therefore consistent with a model in which genetic variation in glycerol-backbone availability modulates overall lipid synthesis capacity and propagates across multiple lipid-class ratios, including DG-centered ratios, without requiring a single headgroup-modifying enzyme as the exclusive causal step.

Furthermore, the plastid-localized GPAT/ATS1 (also known as ACT1) catalyzes the initial sn-1 acylation of glycerol-3-phosphate that commits flux into the prokaryotic pathway of chloroplast/thylakoid glycerolipid biosynthesis (\cite{Nishida1993,Chen2011GPAT,LiBeisson2013}). Consistent with this central role, classical \textit{ats1}/\textit{act1} mutants exhibit reduced plastid GPAT activity and altered thylakoid/galactolipid acyl profiles (\cite{Kunst1988}), and more broadly, strong impairment of plastid glycerolipid synthesis is associated with pronounced developmental defects, underscoring tight coordination between plastid lipid initiation and overall plant growth (\cite{LiBeisson2013}). Accordingly, a GPAT/ATS1-like association influencing anionic and chloroplast-linked lipid ratios is compatible with upstream variation in plastidial lipid initiation propagating to downstream membrane lipid composition.

For the LCAT-like loci (\texttt{SORBI\_3001G103800}, \texttt{SORBI\_3006G214500}), prior work cautions against inferring a single enzymatic function solely from LCAT-family membership. Although the broader LCAT-family includes bona fide phospholipid:diacylglycerol acyltransferase activity (PDAT/LRO1-type transacylation) (\cite{Dahlqvist2000}), AT4G19860 (\textit{AtLCAT-PLA}) functions as a calcium-independent phospholipase A with strong specificity for PC headgroups and shows no detectable LCAT or PDAT activity. When expressed in yeast, it inhibits growth, likely due to toxic accumulation of LPC (\cite{Chen2012}). Therefore, our LCAT-like GWAS signals are consistent with roles in PC remodeling that modulate lysophospholipid intermediates and DG availability. However, distinguishing transacylation-based remodeling from phospholipase-driven remodeling will require independent functional validation.

Together, these loci point to a coherent biochemical axis involving glycerol-3-phosphate (G3P) supply for the glycerol backbone, early plastid steps in membrane lipid biosynthesis, and regulated partitioning between DG and TG pools. This framework provides a simple explanation for the recurrent DG- and TG-related species and ratio associations in our GWAS while remaining appropriately conservative. The genetic signals highlight the regulators of lipid remodeling and metabolic flux. However, the directionality of this flux, as well as its stress- and developmental stage–specific dynamics, remain hypothetical and require experimental validation.

\subsection{Nitrogen sensing, amino-acid metabolism, and hormone signaling as upstream drivers of lipid variation.}

Our lipid GWAS also identifies loci related to N acquisition and N-stress physiology. AA key focus is the tryptophan (Trp)–auxin regulatory axis. Tryptophan (Trp) is not only a protein-forming amino acid but also the main precursor of indole-3-acetic acid (IAA). Trp-dependent IAA biosynthesis is required for root and shoot developmental programs that influence nutrient uptake and stress acclimation (\cite{Stepanova2008,Zhao2001,Fu2022,Liu2024,Wang2025}) Under N-limiting conditions, Trp metabolism is reprogrammed in an adaptive manner. Studies have show that some low-N-tolerant sorghum genotypes exhibit higher endogenous Trp levels than N-sensitive genotypes, and exogenous Trp application increases both Trp and IAA concentrations in sorghum roots under low N, while enhancing root system development and improving physiological parameters associated with C/N metabolism (\cite{Liu2024}). Moreover, the Trp-dependent IAA biosynthetic enzyme TAA1/SAV3 has been implicated in the regulation of chlorophyll accumulation and N assimilation, further supporting a role for Trp--IAA metabolism in integrating N status with photosynthetic capacity and overall growth (\cite{Fu2022}). Together, these observations provide a biologically coherent context for our Trp/IAA-associated GWAS candidate.

A second candidate centers on nitrate sensing mediated by NRT1.1/NPF6.3, which operates both as a dual-affinity nitrate transporter and as a nitrate sensor (\cite{Tsay1993,Liu1999,Liu2003,Ho2009,Krouk2010,Sakuraba2021}). Under fluctuating nitrate availability, NRT1.1 mediates a regulatory switch. In roots, it represses components of the high-affinity nitrate transport system (NRT2.1/NRT3.1) under nitrate-replete conditions, whereas this repression is alleviated at low external nitrate concentrations, thereby enabling efficient nitrate scavenging and exploitation of nitrate-enriched microsites (\cite{Krouk2006,Remans2006}). Recent findings also indicate a shoot-specific role during 
N deficiency. NRT1.1 expression can increase in aerial tissues, and promoter polymorphisms have been linked to higher biomass and reduced leaf chlorosis, suggesting that shoot-localized NRT1.1 activity may help maintain leaf performance under nitrogen stress (\cite{Sakuraba2021}). NRT1.1 further integrates nutrient and hormonal signaling networks. It can mediate auxin uptake in a nitrate-inhibited manner, and nitrate-dependent modulation of auxin transport alters lateral root development (\citep{Krouk2010}). Of particular relevance to our experimental condition, NRT1.1 is also positioned at the interface of N--P crosstalk. It participates in signaling pathways through which N availability modulates the phosphate starvation response and interacts with core PSR regulators, including \textit{PHR1} and \textit{PHO2} (\cite{Medici2019}). This positioning provides a mechanistic link between the N-associated GWAS candidates and the P-responsive regulatory module identified elsewhere in this study.

Finally, amino acid partitioning and glutamine homeostasis provide an additional mechanism through which N status can modulate lipid-related traits. \textit\textit{OsATL6} is a tonoplast-localized AAAP (amino acid/auxin permease) transporter that is induced by glutamine and ammonium supply and primarily sequesters glutamine into root vacuoles for temporary storage (\cite{Ogasawara2021,Rentsch2007,Tegeder2012}). Heterologous expression assays (e.g., in \textit{Xenopus laevis} oocytes) support a primarily unidirectional glutamine transport activity, and OsATL6 knockdown lines exhibit decreased glutamine concentrations in roots but elevated glutamine levels in xylem sap, indicating that the disruption of vacuolar buffering redirects glutamine toward enhanced root-to-shoot translocation (\cite{Ogasawara2021}). The spatial and temporal pattern of OsATL6 accumulation, which is enriched in cell layers near the Casparian strip and occurs before peak activity of ammonium-assimilating enzymes, supports a buffering role that stabilizes glutamine pools during rapid nitrogen assimilation and modulates glutamine supply to the shoot (\cite{Ogasawara2021}).

Thus, the Trp--IAA regulatory module, NRT1.1-mediated nitrate sensing (including its roles in hormonal signaling and N--P integration), and glutamine homeostasis outline a coherent N-stress response framework that can help explain why N-related loci are repeatedly detected among genetic associates of lipid variation under LIN.

\subsection{Lipid GWAS prioritizes ABI5 and AP37 regulatory modules for cold association}

A key outcome of the lipid GWAS analysis is the enrichment of transcriptional regulators at established intersections among abscisic acid signaling (ABA), canonical cold-response pathways (CBF/COR), and oxidative-stress mitigation, which together influence membrane function under chilling and freezing conditions (\cite{Skubacz2016,Ding2019}). Most notably, the bZIP transcription factor \textit{ABI5} repeatedly emerges across species as a node connecting ABA signaling with cold tolerance (\cite{Skubacz2016}).

In apple, \textit{MdABI5} has been shown to enhance cold tolerance by directly activating a positive regulator of cold responses (\textit{MdCBF3}) while repressing negative regulators of cold tolerance (\textit{MdJAZ1/3}).Furthermore, \textit{MdABI5} overexpression improved cold tolerance and reduced oxidative damage, alongside increased antioxidant enzyme activity (\cite{Deng2026}). Evidence from other crop species substantiates a role for ABI5 in ABA-dependent cold-stress responses. In banana, \textit{MaABI5} contributes to ABA-induced cold tolerance through its interaction with a RING-type E3 ubiquitin ligase (\textit{MaC3HC4-1}) (\cite{Chen2018MaABI5}). In cucumber, the WRKY transcription factor \textit{CsWRKY46} is transcriptionally induced by both low temperature and ABA, and transgenic analyses support an ABA-dependent cold-resistance pathway incorporating ABI5-associated regulatory components (\cite{Zhang2016CsWRKY46}). In rice, OsNAC5 fine-tunes cold tolerance by increasing the stability of OsABI5 and thereby promoting the activation of downstream COR genes. In addition, promoter polymorphisms in OsABI5 correlate with natural variation in cold tolerance (\cite{Li2024OsNAC5}). However, rice also suggests that ABI5 can act as a cost--benefit regulator. OsABI5 is ABA/salt-inducible but is reported to decrease under drought and cold in seedlings, and its repression can enhance stress tolerance while reducing fertility, indicating context- and stage-dependent tradeoffs (\cite{Zou2008OsABI5}). Thus, these findings support interpreting an ABI5-associated GWAS signal as variation in ABA-linked cold-response regulation, potentially affecting CBF/COR-related outputs and oxidative-stress control (\cite{Ding2019}).

A second cold-associated transcriptional module in our candidate set is represented by the AP2-domain transcription factor \textit{AP37}. In rice, \textit{AP37} transcript abundance responds rapidly to cold exposure, indicating that \textit{AP37} functions as an early cold-responsive regulator rather than a late downstream marker (\cite{Oh2009,Dubouzet2003}). Constitutive overexpression of \textit{AP37} (OsCc1:\textit{AP37} lines) enhanced low-temperature tolerance at the vegetative stage. Under low-temperature conditions, OsCc1:\textit{AP37} plants exhibited $\sim$15\% higher maximum photochemical efficiency of photosystem II (F$_v$/F$_m$) than non-transgenic controls, whereas OsCc1:\textit{AP59} plants were indistinguishable from controls. These data support the protection of the photosynthetic apparatus under chilling rather than merely a delayed manifestation of stress symptoms (\cite{Oh2009}).

Together, the identification of \textit{ABI5} and \textit{AP37} in our GWAS suggests that regulatory variation in ABA-responsive and AP2/ERF transcriptional networks contributes to natural variation in cold responses. Evidence from multiple crop species links these factors to cold-responsive gene regulation, maintenance of photosynthetic performance, and reduced oxidative damage, providing a coherent context for their prioritization as candidate loci. (\cite{Deng2026,Chen2018MaABI5,Zhang2016CsWRKY46,Li2024OsNAC5,Oh2009,Ding2019}).

\subsection*{Triglyceride-based lipid predictors capture growth-related metabolic state and improve XGBoost prediction of plant height}

The predominance of TG modules in plant height prediction is biologically plausible. TGs are a major energy storage form in seeds and can also accumulate and turn over in vegetative tissues, and their mobilization can supply carbon skeletons and energy to support growth (\cite{XuShanklin2016,Eastmond2006,Fan2017}). In particular, shifts toward relatively shorter-chain TG species may reflect an altered fatty-acid synthesis/elongation balance and carbon allocation that accompanies rapid biomass accumulation (\cite{XuShanklin2016}). The finding that these associations are consistently observed across independent datasets strengthens confidence that they represent true underlying biological relationships rather than artifacts of model overfitting. 

As an additional, model-independent check, the TG-enriched module M1 was collapsed into a single per-sample score, which effectively distinguished individuals in the highest and lowest height quartiles with high accuracy (AUC = 0.861; Supplementary Fig.~S8). These findings support the use of lipid-module summary scores as practical lipidomic indicators of height-associated variation in the SAP. Moreover, plant lipidomics is increasingly used to identify lipid signatures linked to specific conditions and phenotypes, including environmental stress, and has been proposed as a useful framework for biomarker discovery (\cite{Henschel2024}). Related strategies have also been applied in long-lived woody species, where targeted lipidomics combined with supervised learning has identified lipid predictors that discriminate field states in olive trees (\cite{Scala2022}). These lines of evidence support lipid-based markers as a genetic and phenotypic screening method, particularly for traits that require long periods or substantial resources to measure directly.

The absence of robust lipid-based predictors for flowering time is itself informative. Floral transition is predominantly regulated by genetic determinants and environmental cues (e.g., photoperiod and vernalization) acting through well-characterized signaling networks (\cite{Andres2012,Srikanth2011}). Although lipid metabolic processes can intersect with hormonal and stress pathways, our findings indicate that steady-state lipid profiles obtained at a single developmental stage are insufficient to capture the underlying regulatory complexity governing flowering time.

\subsection*{Convergent ratio, ontology, network, and GWAS evidence supports coordinated lipid redistribution under Low-input}

Across analytical layers, the LI lipidome is best interpreted as a coordinated redistribution across interconnected glycolipid, membrane, and neutral-lipid pools rather than isolated changes in a single class. Ratio-based analysis provide a clear within-sample readout of these tradeoffs in compositional data (\cite{Aitchison1982,Greenacre2020}), consistently recovering four LIN signatures: (i) SQDG depletion relative to multiple pools, (ii) PS enrichment within the phospholipid network, (iii) TG enrichment relative to membrane pools and glycerolipid intermediates (including DG), and (iv) lysophospholipid remodeling characterized by increased LPE relative to membrane pools and a reduced LPC/LPE balance, consistent with stress-responsive phospholipid turnover rather than uniform scaling of total lyso-lipid abundance (\cite{Hou2016,Rawat2021}).These signatures were also recovered by OPLS-DA, which robustly separated CTL and LIN. VIP-ranked ratios recapitulated SQDG-centered depletion, PS-centered headgroup redistribution, and the reduced LPC/LPE balance under LI (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig2_opls_da}; Supplementary Fig.~\ref{fig:suppfig_s7_ratios}A). Furthermore, these directional patterns are also supported at the ontology level. LION enrichment highlights LI-associated terms related to neutral-lipid storage (e.g., TGs, lipid storage/lipid droplet, and related glycerolipid categories) together with changes in membrane-associated lipid categories (\cite{Molenaar2019}), providing orthogonal support that the LIN response reflects a structured reallocation of lipid across connected pools. Network-based evidence further supports these changes. The class-level correlation structure in CLR is rewired under LIN, including sign reversals for key pairs linking membrane headgroups and storage pools, such as PC--PS and PS--TG, and for phospholipid and lysophospholipid pairs such as LPC--PC. These shifts indicate that LI changes the direction of coordinated variation among lipid pools rather than simply weakening existing associations (\cite{Aitchison1982}). Finally, GWAS anchors this systems-level remodeling to specific genetic loci whose annotated functions map naturally onto each signature. Plastid and glycerol-backbone control points (e.g., a glycerol-3-phosphate dehydrogenase locus and a plastid GPAT/ATS1-like locus) emerge as highly connected candidates across summed/ratio traits, consistent with upstream regulation of  glycolipid/sulfolipid balance and thereby compatible with SQDG-linked depletion patterns (\cite{Driver2017,Nishida1993}). Neutral-lipid expansion and DG--TG repartitioning are supported by loci linked to TG synthesis and TG turnover (DGAT-like and lipase/catabolic candidates), consistent with the observed TG enrichment relative to membrane pools and intermediates (\cite{Zhang2009,Dahlqvist2000,Eastmond2006,Kelly2013}). Phospholipid headgroup redistribution and lyso-lipid dynamics are further prioritized by (i) phosphate-starvation regulators (PHR/PHL/PAP) associated with broad phospholipid and ratio phenotypes, aligning with the PS-centered remodeling axis (\cite{Rubio2001,Bustos2010,Sun2016,Pant2015,DelVecchio2014,Mehra2017OsPAP21b}), and (ii) LCAT-like loci that coincide with the LINEX RHEA-annotated PC + DG $\leftrightarrow$ LPC + TG module, providing a concrete biochemical hypothesis linking lysophospholipid remodeling (LPC/LPE balance) to DG/TG dynamics through phospholipid remodeling (\cite{Morgat2016Rhea,Chen2012,Dahlqvist2000}). Together, agreement among ratio contrasts, LION enrichment, covariance rewiring, reaction-network connectivity, and GWAS candidate loci supports a unified LIN remodeling program centered on SQDG depletion, PS headgroup redistribution, TG expansion, and lysophospholipid remodeling. At the same time, we interpret these patterns conservatively because reaction direction and causal mechanisms will require functional validation.

\section*{Conclusion}

In summary, population-scale lipidomics in the SAP across contrasting field environments revealed that LIN conditions elicit a structured remodeling of the lipidome characterized by coordinated redistribution among glycolipids/sulfolipids, phospholipids, and triglycerides. Multiple orthogonal analyses converged on four robust signatures of the low-input response, namely, SQDG depletion, TG enrichment, PS redistribution within the phospholipid network, and lysophospholipid remodeling, supported in parallel by ratio-based contrasts, lipid ontology enrichment, covariance rewiring in compositional space, and reaction-network connectivity. Integrating these biochemical patterns with GWAS further anchored lipid remodeling to candidate loci spanning phosphate-starvation regulators, glycerol-backbone, and lipid biosynthesis points, thereby providing genetically informed hypotheses for the mechanisms underlying sorghum lipid plasticity under combined nutrient and cold stress. Beyond biological interpretation, the predictive modeling results indicate that TG-dominated lipid programs can serve as reproducible markers of growth-related phenotypes, highlighting the potential utility of lipid modules as biomarkers in breeding and translational applications. Collectively, SoLD provides a harmonized resource linking sorghum lipid diversity to environment, genotype, and agronomic traits, and establishes a foundation for functional validation of prioritized loci and for deploying lipid-informed markers to improve resilience in low-input production systems. Future work will use targeted approaches, including CRISPR-based perturbation and/or transgenic overexpression of prioritized candidate genes, to test the proposed lipid-remodeling mechanisms and their effects on performance under low-input conditions.

\section*{Materials and methods}

\subsection*{Plant Material and Growth Conditions}

In our study, we used the Sorghum Association Panel (SAP), consisting of 400 accessions designed to cover extensive genetic and phenotypic diversity. This collection includes both temperate-adapted breeding lines and tropical landraces. The panel represents five botanical races, bicolor, caudatum, durra, guinea, and kafir, capturing a diverse range of domestication and adaptation processes.

SAP was first genotyped using simple sequence repeat markers, followed by low-coverage genotyping by sequencing (GBS). For a more comprehensive variation set, Boatwright et al. (ref) resequenced all entries using whole genome sequencing (WGS) with an average depth of 38× (ranging from 25–72×). The variant data from WGS revealed approximately 43.98 million polymorphisms, including roughly 38 million SNPs with 5 million small insertions/deletions. While GBS variants were predominantly located in genic regions, the WGS data were more evenly distributed across genic and intergenic regions. Genome-wide linkage disequilibrium is approximately 20 kb, although there were deviations specific to each chromosome. The consequent high-density variant map establishes the resequenced SAP as a valuable tool for examining diversity and conducting genome wide association studies (GWAS).

We evaluated SAP accessions across two different field settings during two growing seasons (2019 and 2022) at the Pee Dee Research and Education Center, Clemson University, Florence, South Carolina. The "control" condition, herein denoted as C, involved standard agronomic inputs with sufficient levels of nitrogen (N) and phosphorus (P) along with a typical planting schedule. In contrast, the "low input" condition, herein denoted as LI,  featured reduced N and P coupled with earlier planting to mimic a cold stress environment.

\subsection*{Lipidomics Analysis}

\paragraph{Sample Preparation and Extraction.} 
Samples were prepared following the standard extraction protocols explained in the hpc1 paper (ref).


\paragraph{Liquid Chromatography-Mass Spectrometry (LC-MS) Analysis.} Lipid extracts were subjected to high-resolution mass spectrometry employing both positive and negative ionization modes to achieve comprehensive lipid coverage. Chromatographic separation was executed utilizing either reversed-phase columns, such as C18, or hydrophilic interaction chromatography (HILIC) columns, contingent upon the polarity of the lipids. In the case of C18 methodologies, the gradient elution commenced at 1 minute and concluded at 8 minutes, succeeded by an isocratic elution phase from 8 to 9.5 minutes. Data preceding 1 minute and subsequent to 9.5 minutes were omitted to eliminate solvent front and late eluting artifacts. For HILIC methodologies, the gradient initiation occurred at 1 minute, terminating at 16.25 minutes, and was followed by an isocratic elution extending until 18.5 minutes; data before 1 minute and after 18.5 minutes were excluded in a similar manner.

\paragraph{Feature Detection and Data Processing.} Raw LC-MS data, in both positive and negative ion modes, were processed utilizing MZmine 2, an open-source software for the analysis of mass spectrometry data (ref). The pipeline encompassed peak detection, chromatogram construction, deconvolution, and isotope filtering, producing a detailed feature table containing mass-to-charge ratio-retention time (mz-rt) pairs. Isotopic peaks were excluded to minimize redundancy, as a single metabolite can yield multiple co-eluting ions, such as adducts and in-source fragments. Therefore, mz-rt duplicates were handled with care, with potential de-adducting considered via MS-FLO when appropriate. We acknowledge that such degeneracy can lead to an inflated number of features compared to the actual number of metabolites present which is we considered during metabolite identification. 


\paragraph{Blank/extraction-control filtering, intensity thresholds, and sparsity pruning.}
To reduce the background and carryover effects, an extraction control filter was implemented at the feature level. For each feature, the maximum intensity was determined across extraction controls (\(a\)) and biological samples (\(b\)). Features for which \(b < 10\times a\) were eliminated. To preclude the exclusion of borderline yet potentially biological signals, a feature was retained should at least one biological sample exceed the extraction control maximum. Furthermore, a minimum average intensity threshold within the treatment groups of interest (\(\sim 10^{6}\) peak height) was imposed to ensure that downstream analyses would emphasize robust signals. At the sample level, any sample exhibiting \(\geq 70\%\) features as zero (or missing) was excluded prior to normalization and statistical analysis. This pruning of sparsity is essential to prevent unstable scaling and spurious differential signals caused by ultra-sparse profiles. 


\paragraph{MS/MS spectral library matching and cross-referencing of IDs.}
For each feature analyzed by MS/MS, the most intense fragmentation spectrum was queried against the GNPS database. Library matches resulted in putative identifications (levels 2/3), potentially including isomers or near-mass analogs. Features without direct matches were eliminated. To enable quantification with identifications, tables were linked using the feature \emph{row ID} from the MZmine peak list and the corresponding \#Scan\# key in the GNPS results, ensuring a one-to-one correspondence between intensities and candidate identifications. In cases where a feature yielded multiple GNPS hits, a single primary annotation was designated by retaining the highest MQScore (cosine similarity). Ties in the values were resolved based on a greater number of shared fragment ions and a smaller precursor mass error (ppm). All other sub-threshold or lower-ranked candidates were retained for verification but were excluded from subsequent statistical analyses.

\paragraph{Systematic Error Removal Using Random Forest (SERRF).}
Following the cleaning process, the data were then used for SERRF normalization (ref). We used the SERRF server (https://slfan2013.github.io/SERRF-online/\#) to obtain the normalized output. After applying SERRF, only biological samples were preserved. Any zeros were substituted with two-thirds of the minimum nonzero value for that feature to prevent potential infinite logarithmic transformations.

\paragraph{Spatial Correction.}
Finally, we conducted an additional quality control step specifically aimed at eliminating any spatial patterns across our experimental trials. This was achieved using the R package \texttt{SpATS} (ref), which applies a two-dimensional P-spline ANOVA surface over the field coordinates. For every lipid feature, we characterized its intensity as
\begin{align}
  y_{ij} &= \mu + f_{\mathrm{row}}(i) + f_{\mathrm{col}}(j) + f_{\mathrm{row,col}}(i,j) + \varepsilon_{ij},
\end{align}

where 
\begin{itemize}
  \item \(f_{\mathrm{row}}\) and \(f_{\mathrm{col}}\) represent smooth functions that model systematic effects across rows and columns, respectively, 
  \item \(f_{\mathrm{row,col}}\) is a smooth interaction surface that handles more complex spatial gradients. 
\end{itemize}

We utilized the residuals, defined as the difference between observed intensity and the fitted spatial trend, as our analysis-ready spatially corrected intensity data which will be used for \%TIC, CLR, and log-ratio. We used SpATS-adjusted intensities (residuals + fitted mean) to preserve scale and avoid negative values. This methodology effectively corrects for positional artifacts, such as edge effects, that could interfere with subsequent analyses. Detailed smoothing parameters, including the number of knots, penalty orders, and comprehensive model specifications, can be found in our GitHub repository at \texttt{scripts/spats\_qc.R}.

\paragraph{Lipid Quantification}
The identified lipid species were organized into lipid classes and subclasses (see Supplementary Table\~1) based on Lipid Maps (ref). In each sample, the total intensity for a class was obtained by summing the intensities of the species within that class. To manage variability in signal intensity due to different runs or injections, these class totals were normalized relative to the total ion current (TIC) of the sample. As a result, the relative abundances were presented as percentages of the TIC by adding up intensities across all lipid classes in the sample. For each class and its subclasses, we determined the TIC fraction for each sample and then averaged these percentages across samples for each condition (Control, \$n=384\$; LowInput, \$n=362\$). Lipids were categorized into glycerolipid, glycerophospholipid, sphingolipid, sterol, betaine lipid, fatty acid, ether lipid, and terpenoid. Refer to Supplementary Table 2 for the full list. 

\paragraph{Lipid Ratio Identification} To identify lipid class ratios most affected by the shift from (CTL) to LowInput (LIN), we computed class totals and all pairwise class log-ratios, then analyzed these features using orthogonal partial least squares-discriminant analysis (OPLS-DA; \textbackslash{}emph\{ropls\}, R v3.3.2). Ratios with VIP \$>1\$ were considered the most discriminative contributors to LIN--CTL separation. 


\paragraph{Lipid ratio calculation}
For each sample $i$ and lipid class $c$, species intensities were aggregated to class totals,
\[
C_{i,c}=\sum_{k \in c} I_{i,k}.
\]
Because log-ratios are scale-invariant, TIC normalization is not required for ratio computation.
To handle zero class totals, we used a sample-specific pseudo-count,
\[
\varepsilon_i=0.5 \cdot \min\{C_{i,c} : C_{i,c}>0\},
\]
and computed
\[
\ell_{i,c}=\log_{10}(C_{i,c}+\varepsilon_i).
\]
Pairwise class log-ratios were then
\[
r_{i,c_1/c_2}=\ell_{i,c_1}-\ell_{i,c_2}.
\]
Positive LIN--CTL effects indicate enrichment of the numerator class in LIN.

\paragraph{Statistical Tests.}
For class-level comparisons, we computed log-ratios of class-wise abundances (e.g., $\log_{10}(\mathrm{PS}/\mathrm{SQDG})$), which are appropriate for compositional contrasts. For ratio analyses in Section 4, LIN and CTL were compared using a paired design restricted to the genotype intersection (genotypes observed in both conditions). For each of the 33 class ratios, we applied the paired Wilcoxon signed-rank test (two-sided) to the within-genotype LIN--CTL differences.

To assess robustness of directionality against individual genotype influence, we quantified leave-one-out jackknife stability at the pair level, defined as the proportion of single-pair omissions for which the sign of the median paired effect was preserved. A stability value of 1.0 indicates that the effect direction is unchanged when any one genotype pair is removed.

For each ratio, we report:
\begin{itemize}
\item \texttt{n\_pairs} = number of matched LIN--CTL genotype pairs used.
\item \texttt{median\_CTL}, \texttt{median\_LIN} = condition medians of the log-ratio (reported for interpretability).
\item \texttt{effect\_log10} = paired median shift on the log$_{10}$ scale (LIN$-$CTL).
\item \texttt{effect\_fc} = fold-change, computed as $10^{\texttt{effect\_log10}}$; values $>1$ indicate higher ratios in LIN.
\item \texttt{p\_wilcox} = paired Wilcoxon signed-rank $p$-value.
\item \texttt{p\_adj\_BH} = Benjamini--Hochberg adjusted $p$-value (FDR, $m=33$).
\item \texttt{jackknife\_stability} = leave-one-out sign stability (1.0 = direction invariant across all single-pair omissions).
\end{itemize}

For individual species, we applied the centered log-ratio (CLR) transformation to address compositional constraints:
\[
\mathrm{CLR}(x_i)=\ln\!\left(\frac{x_i}{g(\mathbf{x})}\right),
\]
where $g(\mathbf{x})$ is the geometric mean across species within a sample. For each of the 172 common species, we report:
\begin{itemize}
\item \texttt{median\_CLR\_CTL}, \texttt{median\_CLR\_LIN} = group medians of CLR values.
\item \texttt{effect\_CLR} = median CLR difference; \texttt{effect\_fc} = $e^{\texttt{effect\_CLR}}$.
\item \texttt{p\_adj\_BH} = BH-adjusted $p$-value.
\item \texttt{jackknife\_stability} = leave-one-out sign stability.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Principal Component Analysis (PCA)}  

We performed three complementary PCA workflows in R using \emph{stats} package (ref).  First, we ran PCA on the individual lipid species abundances.  Second, we summed abundances by lipid class (SuppTable1) (e.g. TG, DG, PC, MGDG, SQDG) and repeated PCA to highlight broader shifts in lipids. Third, we computed key log ratios metrics using OPLS-DA (explained below) and carried out PCA on these as well. In all cases, data were mean–centered and scaled prior to analysis. For each PCA, we retained the first two principal components for visualization. 

\subsection*{Genome-wide Association Studies (GWAS)} 

GWAS analyses were carried out for each lipid trait under each condition using the mixed linear model (MLM) featured in GEMMA (v2.3) (ref). To address population structure and relatedness, a centered relatedness matrix (kinship) was computed from SNP genotype data. For each lipid trait, the MLM was applied using the kinship matrix to handle population stratification effects. Besides individual traits, GWAS was also applied to summed lipid classes and all possible ratios (refer to Supplementary Table 1), and the first two PCs of the summed classes. GWAS analysis was conducted on the first two PCs for each class. A significance threshold of \(-\log_{10}(p) \geq 7\) was employed in order to account for multiple comparisons. 
%In contrast, a more lenient threshold of \(-\log_{10}(p) \geq 5\) was applied to all other analyses.

\subsection*{Gene Annotation}

SNPs were aligned with the Sorghum bicolor reference genome v3.1 (BTx623). For each marker, a 50 kb segment was designated, spanning 25 kb on either side, and all gene models within this area were retrieved. Functional annotations and homology were obtained from Phytozome (https://phytozome.jgi.doe.gov), SorghumBase (https://sorghumbase.com), and TAIR for corresponding Arabidopsis thaliana orthologs. Genes with known roles in N, P, cold tolerance, or lipid metabolism were specifically noted. We aggregated the frequency of each candidate gene within all lipid GWAS findings and marked those with the highest recurrence showing -log10(p-values) of 7 or greater.

\subsection*{Orthogonal Projections to Latent Structures–Discriminant Analysis (OPLS–DA)}

OPLS–DA was employed to identify the lipid class ratios that most effectively distinguish between CTL and LIN while reducing unrelated variance. The analysis was confined to ratios from glycero- and glycerophospholipid classes, specifically TG, DG, MG, DGDG, MGDG, PC, LPC, PE, LPE, PA, PS, and PG. All possible pairwise ratios between the mean log relative abundances of classes were computed as explained above. OPLS–DA was conducted using the \texttt{ropls} R package (v1.34.0), wherein the ratio matrix was denoted as $\mathbf{X}$ and the response $Y$ was coded as lipid class. A single predictive component (\texttt{predI = 1}) was defined, whereas the number of orthogonal components was determined through cross-validation (\texttt{orthoI = NA}). The decomposition is:
\[
  \mathbf{X} = T_{p} P_{p}^{T} \;+\; T_{o} P_{o}^{T} \;+\; E,
\]
where,
\begin{itemize}
  \item \(T_{p}\) and \(P_{p}\) are the predictive score and loading matrices capturing variation correlated with \(Y\),
  \item \(T_{o}\) and \(P_{o}\) are the orthogonal score and loading matrices capturing structured variation orthogonal to \(Y\),
  \item \(E\) is the residual matrix representing unexplained variation.
\end{itemize}

To address the issue of overfitting and adjust for differing sample sizes (C: $n=394$, LI: $n=363$), we employed a stratified seven-fold cross-validation approach with folds that are balanced to estimate $R^{2}_{Y}$ (the variance in $Y$ explained) and $Q^{2}$ (cross-validated predictivity). The significance of the model was evaluated using 500 label permutations. One-sided exact $p$-values were derived as $(\#\{\text{perm} \geq \text{obs}\} + 1)/(N_{\text{perm}} + 1)$. Furthermore, we present $R^{2}_{X}$ (the fraction of $X$ variance captured on the predictive axis) to enhance the interpretability of the score plots.

Ratios that discriminate between CTL and LIN were prioritized based on Variable Importance in Projection (VIP) scores, with VIP $> 1$ as the threshold. Since VIP signifies contribution rather than direction, the effect size direction was separately summarized through the calculation of group medians of the log ratios. 

\subsubsection*{Lipid Metabolic Network Analysis (LINEX2)}

We used the Lipid Network Explorer (LINEX2; \url{https://exbio.wzw.tum.de/linex/}) to reconstruct lipid networks and to identify condition enriched subgraphs for CTL vs. LIN, considering both the ratio and difference between them. Lipid names were standardized to align with species notation (class plus acyl composition). LINEX2 constructs a global species network based on curated reaction rules, including headgroup interconversions, (de)acylation/editing, elongation, desaturation, and overlays a quantitative association structure (Spearman correlations across samples) onto reaction edges. For enrichment, LINEX2 calculates substrate–product change scores for each reaction reagrading the CTL vs. LIN ratio and difference, employing a greedy local-search procedure to identify subgraphs that optimize the average substrate and product change. Since, \textit{Sorghum bicolor} is not implemented as a default organism in LINEX2, we selected \textit{Oryza sativa} (OSA) as the reference species for network construction.


\subsubsection*{Lipid Ontology Enrichment and Hierarchical Classification (LION/web)}

We conducted functional ontology for lipids using LION/web (Molenaar \emph{et al.}, 2019; \url{http://www.lipidontology.com}). Lipid nomenclature was standardized according to LIPID MAPS annotations. The default settings of LION/web were applied for enrichment statistics and multiple-testing correction. We retained terms for $q \leq 0.10$. A hierarchical classification analysis of individual lipids and functional ontologies was also performed using the LION/web.


\subsection*{Gradient boosting modeling and module-based SHAP interpretation}

\paragraph{Lipid data processing.}
To achieve variance stabilization and address zero values, each lipid intensity was log\textsubscript{10}-transformed with a pseudocount of 1:
\[
L_{ij}^{\prime} = \log_{10}(L_{ij} + 1),
\]
where $L_{ij}$ is the raw intensity of lipid $j$ in sample $i$. Following transformation, lipid columns were median-centered across samples through feature-wise subtraction of the column median, effectively removing global offsets while maintaining inter-sample variability.

\paragraph{Phenotype and population structure covariates.}
The phenotypic traits for SAP include plant height (PH) and days to anthesis (DTA) (Supp.\ Table~10), which served as response variables. To reduce the likelihood of the model capturing population structure rather than biological associations, both response variables and lipid predictors underwent adjustment through residualization with respect to the first three principal components (PCs), employing ordinary least squares (OLS) regression.

Let $y$ denote the phenotype vector and $X_{\mathrm{PC}}$ the PC design matrix (with intercept). We computed the residual phenotype as
\[
y_{\mathrm{adj}} = y - X_{\mathrm{PC}}(X_{\mathrm{PC}}^{\top}X_{\mathrm{PC}})^{-1}X_{\mathrm{PC}}^{\top}y,
\]
i.e., the residuals from the regression $y \sim X_{\mathrm{PC}}$.
For the lipid matrix $L$ (samples $\times$ features), we removed PC effects feature-wise via the same projection:
\[
L_{\mathrm{adj}} = L - X_{\mathrm{PC}}(X_{\mathrm{PC}}^{\top}X_{\mathrm{PC}})^{-1}X_{\mathrm{PC}}^{\top}L.
\]
The adjusted lipid matrix $L_{\mathrm{adj}}$ and the residual phenotype $y_{\mathrm{adj}}$ were used for all downstream modeling. Importantly, residualization was performed separately within each cross-validation fold to prevent data leakage: coefficients were estimated on training data only and applied to both training and test sets.

\paragraph{Lipid module construction via correlation clustering.}
Due to high correlation among lipid species within the same biochemical class, interpreting SHAP values for individual lipids can be misleading---importance tends to be distributed across correlated features, making it difficult to identify which lipids (or lipid programs) are truly predictive. To address this, we grouped lipids into correlation-based modules prior to interpretation.

We computed the pairwise Spearman correlation matrix across all lipid features and converted it to a distance matrix:
\[
D_{jk} = 1 - |r_{jk}|,
\]
where $r_{jk}$ is the Spearman correlation between lipids $j$ and $k$. Hierarchical clustering (average linkage) was performed on this distance matrix, and modules were identified using the dynamic tree cut algorithm (\texttt{dynamicTreeCut} in R) with \texttt{deepSplit = 2} and a minimum cluster size of 3. Lipids not assigned to any cluster were treated as singleton modules. This procedure yielded 27 lipid modules, each characterized by its dominant lipid class (e.g., TG, PC, DG).

\paragraph{XGBoost model training and cross-validation.}
We employed gradient-boosted trees (XGBoost) to model the PC-residualized phenotype as a function of the adjusted lipid features. XGBoost was chosen over random forests for its typically superior performance on tabular data and its built-in L1/L2 regularization, which encourages sparsity when features are correlated.

The model was trained with the following hyperparameters: learning rate $\eta = 0.1$, maximum tree depth = 6, row subsampling = 0.8, column subsampling = 0.8, L2 regularization $\lambda = 1$, and L1 regularization $\alpha = 0.5$. Models were trained for 150--200 boosting rounds.

To assess generalization, we used 5-fold cross-validation with PC residualization performed within each training fold to avoid information leakage. Model performance was summarized per fold using $R^2$, RMSE, and MAE, and we report the mean $\pm$ SD across folds. We report the mean $\pm$ standard deviation of $R^2$ across folds.

\paragraph{SHAP computation and module-level aggregation.}
For model interpretation, a final XGBoost model was trained on all samples (after PC residualization). SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) values were computed using the \texttt{shapviz} package in R, which provides exact TreeSHAP values for gradient-boosted tree ensembles. This yielded an $n \times p$ matrix of SHAP values $\phi_{ij}$, representing the contribution of lipid $j$ to the prediction for sample $i$.

To obtain module-level importance, we aggregated SHAP values within each module. For module $m$ containing lipids $\mathcal{L}_m$, we computed the module SHAP for each sample as:
\[
\Phi_{im} = \sum_{j \in \mathcal{L}_m} \phi_{ij},
\]
and the global module importance as:
\[
\overline{|\Phi|}_m = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}|\Phi_{im}|.
\]
Modules were ranked by $\overline{|\Phi|}_m$ in descending order.

\paragraph{Bootstrap stability analysis.}
To assess the stability of module importance rankings, we performed bootstrap resampling (50--100 iterations). In each bootstrap iteration, we: (1) resampled $n$ observations with replacement, (2) trained an XGBoost model on the bootstrap sample, (3) computed SHAP values and module-level aggregations, and (4) recorded the mean absolute module SHAP. From the bootstrap distribution, we computed 95\% confidence intervals for each module's importance. This procedure identifies modules whose importance is robust to sampling variability, as opposed to those whose rankings are unstable.

\paragraph{Cross-dataset and cross-environment validation.}
For plant height, we validated our findings using an independent phenotype dataset from \cite{Boyles2016}. We computed module SHAP importance separately for each dataset and assessed concordance by: (1) counting the overlap of top-10 modules between datasets, and (2) computing the Pearson correlation of module importance scores across all modules.

For flowering time, we extended the analysis across five environments (our DTA data plus four environments from \cite{Wei2025}: MI20, NE20, IA21\_1, IA21\_2). For each environment, we computed module SHAP importance and identified modules that consistently appeared in the top 10 across multiple environments. This cross-environment consistency analysis reveals lipid programs with reproducible associations, even when overall predictive power is limited.

\paragraph{Tall--short contrast analysis.}
To evaluate whether the top plant-height module (M1; TG-dominant) behaves as a practical marker, we computed a sample-level M1 score as the mean of standardized lipid abundances within M1. Using the same transformed matrix $L'_{ij}=\log_{10}(L_{ij}+1)$, each M1 lipid was $z$-scored across samples, and scores were aggregated as
\[
\mathrm{M1\_score}_i=\frac{1}{|\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{M1}}|}\sum_{j\in\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{M1}}}\frac{L'_{ij}-\mu_j}{\sigma_j},
\]
where $\mu_j$ and $\sigma_j$ are the across-sample mean and standard deviation for lipid $j$. We then quantified association with plant height using Spearman correlation on raw values and on PC-residualized values ($\mathrm{M1\_score}_{\mathrm{adj}}$ vs.\ $\mathrm{PH}_{\mathrm{adj}}$). To test group-level separability, samples were stratified into short and tall groups (bottom and top quartiles of PH), compared with a two-sided Wilcoxon rank-sum test, and summarized by median difference, Cliff's delta, and AUC (probability that a randomly selected tall sample has a higher M1 score than a randomly selected short sample). This module-level score was used as the primary marker metric, with compositional analyses treated as robustness checks.
