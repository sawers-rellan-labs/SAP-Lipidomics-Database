\begin{abstract}
Sorghum is a globally important cereal crop whose genetic diversity underlies its ability to adapt to nutrient deficiency and temperature stress, traits that are increasingly relevant under climate change. Lipids play a major role combating these stresses. However, population-scale lipidomic datasets remain scarce. Here, we describe the Sorghum Lipid Database (SoLD), a curated lipidomics resource derived from the Sorghum Association Panel (SAP; ~380 accessions) profiled at an early developmental stage in two contrasting field conditions: a conventional, nutrient-sufficient control and a low-input, early-planted treatment imposing combined nitrogen and phosphorus limitation together with temperature-related stress. Using high-resolution liquid chromatography–mass spectrometry, we quantified 244 lipid species across both environments and detected a reproducible lipidome reprogramming under low-input conditions. This remodeling was characterized by marked depletion of sulfoquinovosyldiacylglycerols (SQDG), enrichment of triacylglycerols (TG), redistribution within major phospholipid pools which includes a phosphatidylserine (PS)-centered remodeling, and a coordinated lysophospholipid adjustments, reflected in shifts in lysophosphatidylcholine (LPC)/lysophosphatidylethanolamine (LPE) ratios. Genome-wide association studies (GWAS) conducted on low-input individual lipid species as well as on class-level sums and their ratios highlighted hundreds of candidate loci. These included regulatory modules in glycerol-backbone and lipid biosynthesis, diacylglycerol (DG)–TG partitioning, phospholipid turnover, and regulatory pathways associated with nutrient and cold stress responses. Furthermore, gradient-boosted decision tree models coupled with SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP)-based interpretation revealed that TG-enriched lipid modules provide robust predictive power for plant height, whereas flowering time is only weakly predictable from single-stage lipidomic profiles. All the information regarding the database and the experiment can be freely accessible through a Shiny application: https://github.com/sawers-rellan-labs/SAP-Lipidomics-Database.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

%Add Shiny app section. 

\lettrine[lines=2]{\color{color2}S}orghum (\textit{Sorghum bicolor} L.) is a major C\textsubscript{4} cereal and bioenergy crop, grown for its resilience in environmentally harsh conditions. Its ability to maintain productivity under cold stress and nutrient limitation underscores its growing importance for global food and energy security (\cite{Paterson2009}; \cite{Ngara2014}; \cite{George2022}). Sorghum also harbors substantial standing genetic variation shaped by domestication and wide geographic distribution. Diversity panels such as the Sorghum Association Panel (SAP) provide a robust framework for linking genotype to agronomic traits via genome-wide association studies and multi-environment analyses (\cite{Boatwright2022}).

Plants routinely face adverse environmental conditions that limit crop productivity, including nutrient deficiencies, especially nitrogen (N) and phosphorus (P), and low temperatures among the most common constraints (\cite{Schluter2012}; \cite{Silveira2018}; \cite{Emendack2021}). N limitation alters root system architecture and N acquisition, while P scarcity poses an escalating challenge due to finite mineral P reserves and low fertilizer-use efficiency (\cite{Nacry2013}; \cite{LopezValdivia2025}; \cite{Medici2019}). Cold stress, including chilling (0–15\textbackslash{}textdegree C) and freezing (<0\textbackslash{}textdegree C), disrupts membrane fluidity, slows metabolism, and can cause physiological damage that reduces yield (\cite{Ding2019}; \cite{Guo2025}). Together, these abiotic stresses trigger acclimation programs involving extensive membrane reorganization and remodeling of lipid metabolism (\cite{Bohn2007}; \cite{Siebers2015}; \cite{Xu2025}).

Lipids are central to plant stress physiology as key membrane components, energy stores, and precursors of signaling molecules. Stress-induced lipid remodeling stabilizes membranes, adjusts photosynthesis, and reshapes signaling, and is a conserved response to nutrient limitation and low temperature (\cite{Hou2016}; \cite{Rawat2021}; \cite{Li2020}; \cite{Bhattacharya2022}). Phosphate starvation responses, for example, are coordinated by conserved transcriptional networks centered on Phosphate Starvation Response1 (\textit{PHR1}) and related transcription factors (\cite{Rubio2001};\cite{Bustos2010}), which are closely linked to changes in membrane lipid composition and the reprogramming of lipid metabolism. However, population-scale plant lipidomics resources remain limited compared with transcriptomic datasets, particularly those that are quantitatively robust, computationally interoperable, and linked to natural genetic variation and agronomic traits.

Two main challenges limit interpretability and reusability in large-scale lipidomics. First, LC–MS measurements suffer from systematic signal drift, batch effects, and spatial artifacts from field layout. QC-based normalization methods such as SERRF can markedly reduce technical variance when QC samples are well designed (\cite{Fan2019}), and spatial mixed models like SpATS adjust for field-position effects using two-dimensional penalized splines (\cite{RodriguezAlvarez2018}). Second, lipid annotation practices and nomenclature vary widely across studies. Platforms such as GNPS support spectral library matching and collaborative curation (\cite{Wang2016}), while ontology-based resources like LION/web enable enrichment analyses over lipid classes and physicochemical properties (\cite{Molenaar2019}). Together, these approaches support lipidomics datasets that are analytically robust and comparable across experiments.

We present the Sorghum Lipid Database (SoLD), a curated resource linking annotated lipid species to genetic variation in the SAP. Using leaf samples from [developmental stage], we quantified lipid profiles in two contrasting field environments: (i) an optimal control with sufficient nutrients and standard planting (CTL) , and (ii) a combined low-N, low-P, and an earlier planting date replicating cold-stress (LIN). Under LIN conditions, the population showed robust, reproducible lipid remodeling, including sulfoquinovosyldiacylglycerol (SQDG) depletion, triacylglycerol (TG) enrichment, phosphatidylserine (PS) redistribution within the phospholipid network, and coordinated changes in lysophospholipids (LPC/LPE). These patterns indicate coordinated redistribution across interconnected lipid pools rather than isolated class-specific changes. We integrated these profiles with genome-wide association analyses to prioritize candidate loci spanning phosphate-starvation regulators, N metabolism, lipid biosynthesis, and cold stress, generating genetically informed hypotheses for lipid remodeling under combined nutrient and temperature stress. SoLD provides standardized outputs: (i) compositional and ratio-based values and visualization of lipid classes and molecular species; (ii) genome-wide association results from linear mixed models (\cite{Zhou2012}); and (iii) predictive models of agronomic traits using gradient-boosted trees (\cite{Chen2016}) with SHAP-based interpretation (\cite{Lundberg2017}). Finally, we show that TG-enriched lipid modules reproducibly predict plant height across independent datasets, supporting lipids as biomarkers.

\section{Results}

\subsection*{Quality control and normalization of LC-MS features}

We profiled early-stage lipids from SAP grown in two contrasting field conditions: (i) a nutrient-sufficient control field with a normal planting date (CTL) and (ii) a low-input field (LIN) with reduced N and P fertilization and earlier planting to mimic cold stress. The workflow (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1_lipidomics_landscape}A) comprised tissue collection and extraction, high-resolution LC-MS lipidomic profiling, GNPS-assisted lipid annotation, post-acquisition data correction with SERRF and SpATS (\cite{Fan2019}; \cite{RodriguezAlvarez2018}), and data dissemination via the SoLD Shiny app (https://github.com/sawers-rellan-labs/SAP-Lipidomics-Database).

Run-order quality control indicated stable analytical performance in both conditions (Supplementary Figure~\ref{fig:suppfig_s1_qc} A,B). Throughout the sequence, sample TICs matched expectations from system suitability checks, internal standards, and blanks. TIC variability among QC samples was low (CTL QC TIC CV = 7.5\%; LIN QC TIC CV = 6.0\%; Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s1_qc} A,B). SERRF normalization improved technical precision, reducing median QC-RSD from 3.6\% to 2.0\% in CTL and from 1.6\% to 0.3\% in LIN (Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s1_qc} C,D). The proportion of features with QC-RSD $<30\%$ increased from 75.7\% to 94.1\% in CTL and from 77.3\% to 89.3\% in LIN (Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s1_qc} C,D). PCA of normalized data showed tighter sample clustering than unprocessed data (Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s1_qc} E,F). Because samples were field-grown, we also corrected field-position effects using spatial modeling (SpATS) in R, which largely removed broad row and column patterns (Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s1_qc} G,H). These steps yielded normalized, spatially corrected data used for all downstream analyses.

\subsection*{Lipid variation across genotypes}

To test whether lipid differences between CTL and LIN reflected systematic, population-wide reprogramming rather than a few outlier genotypes, we performed two-sample Wilcoxon rank-sum tests with a leave-one-out jackknife stability analysis. For lipid class comparisons, we analyzed pairwise log-ratios from class-level summaries; all 33 class log-ratios differed significantly between conditions (BH-adjusted p<0.05, \cite{Benjamini1995}) with maximal jackknife stability (1.0; Supplementary Table S1). At the species level, we applied a centered log-ratio (CLR) transformation to account for compositionality (\cite{Aitchison1982}). Of the 172 lipid species shared between conditions, 170 (99\%) differed significantly (Supplementary Table S2), and all 172 had perfect jackknife stability (1.0), indicating that the direction of each change was unaffected by excluding any single genotype. This pattern held across lipid classes (Supplementary Table S3), showing that LIN-induced lipidomic remodeling is a robust, uniform response across SAP genotypes rather than an effect of a few outlier accessions.

Given this robustness, we next identified lipids with the greatest genetic variance. MGDG(18:3/18:3) showed the highest variance in both environments (Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s2_topvar} A,B; Supplementary Table~S4). Seven of the ten most variable lipids were shared between CTL and LIN (Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s2_topvar} C), indicating a conserved subset of lipid traits with strong genotype sensitivity across environments. Within this high-variance subset, PC species were the most common class in both conditions (CTL: 7/10; LIN: 6/10; Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s2_topvar} S2D; Supplementary Table S4), justifying their prioritization for subsequent genetic mapping and predictive modeling.

\subsection{Lipidome overview for control and low-input SAP}

Across both environments, we quantified 244 lipid species, including 153 shared, 49 CTL-specific, and 42 LIN-specific species (Supplementary Fig.~S4A; Supplementary Table~S6 A,B,C). TG and PC were the most diverse classes in each environment (TG: 71 in CTL, 69 in LIN; PC: 39 in CTL, 38 in LIN; Supplementary Table~S6 A,B,C), and most species belonged to the glycerolipid and glycerophospholipid (Supplementary Fig.~S4B,D). PCA showed that lipid class strongly structured the dataset, with TGs separating from membrane lipids and galactolipids forming distinct clusters in both conditions (Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s5_pca} A,B).

At the class level (\%TIC), the lipidome was dominated by galactolipids and phospholipids (Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s3_comp} A). Under CTL, MGDG contributed 34.5\% of mean TIC and declined to 32.1\% under LIN, while PC remained relatively stable (25.6\% in CTL vs. 24.8\% in LIN). TG increased from 2.7\% (CTL) to 5.4\% (LIN), and SQDG decreased from 2.9\% (CTL) to 1.4\% (LIN) (Supplementary Fig.~S3A). Compositional contrast analyzes confirmed these trends and revealed coordinated headgroup rebalancing within the phospholipid network. CLR contrasts identified PS as one of the strongest positively shifted classes (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1_lipidomics_landscape}B; Supplementary Table~S5 A), and TG-referenced ALR contrasts showed PS increasing relative to multiple phospholipid pools (e.g., PC, PE, PG) despite modest absolute \%TIC shifts (Supplementary Table~S5 B, Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s3_comp}B). Lysophospholipids also showed reproducible remodeling, with LPC/LPE dynamics under LIN consistent with stress-responsive phospholipid turnover ( \cite{Hou2016}; \cite{Rawat2021}) rather than uniform scaling of total lyso-lipid abundance (Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s3_comp}B; Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1_lipidomics_landscape}; Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s7_ratios}). Collectively, these results define four LI-associated lipid signatures compared to CTL: (i) SQDG depletion, (ii) TG enrichment, (iii) PS enrichment relative to other phospholipid headgroups, and (iv) lysophospholipid remodeling (LPC/LPE).

To characterize structural remodeling beyond total class abundance, we summarized each lipid class in a reduced chemical space defined by its abundance-weighted mean total carbon number and double-bond count across constituent molecular species (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1_lipidomics_landscape}C). In this space, a shift in a class centroid reflects changes in the within-class species mixture (e.g., relatively more longer- vs. shorter-chain species), rather than changes in absolute signal alone. TG occupied the high-carbon region and shifted toward higher weighted mean total carbon under LIN, with only modest changes in mean unsaturation (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1_lipidomics_landscape}C), indicating preferential enrichment of longer-chain TG species under LIN. PE showed the largest increase in unsaturation among membrane-associated classes (from ~2.68 to 3.38 weighted mean double bonds, with minimal carbon change), consistent with relatively more unsaturated PE species under LIN. We interpret these centroid shifts as descriptive evidence of coordinated acyl-chain remodeling (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1_lipidomics_landscape}B), without inferring specific enzymatic fluxes. Most other classes maintained distinct carbon/unsaturation signatures with limited centroid movement, suggesting tighter compositional constraints on major membrane-associated lipid pools.

LION enrichment analysis confirmed these shifts. LIN increased ontology terms linked to neutral-lipid accumulation and storage, including \textit{triacylglycerols [GL0301]}, \textit{glycerolipids [GL]}, \textit{diacylglycerols [GL0201]}, \textit{lipid storage}, and \textit{lipid droplet} (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1_lipidomics_landscape}D) (\cite{Molenaar2019}). In contrast, CTL was enriched for membrane phospholipid categories and descriptors, including \textit{glycerophospholipids [GP]}, \textit{glycerophosphocholines [GP01]/diacylglycerophosphocholines [GP0101]}, \textit{glycerophosphoethanolamines [GP02]/diacylglycerophosphoethanolamines [GP0201]}, and \textit{membrane component} (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1_lipidomics_landscape}D). Thus, LION supports that LIN is associated with a coordinated shift toward TG and concomitant phospholipid/membrane remodeling detected by both abundance contrasts and acyl-chain chemical-space changes.


\subsection*{Recurrent GWAS signals across low-input lipid traits converge on shared stress- and growth-related processes}

We next tested whether recurrent gene–lipid associations under LIN converged on shared biological processes across both individual lipid traits and higher-order class-sum and class-ratio (SR) traits. We applied an empirical recurrence filter based on repeated detection across phenotypes. For the individual-lipid GWAS, we retained gene–trait associations with $p \le 1\times10^{-7}$ for each phenotype and counted how often each gene recurred across traits. Under LIN conditions, 141 lipid phenotypes had at least one significant gene association, yielding 10,884 unique candidate genes. Restricting to genes detected in $\geq 7$ phenotypes reduced this to 48 recurrent candidates (approximately the top 0.5\% of the recurrence distribution). Of these, 30 genes had functional annotations in our GO-based summary, while the rest were uncharacterized or sparsely annotated. The most recurrent loci were associated with up to 11 phenotypes, indicating that only a small subset of genes showed repeatedly detectable effects across the LIN lipidome.

Although this recurrent gene set was not dominated by canonical lipid-metabolic enzymes with our filtering scheme, its annotations converged on biological processes likely engaged under LIN management. These included nutrient-status signaling and remobilization (tryptophan synthase genes, a phosphate-starvation regulator), cell-wall remodeling and growth modulation (expansin genes, a callose synthase), and defense-associated functions (thaumatin-like and defensin-like proteins, immunity-related kinases). Together, this pattern indicates that LIN conditions elicit a broader physiological response than nutrient limitation alone, integrating nutrient stress with structural remodeling and increased defense under reduced agrochemical inputs. Thus, the recurrent GWAS signals from individual lipid traits appear to capture coordinated stress adaptation and whole-plant acclimation, rather than changes limited to lipid biochemistry.

We next tested whether this pattern extended to SR. Using the same gene-level threshold ($p \le 1\times10^{-7}$), the combined analysis of class sums and ratios identified 6,516 candidate genes. Restricting attention to genes detected in $\geq 50$ phenotypes yielded 31 highly recurrent candidates (about the top 0.5\%). As with individual lipid species, only a few loci showed repeated effects across the LIN lipidome, but these recurrent loci were enriched for higher-order regulation of lipid-class balance rather than specific molecular lipids.

Functional GO annotation and orthology-based curation showed that these recurrent SR candidates are biased toward processes related to growth, stress acclimation, and regulatory control, rather than canonical lipid metabolism. Growth- and development-related candidates included a pentatricopeptide repeat protein orthologous to the slow-growth regulator \textit{SLO1}, an SS18 N-terminal domain protein implicated in seed and leaf ontogeny, and a DOG1-domain transcription-associated factor. A second subset was linked to abiotic stress acclimation and surface-barrier biosynthesis, including a CER1/WDA1-like aldehyde oxygenase for wax and cutin formation and a cullin-like protein involved in cold- and salt-responsive signaling. Additional defense- and signaling-related loci included a CAP/PR1-like protein, kinase-associated genes, and transport genes implicated in auxin and ion homeostasis. Overall, LIN lipid class sums and ratios appear to report an integrated physiological state shaped by developmental plasticity, stress adaptation, and defense-associated regulatory networks.

\subsection*{Recurrent GWAS signals across control lipid traits emphasize basal metabolism, growth regulation, and cell-wall-associated processes}

We also explored whether recurrent gene–lipid associations under CTL conditions converged on common biological processes across both individual lipid species and SR traits. For the individual-lipid GWAS, we kept gene-level associations with $p \le 1 \times 10^{-7}$ for each phenotype and counted how often each gene was associated across traits. This yielded 3,478 candidate genes under control conditions. Applying a recurrence threshold of association with $\geq 13$ phenotypes, approximately the top 0.5\% of genes reduced this to 17 highly recurrent candidates/

Functional GO annotation of these recurrent candidates highlighted roles in lipid metabolism and transport, photosynthesis and chloroplast function, and central carbohydrate metabolism. The most strongly recurrent genes were mainly lipid-related, including a lipoxygenase, a lipase-like hydrolase, and an acyltransferase-like gene involved in isoprenoid and farnesyl diphosphate biosynthesis, pointing to repeated associations with lipid turnover and lipid-derived signaling. Other recurrent genes encoded putative peptide transporters, a photosystem II chlorophyll-binding protein, Rubisco activase, and enzymes of glucose- and fructose-phosphate metabolism, such as glucose-6-phosphate 1-epimerase and a phosphofructokinase-related protein. Overall, these recurrent GWAS signals from individual lipid traits in the CTL environment are closely linked to core metabolism, chloroplast-associated functions, and membrane transport.

We extended the recurrence analysis to SR phenotypes under CTL conditions. Using the same gene-level threshold (\(p \le 1 \times 10^{-7}\)), the combined control sum/ratio analysis identified 4,385 putative loci. Restricting to genes detected in \(\geq 72\) phenotypes (the upper ~0.5\% of the recurrence distribution) reduced this to 20 highly recurrent candidates, again yielding a compact set of loci with repeated effects across many traits. For CTL SR, these loci were mainly enriched for genes involved in growth regulation, cell-wall dynamics, signaling, and development, rather than a broad stress-response signature.

Functional annotation and orthology-based curation highlighted several coherent themes. Many recurrent loci were linked to growth and development, including an \textit{NGR2/LAZY4}-related gene, a calcium-dependent protein kinase (\textit{CDPK26}-like), and a large ribosomal subunit protein uL15, orthologous to a pollen-development factor. A second group was associated with cell-wall organization and surface remodeling, including a polygalacturonase, a receptor-like kinase with predicted polysaccharide binding, and an elongation-factor-related gene implicated in cell-wall formation. We also detected a wound- and jasmonate-inducible \textit{TIFY} gene, consistent with roles in defense and tissue repair, and an ortholog of a microfibrillar-associated protein linked to yield-related traits. Overall, these recurrent CTL SR loci indicate that lipid-class balance under control conditions is tightly coupled to endogenous growth, cell-wall remodeling, and developmental programs, with relatively limited input from inducible stress-response pathways compared with the low-input condition.

\subsection*{LINEX-based reaction mapping and ratio-network rewiring}

To place lipid associations in a biochemical context, we queried LINEX2 (\cite{Morgat2016Rhea}) for RHEA-annotated reactions linking glycerolipids and glycerophospholipids. The curated subnetwork (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig4_linex_network}A; Supplementary Table~S11) captures four candidate branches connecting DG/MG, TG, and lysophospholipid pools: (i) PC+DG$\leftrightarrow$LPC+TG (LCAT-like), (ii) TG$\leftrightarrow$DG (PNPLA1-labeled lipase branch), (iii) PE+DG$\leftrightarrow$TG+LPE (LRO1/PDAT-like), and (iv) DG+MG$\leftrightarrow$TG (PNPLA3-labeled branch). These links are annotation-based hypotheses and do not directly establish \emph{in vivo} flux direction. As CTL and LIN were sampled in different field years, all LIN--CTL contrasts in this section are interpreted as between-trial compositional differences rather than isolated causal effects of LIN.

To extend beyond single-edge annotation, we built class log-ratio networks from class totals using the same compositional framework explained in \emph{Methods}.  Within each condition, we computed Spearman correlations among ratio features (3003 possible ratio--ratio edges) and controlled for multiplicity by BH adjustment. Of these 3003 edges, 2102 (CTL) and 2100 (LIN) were significant at BH-adjusted $p<0.05$. For visualization, we retained strong edges defined as BH-adjusted \(p<0.05\) and \(|r|\ge 0.55\), and then plotted the top 140 edges per condition (ranked by \(|r|\)) for matched network density (Supplementary Fig.~\ref{fig:suppfig_ratio_network_ab}A,B).

We then quantified differential rewiring between conditions (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig4_linex_network}C) using edge-wise $\Delta r=r_{\mathrm{LI}}-r_{\mathrm{C}}$. Across all 3003 edges, rewiring classes included 278 sign-flips, 586 gained-in-LIN edges, 588 lost-in-LIN edges, and 257 strength-rewired edges (edge-state definitions in Supplementary Table~SXX). For the displayed high-effect subset (\(|\Delta r|\ge 0.25\), with significance in at least one condition), 98 edges were retained, with 48 sign-flips, 32 lost in LIN, 17 gained in LIN, and 1 strength-rewired edge (significant in both conditions with the same sign but changed magnitude). Representative sign-flip edges include DG/PC--LPC/PS ($r_{\mathrm{C}}=+0.609$, $r_{\mathrm{LI}}=-0.322$, $\Delta r=-0.931$), PC/PG--PG/PS ($-0.581\rightarrow+0.188$, $\Delta r=+0.770$), and PC/PE--PE/PA ($+0.320\rightarrow-0.444$, $\Delta r=-0.764$). The predominance of sign reversals indicates qualitative reorganization of lipid-ratio coordination, not merely uniform scaling of lipid pools.

As a mechanistic cross-check, we translated each LINEX reaction into a simple class-level balance score per sample. For sample \(i\), let \(\ell_{i,c}=\log_{10}(C_{i,c}+\varepsilon_i)\) denote the log-abundance of lipid class \(c\). For each reaction, we computed
\[
S_{i,\mathrm{rxn}}=\text{mean}(\ell_{i,\mathrm{products}})-\text{mean}(\ell_{i,\mathrm{substrates}}),
\]
so that a larger \(S_{i,\mathrm{rxn}}\) indicates a relative enrichment of the product side within that sample. Concretely, we used:
\begin{itemize}
\item LCAT*: \(S_i=\mathrm{mean}(\ell_{i,\mathrm{LPC}},\ell_{i,\mathrm{TG}})-\mathrm{mean}(\ell_{i,\mathrm{PC}},\ell_{i,\mathrm{DG}})\);
\item PNPLA1: \(S_i=\ell_{i,\mathrm{DG}}-\ell_{i,\mathrm{TG}}\);
\item LRO1: \(S_i=\mathrm{mean}(\ell_{i,\mathrm{TG}},\ell_{i,\mathrm{LPE}})-\mathrm{mean}(\ell_{i,\mathrm{PE}},\ell_{i,\mathrm{DG}})\);
\item PNPLA3: \(S_i=\ell_{i,\mathrm{TG}}-\mathrm{mean}(\ell_{i,\mathrm{DG}},\ell_{i,\mathrm{MG}})\).
\end{itemize}

We compared scores between LIN and CTL using paired, genotype-matched tests (\(n = 355\) pairs) and summarized effects as \(\mathrm{median}(S_{\mathrm{LI}}) - \mathrm{median}(S_{\mathrm{C}})\). Positive LIN–CTL effect sizes indicate a shift toward products, and negative values indicate a shift toward substrates. LIN–CTL effects were positive for PNPLA3 (+0.327), LCAT* (+0.238), and LRO1 (+0.232), and negative for PNPLA1 (-0.268), with BH–adjusted \(p < 10^{-56}\) (Supplementary Table~SXX; Fig.~\ref{fig:fig4_linex_network}E). Thus, branches producing TG-associated product states (and lysophospholipid coproducts in phospholipid-linked transfers) were directionally consistent with the inferred remodeling pattern, while the TG$\rightarrow$DG hydrolytic branch showed the opposite trend.

These network findings are concordant with the lipidome overview (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1_lipidomics_landscape}). TG enrichment, SQDG depletion, PS/phospholipid headgroup rebalancing, and LPC/LPE remodeling were identified here as coordinated changes in ratio architecture rather than isolated abundance shifts. Together, LINEX topology, differential rewiring, and reaction-consistency scoring support a coherent between-trial remodeling program and provide mechanistic hypotheses for future within-trial validation.

Finally, we cross-referenced LINEX reaction branches with GWAS candidate genes (Supplementary Table~\ref{tab:supp_linex_gwas_gene_support}; branch-level summary in Supplementary Table~\ref{tab:supp_linex_gwas_branch_support}). LIN showed strong support for LCAT-like and TG/DG-cycle genes, including SORBI\_3001G103800 (LCAT-like 1; 19 LIN traits, 14 reaction-linked), SORBI\_3001G041900 (PNPLA-domain TAG lipase-like; 8 LIN traits, 8 reaction-linked), and SORBI\_3010G170000 (DGAT1; 22 LIN traits, 7 reaction-linked), with best $p$-values ranging from $3.16\times10^{-15}$ to $1.13\times10^{-9}$. These concordant network and genetic signals support a coherent between-trial remodeling program and provide mechanistic hypotheses for future within-trial validation.

\section{Discussion}

Our lipidomics analysis indicates that the SAP undergoes substantial lipid remodeling under LIN conditions, with TG, PS, LPC/LPE, and SQDG showing the most pronounced changes. We identified candidate genes associated with these lipid traits and with broader stress-response pathways, but functional validation will be needed to assess their roles. We also found that lipidomic features can predict some phenotypes, such as plant height, whereas the prediction of flowering time was weaker. Finally, we developed an interactive Shiny application to support the visualization and exploration of the dataset.


\subsection{Nutrient sensing and recycling pathways provide upstream context for lipid variation under LowInput}

Several LIN candidates converge on a common theme of nutrient stress and remobilization. Two adjacent loci, \texttt{SORBI\_3001G056400} and \texttt{SORBI\_3001G056500}, encode tryptophan synthase family members, placing amino acid metabolism upstream of the Trp–IAA regulatory module. Allelic variation that affects tryptophan availability is mechanistically interpretable because tryptophan is the main precursor of indole-3-acetic acid (IAA). This links nitrogen-stress physiology, hormone-regulated development, and lipid phenotypes without implying direct enzymatic action on those lipids. (\cite{Stepanova2008,Zhao2001,Fu2022,Liu2024,Wang2025}).

Another nutrient-associated candidate is \texttt{SORBI\_3001G384500}, whose maize ortholog, \texttt{Zm00001d029012}, is a strong candidate for nitrogen-uptake traits in seedling waxy maize. This ortholog was consistently detected across environments by integrating GWAS and co-expression network analyses and was one of four genes repeatedly associated with nitrogen-uptake phenotypes \citep{Luo2025}. Although these data do not prove an equivalent function in sorghum, the cross-species concordance supports interpreting the LRR-RLK–like signal as part of nutrient-stress signaling, consistent with established roles of LRR-RLK families in plant development and abiotic/biotic stress responses \citep{Dufayard2017}.

In parallel, the phosphate-starvation regulator \texttt{SORBI\_3001G384300} (a \textit{PHR1}-like MYB-CC transcription factor) supports a phosphorus-homeostasis axis already strongly implicated by phospholipid-associated GWAS signals. \textit{PHR1} coordinates transcriptional responses to phosphate limitation as a key upstream regulator of phosphate-starvation programs that coincide with phospholipid remodeling, making a PHR1-like association more consistent with regulatory variation in nutrient-stress signaling than with direct effects on lipid enzymatic activity (\cite{Pant2015,Rubio2001,Bustos2010,Nilsson2007,Rouached2011}).

Finally, another candidate gene \texttt{SORBI\_3002G117000} encodes an ATG8-interacting protein, consistent with roles in starvation-induced recycling pathways. In plants, ATG8-interacting proteins such as ATI1/ATI2 act as selective autophagy cargo receptors during carbon starvation and related stresses, and ATG8 itself is lipidated with phosphatidylethanolamine (PE) during autophagosome biogenesis (\cite{Honig2012,Wu2021ATI,Michaeli2014}). Thus, an ATI-like candidate in our lipid GWAS supports genetic variation in nutrient-stress–induced recycling capacity that might modulate membrane and metabolite turnover under LIN conditions.

Thus, Trp biosynthesis, nitrate-linked signaling candidates, phosphate-starvation regulation, and ATG8-mediated recycling define a coherent nutrient-stress framework that may underlie the enrichment of nutrient-signaling candidates among lipid-associated loci under LIN management.


\subsection{Cell-wall remodeling candidates implicate wall plasticity and growth adjustment under LowInput}

A prominent theme among the LIN candidates is cell-wall remodeling, highlighted by \texttt{SORBI\_3004G107800} (annotated as a \textit{CALS8}/GSL-type callose synthase in \textit{Arabidopsis}) and three expansin-family loci (\texttt{SORBI\_3007G018000}, \texttt{SORBI\_3001G238100}, \texttt{SORBI\_3001G238000}). Callose, a $\beta$-1,3-glucan polymer, is rapidly synthesized and degraded across diverse developmental and stress contexts, modulating wall permeability and mechanics. Thus, natural allelic variation at a callose synthase locus reflects variation in stress-inducible wall reinforcement and/or wall gating under nutrient limitation and early-season cold.

The expansin candidates provide a complementary, well-characterized route to altered wall plasticity. Expansins are a conserved class of plant cell-wall proteins that promote slow, irreversible wall extension under turgor-driven tensile stress by loosening cellulose-enriched networks without detectable hydrolytic activity \citep{McQueenMason1995,Cosgrove2000,Cosgrove2016,Cosgrove2024}. Current models posit that expansins disrupt noncovalent interactions between aligned wall polysaccharides, enabling controlled wall yielding in response to developmental and environmental cues \citep{Cosgrove2024,Ma2013}. Plant expansins are classified into EXPAs, EXPBs, and expansin-like EXLA/EXLB families, with extensive functional diversification. EXPAs are central to acid-growth responses and can act at cellulose–cellulose junctions with maximal activity at acidic pH, whereas EXPBs show strong activity in arabinoxylan-rich grass walls and can facilitate rapid wall penetration or loosening \citep{Kende2004,Cosgrove2024,Cosgrove1997,Valdivia2007,Valdivia2009,Yennawar2006,Tabuchi2011}. Across species, expansins are repeatedly linked to growth and stress acclimation, and transgenic studies show that altered expansin expression can modify cell length, wall stiffness, and whole-plant architecture \citep{Ma2013,Marowa2016,Samalova2022,Wang2024}. 

Together, the callose synthase candidate and multiple expansin loci support a coherent view in which LIN-associated genetic variation includes regulators of both wall deposition and wall loosening. This candidate set offers an upstream basis for the plastic adjustment of growth and cell morphology under abiotic stress, while maintaining a conservative inference and downstream phenotypic consequences still require targeted functional validation.

\subsection{Growth and developmental regulators among LowInput candidates implicate organellar function, reproductive programs, and architectural control}

Beyond loci linked to nutrient stress and cell wall biology, the LIN candidate set includes several genes with established roles in growth and development. \texttt{SORBI\_3001G256400}, the closest homolog of \textit{SLO1} (SLOw growth1) in \textit{Arabidopsis}, encodes a mitochondrial pentatricopeptide repeat (PPR) protein required for specific RNA-editing events in complex~I subunit transcripts \citep{Sung2010}. \textit{SLO1} disruption in \emph{Arabidopsis} causes pleiotropic growth defects consistent with impaired mitochondrial function, supporting a model in which natural variation at an \textit{SLO1}-like locus modulates whole-plant vigor via altered organelle performance \citep{Sung2010}. 

Two additional candidates highlight roles for transcriptional regulation in development. \texttt{SORBI\_3002G153100} encodes a \textit{TGA10} (bZIP65)-like transcription factor. In \emph{Arabidopsis}, \textit{TGA10} functions redundantly with \textit{TGA9} and double mutants reveal an essential role in anther development and male fertility via interactions with ROXY-type glutaredoxins and regulation of tapetal gene expression \citep{Murmu2010}. \texttt{SORBI\_3001G101700} is similar to a \textit{GIF1}/ANGUSTIFOLIA3-like transcriptional coactivator. In maize, \textit{GIF1} (AN3) forms complexes with growth-regulating factors  to control leaf and internode elongation and meristem determinacy, and loss-of-function alleles cause dwarfism and severe architectural defects \citep{Zhang2018}. 

Thus, \textit{SLO1}-, \textit{TGA10}-, and \textit{GIF1}-like candidates indicate that LIN-associated signals include regulators of organelle function and developmental transcriptional networks. These loci offer a mechanistic framework for interpreting growth and fitness variation under LIN field stress. The causal variants and their specific contributions to lipid and phenotypic associations will be planned for targeted functional validation.

\subsection{Cuticle barrier formation and ubiquitin-mediated proteostasis as cold-linked candidates under LowInput}

Two LIN candidates are consistent with upstream processes that influence cold and stress performance without directly modifying the measured lipid species. \texttt{SORBI\_3001G222700}, similar to \textit{CER1} protein, is a wax/cuticle-associated locus homologous to the \textit{CER1}/WDA1 in rice. It is involved in very-long-chain fatty acid–derived wax biosynthesis and cuticle formation \citep{Jung2006WDA1}. Cuticular waxes form a hydrophobic barrier on aerial epidermal tissues and limit non-stomatal water loss while protecting against diverse stresses \citep{Jung2006WDA1,LeeSuh2022}. Temperature stress often alters wax deposition and composition, and cold can increase leaf wax load in multiple species, linking wax-related genetic variation to cold-associated field performance \citep{LeeSuh2022}. 

\texttt{SORBI\_3002G141600} has a homolog in rice as a cullin-like protein (CUL1-like). Cullins act as core scaffolds in cullin–RING E3 ubiquitin ligase complexes that control protein turnover in major hormone and stress-response pathways. Cullin-mediated ubiquitination has been implicated in cold-response regulation by modulating the stability of key transcription factors in canonical cold signaling cascades, including ICE1/CBFs \citep{WangXie2024}. 

Together, a \textit{WDA1-like} wax locus and a CUL1-like proteostasis locus provide a strong, cold-linked framework for interpreting the LIN candidates and indicate that these associations mainly reflect upstream barrier and regulatory mechanisms rather than direct lipid-modifying enzymes.

\subsection{Lipid oxidation, neutral-lipid turnover, and isoprenoid precursor supply in Control}

Within the CTL condition, recurrent GWAS candidates included loci linked to lipid and isoprenoid metabolism. One representative signal, \texttt{SORBI\_3006G248300} belongs to a lipoxygenase clade involved in oxylipin biosynthesis. Its homolog in maize, \textit{ZmLOX6} oxygenates polyunsaturated fatty acids, generating hydroperoxide intermediates and downstream oxylipins. Its transcript levels are strongly regulated by hormone- and pathogen-related stimuli, being induced by jasmonic acid and repressed by ABA, salicylic acid, and ethylene in the reported system. This supports interpreting a LOX6-like association in sorghum as standing genetic variation in a lipid-oxidation module that affects the production of lipid-derived metabolites and thus lipid profiles under CTL conditions.

A second candidate in the same locus group, \texttt{SORBI\_3006G248100} (orthologous to \textit{AtOBL1}), is consistent with allelic variation in neutral-lipid turnover at lipid droplets. \textit{AtOBL1} localizes to lipid droplets and acts as a lipase on TG, DG, and MG, directly modulating acyl flux among storage and intermediate glycerolipid pools. In a lipid-focused GWAS, an \textit{OBL1}-like association most conservatively highlights a candidate regulator of TG/DG/MG interconversion and acyl remobilization, rather than a single deterministic shift in net storage versus mobilization.

Finally, \texttt{SORBI\_3006G248000} encodes a farnesyl-pyrophosphate synthase (FPS)-like trans-prenyltransferase that acts upstream of diverse isoprenoid end-products. Trans-prenyltransferases synthesize core prenyl diphosphate backbones (including FPP) via sequential condensations, supplying branch-point intermediates for major terpenoid pathways (e.g., sterols, carotenoids, and related metabolites). 

Together, the LOX6-like, OBL1-like, and FPS-like candidates define a coherent theme under CTL conditions. Lipid oxidation and oxylipin biology, neutral-lipid turnover at lipid droplets, and provision of upstream isoprenoid precursors, while recognizing that GWAS primarily prioritizes loci for later functional validation rather than proving direct causal enzymatic roles.

\subsection{Growth and developmental regulation candidates under Control lipid GWAS}

Several recurrent CTL-associated loci map to core pathways that control vegetative growth and reproduction. Although they do not encode canonical lipid enzymes, they provide a developmental framework for why specific lipid traits repeatedly colocalize with the same genomic regions under CTL conditions. First,  \textttt{OS07G0614400} (rice ortholog of \texttt{SORBI\_3002G373700}) encodes a \textit{DRO1}-family homolog (DRL1-like) involved in root gravitropism and root growth angle variation. It is specifically expressed in root-tip columella cells, is auxin regulated, and controls root growth angle and root system architecture \citep{Kitomi2020}.  Second, \texttt{OsCDPK25} (rice homolog of \texttt{SORBI\_3005G025400}) encodes a calcium-dependent protein kinase. Perturbing \textit{OsCPK25/26} expression in rice alters stamen number, indicating a key role in reproductive organ development \citep{Zhang2014}. Third, Cen8.t00833/Os08g0310100 (homolog of  \texttt{SORBI\_3003G141700}) encodes an ortholog of the mitochondrial large-subunit ribosomal protein \textit{MRPL15}. \textit{OsMRPL15} knockout causes male sterility with pollen defects linked to impaired mitochondrial function, consistent with an essential role for mitochondrial translation in pollen development \citep{Xie2023}. 

Together, these DRL1-, CDPK25-, and MRPL15-like loci define a CTL-specific theme of natural genetic variation in plant architecture, reproductive development, and organellar function.

\section*{Conclusion}

Population-scale lipidomics in SAP across contrasting field environments showed that LIN conditions trigger structured lipidome remodeling, with coordinated shifts among glycolipids/sulfolipids, phospholipids, and triglycerides. Orthogonal analyses identified four core low-input signatures: SQDG depletion, TG enrichment, PS redistribution within the phospholipid network, and lysophospholipid remodeling, supported by ratio-based contrasts, lipid ontology enrichment, covariance rewiring, and reaction-network connectivity. Integrating these patterns with GWAS linked lipid remodeling to candidate loci involving phosphate-starvation regulators, glycerol-backbone metabolism, and lipid biosynthesis, yielding genetically informed hypotheses for sorghum lipid plasticity under combined nutrient and cold stress. Predictive modeling further showed that TG-dominated lipid programs are reproducible markers of growth-related phenotypes, supporting the use of lipid modules as biomarkers in breeding and translational applications. SoLD thus provides a harmonized resource connecting sorghum lipid diversity with environment, genotype, and agronomic traits, and establishes a basis for validating prioritized loci and deploying lipid-informed markers to enhance resilience in low-input systems. Future work will use targeted approaches, including CRISPR-based perturbation and transgenic overexpression of candidate genes, to test lipid-remodeling mechanisms and their effects on performance under low-input conditions.

\section*{Materials and methods}

\subsection*{Plant Material and Growth Conditions}

In our study, we used the Sorghum Association Panel (SAP), consisting of 400 accessions designed to cover extensive genetic and phenotypic diversity. This collection includes both temperate-adapted breeding lines and tropical landraces. The panel represents five botanical races, bicolor, caudatum, durra, guinea, and kafir, capturing a diverse range of domestication and adaptation processes.

SAP was first genotyped using simple sequence repeat markers, followed by low-coverage genotyping by sequencing (GBS). For a more comprehensive variation set, \cite{Boatwright2022} et al. resequenced all entries using whole genome sequencing (WGS) with an average depth of 38× (ranging from 25–72×). The variant data from WGS revealed approximately 43.98 million polymorphisms, including roughly 38 million SNPs with 5 million small insertions/deletions. While GBS variants were predominantly located in genic regions, the WGS data were more evenly distributed across genic and intergenic regions. Genome-wide linkage disequilibrium is approximately 20 kb, although there were deviations specific to each chromosome. The consequent high-density variant map establishes the resequenced SAP as a valuable tool for examining diversity and conducting genome wide association studies (GWAS).

We evaluated SAP accessions across two different field settings during two growing seasons (2019 and 2022) at the Pee Dee Research and Education Center, Clemson University, Florence, South Carolina. The "control" condition, herein denoted as C, involved standard agronomic inputs with sufficient levels of nitrogen (N) and phosphorus (P) along with a typical planting schedule. In contrast, the "low input" condition, herein denoted as LI,  featured reduced N and P coupled with earlier planting to mimic a cold stress environment.

\subsection*{Lipidomics Analysis}

\paragraph{Sample Preparation and Extraction.} 
Samples were prepared following the standard extraction protocols explained in the paper by \cite{Barnes2022}.

\paragraph{Liquid Chromatography-Mass Spectrometry (LC-MS) Analysis.} Lipid extracts were subjected to high-resolution mass spectrometry employing both positive and negative ionization modes to achieve comprehensive lipid coverage. Chromatographic separation was executed utilizing either reversed-phase columns, such as C18, or hydrophilic interaction chromatography (HILIC) columns, contingent upon the polarity of the lipids. In the case of C18 methodologies, the gradient elution commenced at 1 minute and concluded at 8 minutes, succeeded by an isocratic elution phase from 8 to 9.5 minutes. Data preceding 1 minute and subsequent to 9.5 minutes were omitted to eliminate solvent front and late eluting artifacts. For HILIC methodologies, the gradient initiation occurred at 1 minute, terminating at 16.25 minutes, and was followed by an isocratic elution extending until 18.5 minutes; data before 1 minute and after 18.5 minutes were excluded in a similar manner.

\paragraph{Feature Detection and Data Processing.} Raw LC-MS data, in both positive and negative ion modes, were processed utilizing MZmine 2, an open-source software for the analysis of mass spectrometry data (\cite{Pluskal2010MZmine2}). The pipeline encompassed peak detection, chromatogram construction, deconvolution, and isotope filtering, producing a detailed feature table containing mass-to-charge ratio-retention time (mz-rt) pairs. Isotopic peaks were excluded to minimize redundancy, as a single metabolite can yield multiple co-eluting ions, such as adducts and in-source fragments. Therefore, mz-rt duplicates were handled with care, with potential de-adducting considered via MS-FLO when appropriate. We acknowledge that such degeneracy can lead to an inflated number of features compared to the actual number of metabolites present, which we considered during metabolite identification. 

\paragraph{Blank/extraction-control filtering, intensity thresholds, and sparsity pruning.}
To reduce background and carryover effects, an extraction control filter was implemented at the feature level. For each feature, the maximum intensity was determined across extraction controls (\(a\)) and biological samples (\(b\)). Features for which \(b < 10\times a\) were eliminated. To preclude the exclusion of borderline yet potentially biological signals, a feature was retained if at least one biological sample exceeded the extraction control maximum. Furthermore, a minimum average intensity threshold within the treatment groups of interest (\(\sim 10^{6}\) peak height) was imposed to ensure that downstream analyses would emphasize robust signals. At the sample level, any sample exhibiting \(\geq 70\%\) features as zero (or missing) was excluded prior to normalization and statistical analysis. This pruning of sparsity is essential to prevent unstable scaling and spurious differential signals caused by ultra-sparse profiles. 


\paragraph{MS/MS spectral library matching and cross-referencing of IDs.}
For each feature analyzed by MS/MS, the most intense fragmentation spectrum was queried against the GNPS database. Library matches resulted in putative identifications (levels 2/3), potentially including isomers or near-mass analogs. Features without direct matches were eliminated. To enable quantification with identifications, tables were linked using the feature \emph{row ID} from the MZmine peak list and the corresponding \#Scan\# key in the GNPS results, ensuring a one-to-one correspondence between intensities and candidate identifications. In cases where a feature yielded multiple GNPS hits, a single primary annotation was designated by retaining the highest MQScore (cosine similarity). Ties in the values were resolved based on a greater number of shared fragment ions and a smaller precursor mass error (ppm). All other sub-threshold or lower-ranked candidates were retained for verification but were excluded from subsequent statistical analyses.

\paragraph{Systematic Error Removal Using Random Forest (SERRF).}
Following the cleaning process, the data were then used for SERRF normalization (\cite{Fan2019}). We used the SERRF server (https://slfan2013.github.io/SERRF-online/\#) to obtain the normalized output. After applying SERRF, only biological samples were preserved. Any zeros were substituted with two-thirds of the minimum nonzero value for that feature to prevent potential infinite logarithmic transformations.

\paragraph{Spatial Correction.}
Finally, we conducted an additional quality control step specifically aimed at eliminating any spatial patterns across our experimental trials. This was achieved using the R package \texttt{SpATS} (\cite{RodriguezAlvarez2018}), which applies a two-dimensional P-spline ANOVA surface over the field coordinates. For every lipid feature, we characterized its intensity as
\begin{align}
  y_{ij} &= \mu + f_{\mathrm{row}}(i) + f_{\mathrm{col}}(j) + f_{\mathrm{row,col}}(i,j) + \varepsilon_{ij},
\end{align}

where 
\begin{itemize}
  \item \(f_{\mathrm{row}}\) and \(f_{\mathrm{col}}\) represent smooth functions that model systematic effects across rows and columns, respectively, 
  \item \(f_{\mathrm{row,col}}\) is a smooth interaction surface that handles more complex spatial gradients. 
\end{itemize}

We utilized the residuals, defined as the difference between observed intensity and the fitted spatial trend, as our analysis-ready spatially corrected intensity data which will be used for \%TIC, CLR, and log-ratio. We used SpATS-adjusted intensities (residuals + fitted mean) to preserve scale and avoid negative values. This methodology effectively corrects for positional artifacts, such as edge effects, that could interfere with subsequent analyses. Detailed smoothing parameters, including the number of knots, penalty orders, and comprehensive model specifications, can be found in our GitHub repository at \texttt{scripts/spats\_qc.R}.

\paragraph{Lipid Quantification}
The identified lipid species were organized into lipid classes and subclasses (see Supplementary Table\~1) based on Lipid Maps (\cite{Fahy2009LipidMapsClass}). In each sample, the total intensity for a class was obtained by summing the intensities of the species within that class. To manage variability in signal intensity due to different runs or injections, these class totals were normalized relative to the total ion current (TIC) of the sample. As a result, the relative abundances were presented as percentages of the TIC by adding up intensities across all lipid classes in the sample. For each class and its subclasses, we determined the TIC fraction for each sample and then averaged these percentages across samples for each condition (CTL, \$n=384\$; LIN, \$n=362\$). Lipids were categorized into glycerolipid, glycerophospholipid, sphingolipid, sterol, betaine lipid, fatty acid, ether lipid, and terpenoid. Refer to Supplementary Table 2 for the full list. 

\paragraph{Lipid Ratio Identification} To identify lipid class ratios most affected by the shift from CTL to LIN, we computed class totals and all pairwise class log-ratios, then analyzed these features using orthogonal partial least squares-discriminant analysis (OPLS-DA; \emph{ropls}, R v3.3.2). Ratios with VIP \$>1\$ were considered the most discriminative contributors to LIN--CTL separation. 

\paragraph{Lipid ratio calculation}
For each sample $i$ and lipid class $c$, species intensities were aggregated to class totals,
\[
C_{i,c}=\sum_{k \in c} I_{i,k}.
\]
Because log-ratios are scale-invariant, TIC normalization is not required for ratio computation.
To handle zero class totals, we used a sample-specific pseudo-count,
\[
\varepsilon_i=0.5 \cdot \min\{C_{i,c} : C_{i,c}>0\},
\]
and computed
\[
\ell_{i,c}=\log_{10}(C_{i,c}+\varepsilon_i).
\]
Pairwise class log-ratios were then
\[
r_{i,c_1/c_2}=\ell_{i,c_1}-\ell_{i,c_2}.
\]
Positive LIN--CTL effects indicate the enrichment of the numerator class in LIN.

\paragraph{Statistical Tests.}
For class-level comparisons, we computed log-ratios of class-wise abundances (e.g., $\log_{10}(\mathrm{PS}/\mathrm{SQDG})$), which are appropriate for compositional contrasts. For ratio analyses in Section 4, LIN and CTL were compared using a paired design restricted to the genotype intersection (genotypes observed in both conditions). For each of the 33 class ratios, we applied the paired Wilcoxon signed-rank test (two-sided) to the within-genotype LIN--CTL differences.

To assess the robustness of directionality against individual genotype influence, we quantified leave-one-out jackknife stability at the pair level, defined as the proportion of single-pair omissions for which the sign of the median paired effect was preserved. A stability value of 1.0 indicates that the effect direction is unchanged when any one genotype pair is removed.

For each ratio, we report:
\begin{itemize}
\item \texttt{n\_pairs} = number of matched LIN--CTL genotype pairs used.
\item \texttt{median\_CTL}, \texttt{median\_LIN} = condition medians of the log-ratio (reported for interpretability).
\item \texttt{effect\_log10} = paired median shift on the log$_{10}$ scale (LIN$-$CTL).
\item \texttt{effect\_fc} = fold-change, computed as $10^{\texttt{effect\_log10}}$; values $>1$ indicate higher ratios in LIN.
\item \texttt{p\_wilcox} = paired Wilcoxon signed-rank $p$-value.
\item \texttt{p\_adj\_BH} = Benjamini--Hochberg adjusted $p$-value (FDR, $m=33$).
\item \texttt{jackknife\_stability} = leave-one-out sign stability (1.0 = direction invariant across all single-pair omissions).
\end{itemize}

For individual species, we applied the centered log-ratio (CLR) transformation to address compositional constraints:
\[
\mathrm{CLR}(x_i)=\ln\!\left(\frac{x_i}{g(\mathbf{x})}\right),
\]
where $g(\mathbf{x})$ is the geometric mean across species within a sample. For each of the 172 common species, we report:
\begin{itemize}
\item \texttt{median\_CLR\_CTL}, \texttt{median\_CLR\_LIN} = group medians of CLR values.
\item \texttt{effect\_CLR} = median CLR difference; \texttt{effect\_fc} = $e^{\texttt{effect\_CLR}}$.
\item \texttt{p\_adj\_BH} = BH-adjusted $p$-value.
\item \texttt{jackknife\_stability} = leave-one-out sign stability.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Principal Component Analysis (PCA)}  

We performed three complementary PCA workflows in R using \emph{stats} package.  First, we ran PCA on the individual lipid species abundances.  Second, we summed the abundances by lipid class (SuppTable1) (e.g., TG, DG, PC, MGDG, SQDG) and repeated PCA to highlight broader shifts in lipids. Third, we computed key log ratio metrics using OPLS-DA (explained below) and carried out PCA on these as well. In all cases, data were mean–centered and scaled prior to analysis. For each PCA, we retained the first two principal components for visualization. 

\subsection*{Genome-wide Association Studies (GWAS)} 

GWAS analyzes were carried out for each lipid trait under each condition using the mixed linear model (MLM) featured in GEMMA (v2.3) (\cite{Zhou2012}). To address population structure and relatedness, a centered relatedness matrix (kinship) was computed from SNP genotype data. For each lipid trait, the MLM was applied using the kinship matrix to handle population stratification effects. Besides individual traits, GWAS was also applied to summed lipid classes and all possible ratios (refer to Supplementary Table 1). GWAS analysis was conducted on the first two PCs for each class. A significance threshold of \(-\log_{10}(p) \geq 7\) was employed in order to account for multiple comparisons. 

\subsection*{Gene Annotation}

SNPs were aligned with the Sorghum bicolor reference genome v3.1 (BTx623). For each marker, a 50 kb segment was designated, spanning 25 kb on either side, and all gene models within this area were retrieved. Functional annotations and homology were obtained from Phytozome (https://phytozome.jgi.doe.gov), SorghumBase (https://sorghumbase.com), and TAIR for corresponding Arabidopsis thaliana orthologs. Genes with known roles in N, P, cold tolerance, or lipid metabolism were specifically noted. We aggregated the frequency of each candidate gene within all lipid GWAS findings and marked those with the highest recurrence, showing -log10(p-values) of 7 or greater.

\subsubsection*{Lipid Metabolic Network Analysis (LINEX2)}

We used the Lipid Network Explorer (LINEX2; \url{https://exbio.wzw.tum.de/linex/}) to reconstruct lipid networks and identify condition enriched subgraphs for CTL vs. LIN, considering both the ratio and difference between them. Lipid names were standardized to align with species notation (class plus acyl composition). LINEX2 constructs a global species network based on curated reaction rules, including headgroup interconversions, (de)acylation/editing, elongation, desaturation, and overlays a quantitative association structure (Spearman correlations across samples) onto the reaction edges. For enrichment, LINEX2 calculates substrate–product change scores for each reaction regarding the CTL vs. LIN ratio and difference, employing a greedy local-search procedure to identify subgraphs that optimize the average substrate and product change. Since \textit{Sorghum bicolor} is not implemented as a default organism in LINEX2, we selected \textit{Oryza sativa} (OSA) as the reference species for network construction.


\subsubsection*{Lipid Ontology Enrichment and Hierarchical Classification (LION/web)}

We conducted a functional ontology for lipids using LION/web (Molenaar \emph{et al.}, 2019; \url{http://www.lipidontology.com}). Lipid nomenclature was standardized according to LIPID MAPS annotations. The default settings of LION/web were applied for enrichment statistics and multiple-testing correction. We retained terms for $q \leq 0.10$. A hierarchical classification analysis of individual lipids and functional ontologies was also performed using LION/web.
