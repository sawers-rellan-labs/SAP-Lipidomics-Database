\begin{abstract}
Sorghum (\textit{Sorghum bicolor}) is a climate-resilient crop whose acclimation to nutrient limitation and low temperature likely involves extensive lipidome reconfiguration. Lipids are key membrane components, carbon and energy stores, and mediators of stress signaling, yet population-scale lipidomics data for sorghum are limited. We present the Sorghum Lipid Database (SoLD), a curated lipidomics resource from the Sorghum Association Panel grown under two field regimes: (i) a nutrient-sufficient and usual date planting environment and (ii) a low-input treatment with reduced nitrogen and phosphorus, earlier planting, and no application of insecticides, herbicides, or pesticides. Using high-resolution LC–MS, we quantified 244 lipid species and detected broad, largely conserved compositional shifts across field trials. We identified four reproducible low-input associated lipid signatures with respect to control: (i) depletion of sulfoquinovosyldiacylglycerol, (ii) accumulation of triacylglycerol, (iii) phospholipid redistribution centered on phosphatidylserine, and (iv) coordinated lysophospholipid remodeling, reflected in altered lysophosphatidylcholine to lysophosphatidylethanolamine ratios. Analyses of lipid chemical space, lipid ontology enrichment, and correlations among lipid class ratios supported these compositional changes. GWAS of lipid species, class sums, and class ratios revealed recurrent, environment-specific loci. CTL-associated loci were enriched for genes in lipid and isoprenoid metabolism, developmental regulation, and cell-wall biosynthesis and modification. LIN-associated loci were enriched for genes in nutrient-stress signaling, cell-wall remodeling, defense, developmental control, and cold-related barrier formation and proteostasis. LINEX-based network highlighted diacylglycerol–triacylglycerol and phospholipid branches as likely contexts for lipid rebalancing under low-input condition, implicating reactions potentially catalyzed by LCAT-like, PNPLA-like, and DGAT1-associated enzymes. Thus, SoLD provides a framework connecting sorghum lipid diversity with environment and genetic variation. All the information regarding the database and the experiment can be freely accessible through a Shiny application: https://github.com/sawers-rellan-labs/SAP-Lipidomics-Database.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

%Add Shiny app section. 


Plants routinely face adverse environmental conditions that limit crop productivity, including nutrient deficiencies, especially nitrogen (N) and phosphorus (P), and low temperatures among the most common constraints (\cite{Schluter2012}; \cite{Silveira2018}; \cite{Emendack2021}). Nitrogen limitation engages nutrient-stress signaling that intersects with developmental reprogramming via Trp--IAA (auxin) pathways, reshaping root growth and resource allocation (\cite{Stepanova2008,Zhao2001,Fu2022,Liu2024,Wang2025}), while P scarcity poses an escalating challenge due to finite mineral P reserves and low fertilizer-use efficiency (\cite{Nacry2013}; \cite{LopezValdivia2025}; \cite{Medici2019}). Cold stress, including chilling (0--15\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}) and freezing ($<0\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$), disrupts membrane fluidity, slows metabolism, and can cause physiological damage that reduces yield \cite{Ding2019,Guo2025}. Together, these abiotic stresses trigger acclimation programs involving extensive membrane reorganization and remodeling of lipid metabolism (\cite{Bohn2007}; \cite{Siebers2015}; \cite{Xu2025}).

Lipids are central to plant stress physiology as key membrane components, energy stores, and precursors of signaling molecules. Stress-induced lipid remodeling stabilizes membranes, adjusts photosynthesis, and reshapes signaling, and is a conserved response to nutrient limitation and low temperature (\cite{Hou2016}; \cite{Rawat2021}; \cite{Li2020}; \cite{Bhattacharya2022}). Phosphate starvation responses, for example, are coordinated by conserved transcriptional networks centered on the master regulator, Phosphate Starvation Response1 (\textit{PHR1}), and related transcription factors (\cite{Rubio2001};\cite{Bustos2010}), which are involved in phosphate deficiency responses, including genes implicated in P recycling from membrane phospholipids and associated lipid remodeling. However, population-scale plant lipidomics resources remain limited compared with transcriptomic datasets, particularly those that are quantitatively robust, computationally interoperable, and linked to natural genetic variation and agronomic traits.

Two main challenges limit interpretability and reusability in large-scale lipidomics. First, LC--MS measurements are affected by systematic signal drift, batch effects, and spatial artifacts associated with field layout. Random forest-based QC normalization methods such as SERRF can reduce technical variance when QC samples are well designed \citep{Fan2019}, and spatial mixed models such as SpATS adjust field-position effects using two-dimensional penalized splines \citep{RodriguezAlvarez2018}. Second, lipid annotation practices and nomenclature vary widely across studies. Platforms such as GNPS support spectral library matching and collaborative curation \citep{Wang2016}, while ontology-based resources such as LION/web enable enrichment analyzes over lipid classes and physicochemical properties \citep{Molenaar2019}. Together, these approaches support lipidomics datasets that are analytically robust and more readily comparable across experiments.

Here, we present the Sorghum Lipid Database (SoLD), a curated resource that links annotated lipid species to natural genetic variation in the SAP. Using leaf samples collected at an early developmental stage [V6 stage???], we profiled lipid variation under two contrasting field regimes. The control environment provided sufficient nutrients and a standard planting schedule (CTL), whereas the low-input regime combined reduced N and P inputs with earlier planting and minimal chemical management, such as no insecticides, pesticides, or herbicides, to impose a realistic, multifactorial stress environment (LIN). CTL and LIN cohorts were sampled in different field years, so we interpret their contrasts conservatively as between-trial differences. After QC-based normalization and spatial adjustment, global lipid signal distributions were broadly comparable across cohorts rather than showing obvious run- or layout-driven artifacts.

Across the population, LIN in comparison to CTL, was associated with reproducible lipid remodeling characterized by SQDG depletion, TG enrichment, PS-centered redistribution within the phospholipid network, and coordinated lysophospholipid changes reflected in an altered LPC/LPE balance. These patterns indicate coordinated redistribution across interconnected lipid pools rather than isolated class-specific shifts. We integrated lipid profiles with genome-wide association analyzes across individual lipid species and class-sum/class-ratio traits to prioritize recurrent candidate loci in both environments. CTL-associated candidates were enriched for core metabolic and chloroplast-linked functions, including lipid/isoprenoid metabolism, transport, photosynthesis, and carbohydrate metabolism. LIN-associated candidates shifted toward nutrient-status signaling and remobilization, including Trp--IAA-related loci, phosphate-starvation regulators, and recycling-related candidates, together with stress-adaptive processes such as barrier formation and cold/defense-linked regulation. Finally, we provide an interactive SoLD Shiny application that enables exploration of all datasets and results, available at \url{https://github.com/sawers-rellan-labs/SAP-Lipidomics-Database}.

\section{Results}

\subsection*{Quality control and normalization of LC-MS features shows comparable results between conditions}

We profiled early-stage lipids from SAP grown in two contrasting field conditions defined earlier. The workflow (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1_lipidomics_landscape}A) comprised tissue collection and extraction, high-resolution LC-MS lipidomic profiling, GNPS-assisted lipid annotation, post-acquisition data correction with SERRF and SpATS (\cite{Fan2019}; \cite{RodriguezAlvarez2018}), and data dissemination via the SoLD Shiny app (\url{https://github.com/sawers-rellan-labs/SAP-Lipidomics-Database)}.

Run-order quality control indicated stable analytical performance in both conditions (Supplementary Figure~\ref{fig:suppfig_s1_qc} A,B). Throughout the sequence, sample total ion current (TIC) matched expectations from system checks, internal standards, and blanks. TIC variability among QC samples was low (CTL QC TIC CV = 7.5\%; LIN QC TIC CV = 6.0\%; Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s1_qc} A,B). SERRF normalization improved technical precision, reducing median QC-RSD from 3.6\% to 2.0\% in CTL and from 1.6\% to 0.3\% in LIN (Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s1_qc} C,D). The proportion of features with QC-RSD $<30\%$ increased from 75.7\% to 94.1\% in CTL and from 77.3\% to 89.3\% in LIN (Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s1_qc} C,D). PCA of normalized data showed tighter sample clustering than unprocessed data (Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s1_qc} E,F). Field-grown samples were additionally corrected for field-position effects using spatial modeling (SpATS) in R, which largely removed broad row and column patterns.(Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s1_qc} G,H).

At the global scale, normalization and spatial correction preserved overall lipid signal distributions across conditions, enabling conservative cross-condition comparisons without evidence of systematic intensity inflation or attenuation in either cohort. The corrected trait matrix was then used for all downstream analyzes.

\subsection*{Lipid variation across genotypes}

To test whether lipid differences between CTL and LIN reflected systematic, population-wide reprogramming rather than a few outlier genotypes, we performed two-sample Wilcoxon rank-sum tests with leave-one-out jackknife stability analysis. For lipid class (explained in \texttt{Methods}) comparisons, we analyzed pairwise log-ratios from class-level summaries. All 33 class log-ratios differed significantly between conditions (BH-adjusted $p<0.05$, \cite{Benjamini1995}) with maximal jackknife stability (1.0; Supplementary Table~S1). At the species level, we applied a centered log-ratio (CLR) transformation to account for compositionality (\cite{Aitchison1982}). Of the 153 lipid species shared between conditions (Supplementary Table~S6A), 151 (98.7\%) differed significantly (Supplementary Table~S2), and all 153 had perfect jackknife stability (1.0; Supplementary Tables~S2--S3), indicating that the effect direction was unchanged when any single genotype was excluded. Together, these results indicate that lipidome differences between LIN and CTL are broadly distributed across SAP genotypes rather than driven by a small number of outlier accessions.

Given this robustness, we next identified lipids with the greatest genetic variance. MGDG(18:3/18:3) showed the highest variance in both environments (Supplementary Fig.~\ref{fig:suppfig_s2_topvar}A,B; Supplementary Table~S4). Seven of the ten most variable lipids were shared between CTL and LIN (Supplementary Fig.~\ref{fig:suppfig_s2_topvar}C), indicating a conserved subset of lipid traits with strong genotype sensitivity across environments. Within this high-variance subset, PC species were the most common class in both conditions (CTL: 7/10; LIN: 6/10; Supplementary ~\ref{fig:suppfig_s2_topvar}D; Supplementary Table~S4).

The recurrence of the same high-variance lipid traits across conditions provides an internal consistency check, indicating that genotype-dependent lipid variability is conserved in both cohorts rather than driven mainly by condition-specific or instrument-related technical artifacts.

\subsection{Lipidome overview for CTL and LIN SAP}

Across both environments, we quantified 244 lipid species, including 153 shared, 49 CTL-specific, and 42 LIN-specific species (Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s4_counts} A; Supplementary Table~S6 A,B,C). TG and PC were the most diverse classes in each environment (TG: 71 in CTL, 69 in LIN; PC: 39 in CTL, 38 in LIN; Supplementary Table~S6 A,B,C), and most species belonged to the glycerolipid and glycerophospholipid (Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s4_counts} B,D). PCA showed that lipid class strongly structured the dataset, with TGs separating from membrane lipids and galactolipids forming distinct clusters in both conditions (Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s5_pca} A,B).

At the class level (\%TIC), the lipidome was dominated by galactolipids and phospholipids (Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s3_comp} A). Under CTL, MGDG contributed 34.5\% of mean TIC and 32.1\% under LIN, while PC remained relatively stable (25.6\% in CTL vs. 24.8\% in LIN). TG increased from 2.7\% (CTL) to 5.4\% (LIN), and SQDG decreased from 2.9\% (CTL) to 1.4\% (LIN) (Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s3_comp} A). Compositional contrast analyzes confirmed these trends and revealed coordinated headgroup rebalancing within the phospholipid network. CLR contrasts identified PS as one of the strongest positively shifted classes (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1_lipidomics_landscape}B; Supplementary Table~S5 A), and TG-referenced ALR contrasts showed PS increasing relative to multiple phospholipid pools (e.g., PC, PE, PG) despite modest absolute \%TIC shifts (Supplementary Table~S5 B, Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s3_comp}B). Lysophospholipids also showed reproducible remodeling, with LPC/LPE dynamics under LIN consistent with stress-responsive phospholipid turnover ( \cite{Hou2016}; \cite{Rawat2021}) rather than uniform scaling of total lyso-lipid abundance (Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s3_comp}B; Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1_lipidomics_landscape}). These results define four LIN-associated lipid signatures compared to CTL: (i) SQDG depletion, (ii) TG enrichment, (iii) PS enrichment relative to other phospholipid headgroups, and (iv) lysophospholipid remodeling (LPC/LPE). SQDG depletion is notable because phosphate starvation alone usually triggers membrane remodeling with increased galactolipids, including SQDG, and reduced phospholipids \citep{Pfaff2020}. Thus, reduced SQDG in LIN supports the view that the remodeling reflects an integrated response to the combined field regime rather than a canonical phosphate-starvation signature. Cold-stress lipidomics likewise indicate that SQDG often remains stable while other lipid classes shift compositionally \citep{Yan2025}. Second, TG enrichment is consistent with stress-driven diversion of DG into storage lipids, matching evidence that freezing tolerance in \emph{Arabidopsis} relies on DGAT1-dependent TG accumulation and partitioning between TG and DG/PA pools \citep{Tan2018}. Stress-responsive lipidomes similarly show elevated TG with a reduced DG:TG ratio \citep{Yan2025,Pfaff2020}. Third, the relative increase in PS versus other phospholipid headgroups agrees with cold-stress phospholipid remodeling, where PS elevations have been reported in multiple datasets \citep{Yan2025,Zhang2020}. Fourth, the LPC/LPE signature is consistent with enhanced phospholipid turnover under stress, as both LPC and LPE increase under cold treatments in independent studies. \citep{Yan2025,Zhang2020}.

LION enrichment analysis confirmed these shifts. LIN increased ontology terms linked to neutral-lipid accumulation and storage, including \textit{triacylglycerols [GL0301]}, \textit{glycerolipids [GL]}, \textit{diacylglycerols [GL0201]}, \textit{lipid storage}, and \textit{lipid droplet} (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1_lipidomics_landscape}D) (\cite{Molenaar2019}). In contrast, CTL was enriched for membrane phospholipid categories and descriptors, including \textit{glycerophospholipids [GP]}, \textit{glycerophosphocholines [GP01]/diacylglycerophosphocholines [GP0101]}, \textit{glycerophosphoethanolamines [GP02]/diacylglycerophosphoethanolamines [GP0201]}, and \textit{membrane component} (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1_lipidomics_landscape}D). Thus, LION supports that LIN is associated with a coordinated shift toward TG and concomitant phospholipid/membrane remodeling detected by both abundance contrasts and acyl-chain chemical-space changes.

Furthermore, to characterize structural remodeling beyond total class abundance, we summarized each lipid class in a reduced chemical space defined by its abundance-weighted mean total carbon number and double-bond count across constituent molecular species (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1_lipidomics_landscape}C). In this space, a shift in a class centroid reflects changes in the within-class species mixture (e.g., relatively more longer- vs. shorter-chain species), rather than changes in absolute signal alone. TG occupied the high-carbon region and shifted toward higher weighted mean total carbon under LIN, with only modest changes in mean unsaturation (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1_lipidomics_landscape}C), indicating preferential enrichment of longer-chain TG species under LIN. PE showed the largest increase in unsaturation among membrane-associated classes (from ~2.68 to 3.38 weighted mean double bonds, with minimal carbon change), consistent with relatively more unsaturated PE species under LIN. We interpret these centroid shifts as descriptive evidence of coordinated acyl-chain remodeling (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1_lipidomics_landscape}B), without inferring specific enzymatic fluxes. Most other classes maintained distinct carbon/unsaturation signatures with limited centroid movement, suggesting tighter compositional constraints on major membrane-associated lipid pools.

Together, the abundance-, composition-, ontology-, and chemical-space–based analyzes show a coordinated lipidome redistribution in response to LIN, marked by SQDG depletion, TG enrichment, PS headgroup rebalancing, and extensive lysophospholipid remodeling.

\subsection*{Recurrent GWAS signals across CTL lipid traits emphasize basal metabolism, growth regulation, and cell-wall-associated processes}

We performed GWAS separately for each lipid trait individually and for their class sums and class ratios measured under CTL. We then assessed whether recurrent gene--lipid associations converged on common biological processes. For the individual-lipid GWAS, we kept gene-level associations with $p \le 1 \times 10^{-7}$ for each phenotype and counted how often each gene was associated across traits. This yielded 3,478 candidate genes. Applying a recurrence threshold of association with $\geq 13$ phenotypes, approximately the top 0.5\% of genes reduced this to 17 highly recurrent candidates (Table \ref{tab:T1_control_recurrent_combined}; SuppTable S7).

Functional and GO annotation of these recurrent candidates highlighted roles in lipid metabolism and transport, photosynthesis and chloroplast function, and central carbohydrate metabolism (Table \ref{tab:T1_control_recurrent_combined}). The most strongly recurrent genes were mainly lipid-related, including a lipoxygenase, a lipase-like hydrolase, and an acyltransferase-like gene involved in isoprenoid and farnesyl diphosphate biosynthesis, pointing to repeated associations with lipid turnover and lipid-derived signaling. Other recurrent genes encoded putative peptide transporters, a photosystem II chlorophyll-binding protein, Rubisco activase, and enzymes of glucose- and fructose-phosphate metabolism, such as glucose-6-phosphate 1-epimerase and a phosphofructokinase-related protein \citep{GeneOntologyConsortium2026}. These recurrent GWAS signals from individual lipid traits in the CTL environment are closely linked to lipid metabolism, chloroplast-associated functions, and membrane transport.

We extended the recurrence analysis to class sum and class ratio phenotypes under CTL conditions. Using the same gene-level threshold, the combined CTL sum/ratio analysis identified 4,385 putative loci. Restricting to genes detected in \(\geq 72\) phenotypes (the upper ~0.5\% of the recurrence distribution) reduced this to 20 highly recurrent candidates, again yielding a compact set of loci with repeated effects across many traits (Table \ref{tab:T1_control_recurrent_combined}; SuppTable S8).

Several recurrent CTL-associated loci also mapped to genes with established roles in plant growth and development. These included a \textit{DRO1}/DRL1-like locus (\texttt{SORBI\_3002G373700}), a calcium-dependent protein kinase locus related to \textit{OsCPK25/26} (\texttt{SORBI\_3005G025400}), and an \textit{MRPL15}-like mitochondrial ribosomal protein locus (\texttt{SORBI\_3003G141700}) (Table~\ref{tab:T1_control_recurrent_combined}). In rice, \textit{DRO1}-family genes regulate root growth angle and root system architecture \citep{Kitomi2020}. Altered expression of \textit{OsCPK25/26} affects reproductive organ development \citep{Zhang2014OsCPK25_26}, and \textit{OsMRPL15} is required for normal pollen development and mitochondrial function \citep{Xie2023OsMRPL15}. Together, these recurrent loci indicate that CTL lipid-associated signals are connected not only to core lipid metabolism but also to biological processes relating to plant architecture, reproductive development, and organellar function.

\subsection*{Recurrent GWAS signals across low-input lipid traits converge on shared stress- and growth-related processes}

We tested the genome wide associated signals in the LIN conditions across both individual lipid traits and higher-order class-sum and class-ratio traits as well. We applied the same filters based on $p$-value and repeated detection across phenotypes. For the individual-lipid GWAS, we retained gene–trait associations with $p \le 1\times10^{-7}$ for each phenotype and counted how often each gene recurred across traits. Under LIN conditions, 141 lipid phenotypes had at least one significant gene association, yielding 10,884 unique candidate genes. Restricting to genes detected in $\geq 7$ phenotypes reduced this to 48 recurrent candidates (approximately the top 0.5\% of the recurrence distribution) (\ref{tab:T2_lowinput_recurrent_combined}; SuppTable S9) . Of these, 30 genes had functional annotations in our functional and GO-based summary, while the rest were uncharacterized (\cite{GeneOntologyConsortium2026}). The most recurrent loci were associated with up to 11 phenotypes, indicating that only a small subset of genes showed repeatedly detectable effects across the LIN lipidome (Table \ref{tab:T2_lowinput_recurrent_combined}).

Although this recurrent gene set was not dominated by lipid-metabolic enzymes according to our filtering scheme, its annotations converged on biological processes likely engaged under LIN management. These included nutrient-status signaling and remobilization (tryptophan synthase genes, a phosphate-starvation regulator), cell-wall remodeling and growth modulation (expansin genes, a callose synthase), and defense-associated functions (thaumatin-like and defensin-like proteins, immunity-related kinases) (Table \ref{tab:T2_lowinput_recurrent_combined}; SuppTable S9) (\cite{GeneOntologyConsortium2026}). Together, these patterns indicate that LIN conditions elicit a broader physiological response than nutrient limitation alone, integrating structural remodeling and increased defense. Thus, the recurrent GWAS signals from individual lipid traits appear to capture coordinated stress adaptation and whole-plant acclimation, rather than changes limited to lipid biochemistry.

Using the same gene-level threshold ($p \le 1\times10^{-7}$), the combined analysis of class sums and ratios identified 6,516 candidate genes. Restricting attention to genes detected in $\geq 50$ phenotypes yielded 31 highly recurrent candidates (about the top 0.5\%) (Table \ref{tab:T2_lowinput_recurrent_combined}; SuppTable S10). As with individual lipid species, only a few loci showed repeated effects across the LIN lipidome, however, these recurrent loci were enriched for higher-order regulation of lipid-class balance rather than specific molecular lipids.

Functional GO annotation and orthology-based curation indicated that these recurrent SR candidates were enriched for processes related to growth, stress acclimation, and regulatory control rather than canonical lipid metabolism. Growth- and development-related candidates included a pentatricopeptide repeat protein orthologous to the slow-growth regulator \textit{SLO1}, whose Arabidopsis homolog is required for mitochondrial RNA editing and normal growth and development \citep{Sung2010}. A second subset was linked to abiotic stress acclimation and surface-barrier biosynthesis, including a \textit{CER1}/\textit{WDA1}-like wax locus associated with wax and cuticle formation \citep{Jung2006WDA1} and a cullin-like regulatory candidate consistent with stress-responsive ubiquitin-mediated signaling \citep{WangXie2024Cullin}. Overall, LIN lipid class sums and ratios appear to report an integrated physiological state shaped by developmental plasticity, stress adaptation, and regulatory networks rather than by direct lipid-metabolic enzymes alone.

\subsection*{LINEX-based reaction mapping, reaction-balance scoring, and GWAS support}

To place lipid associations in biochemical context, we queried LINEX2 (\cite{Morgat2016Rhea}) for RHEA-annotated reaction links between lipid classes and candidate enzymes. LINEX2 does not provide a sorghum reference dataset, so we used the rice reference instead. The curated subnetwork (Fig.~\ref{fig:Fig4_linex_rewiring_abc}A; Supplementary Table~S11) captured four candidate branches connecting DG/MG, TG, and lysophospholipid pools: (i) PC+DG$\leftrightarrow$LPC+TG (LCAT-like transfer, hypothetical), (ii) TG$\leftrightarrow$DG (PNPLA1-labeled lipase branch), (iii) PE+DG$\leftrightarrow$TG+LPE (LRO1/PDAT-like transfer), and (iv) DG+MG$\leftrightarrow$TG (PNPLA3-labeled branch). These links are annotation-based hypotheses and do not directly establish \emph{in vivo} flux direction. Since CTL and LIN were sampled in different field years/environments, all LIN--CTL contrasts in this section are interpreted as genotype-matched between-trial compositional differences rather than isolated causal effects of LIN management.

To summarize these candidate branches at the class level, we translated each LINEX reaction into a reaction-balance score for each sample. For sample \(i\), let \(\ell_{i,c}=\log_{10}(C_{i,c}+\varepsilon_i)\) denote the log-abundance of class \(c\). For each reaction, we computed
\[
S_{i,\mathrm{rxn}}=\mathrm{mean}(\ell_{i,\mathrm{products}})-\mathrm{mean}(\ell_{i,\mathrm{substrates}}),
\]
so larger \(S_{i,\mathrm{rxn}}\) indicates a more product-weighted balance within that sample, whereas smaller values indicate a more substrate-weighted balance. We used:
\begin{itemize}
\item LCAT*: \(S_i=\mathrm{mean}(\ell_{i,\mathrm{LPC}},\ell_{i,\mathrm{TG}})-\mathrm{mean}(\ell_{i,\mathrm{PC}},\ell_{i,\mathrm{DG}})\);
\item PNPLA1: \(S_i=\ell_{i,\mathrm{DG}}-\ell_{i,\mathrm{TG}}\);
\item LRO1: \(S_i=\mathrm{mean}(\ell_{i,\mathrm{TG}},\ell_{i,\mathrm{LPE}})-\mathrm{mean}(\ell_{i,\mathrm{PE}},\ell_{i,\mathrm{DG}})\);
\item PNPLA3: \(S_i=\ell_{i,\mathrm{TG}}-\mathrm{mean}(\ell_{i,\mathrm{DG}},\ell_{i,\mathrm{MG}})\).
\end{itemize}
These scores are intended as branch-aligned summaries of lipid-pool balance and not as direct measures of enzymatic activity or metabolic flux.

We compared reaction-balance scores between LIN and CTL using paired, genotype-matched tests (\(n=355\) pairs) and summarized effects as \(\mathrm{median}(S_{\mathrm{LIN}})-\mathrm{median}(S_{\mathrm{CTL}})\). Positive LIN--CTL effects indicate shifts toward more product-weighted balances, whereas negative effects indicate shifts toward more substrate-weighted balances. Effects were positive for PNPLA3 (+0.327), LCAT* (+0.238), and LRO1 (+0.232), and negative for PNPLA1 (-0.268), with BH-adjusted \(p<10^{-56}\) (Fig.~\ref{fig:Fig4_linex_rewiring_abc}B; Supplementary Table~S12). Thus, candidate branches associated with TG-side product states, together with lysophospholipid coproducts in phospholipid-linked transfers, showed directionally concordant balance shifts, whereas the TG$\rightarrow$DG hydrolytic branch showed the opposite pattern.

\subsection{An interactive SoLD Shiny application enables exploration of lipid and GWAS results}

The SoLD webserver provides an integrated environment to explore lipid phenotypes and their genetic associations in the SAP across multiple field environments. Users can examine lipid abundance patterns, compare experimental conditions, and investigate the corresponding GWAS results and recurrence-based gene summaries. A key feature is hierarchical filtering (class $\rightarrow$ subclass $\rightarrow$ individual lipid), with support for multiple phenotype formulations, including individual lipid traits, class-level sums (\cite{Fahy2009LipidMapsClass}), and class-derived ratios. The application also supports different data representations (e.g., Raw, \%TIC, CLR), allowing users to assess whether patterns reflect changes in absolute abundance or redistribution within the compositional lipidome.

Figure~\ref{fig:Fig5_shiny_app} A summarizes the main modules in the SoLD Shiny application. The web server supports the analysis of lipid phenotypes at multiple resolutions, including including individual species, class-level sums, and inter-class ratios. with hierarchical filtering by class, subclass, and compound. Visualization tools include a Data Preview module, boxplots for exploratory distributional assessment, a Volcano Plot module for differential abundance, and Correlation and Network modules for association structures. Multivariate analyses use Heatmap, PCA, and t-SNE/UMAP modules to reveal higher-order patterns and sample structure. Genetic information is integrated via GWAS and Gene Hits modules. The GWAS module displays trait-specific associations, while Gene Hits aggregates recurrent candidate genes under user-defined settings, including dataset, trait type (individual species, class sums, or ratios), lipid class/subclass filters, and significance thresholds (e.g., $-\log_{10}(p)\ge 5, 6,$ or $7$). The Genotype module aids experimental design by identifying accessions within specified lipid value ranges, enabling targeted selection of lines with extreme phenotypes. The SoLD Shiny application makes it easier to visualize and examine specific lipid species or lipid classes by bringing filtering, plotting, and summary outputs into a single interface. For example, in the Boxplots module (Fig.~\ref{fig:Fig5_shiny_app}B), setting Class = Terpenoid and Subclass = Triterpenoid reveals a strong shift between conditions for summed triterpenoids, with a significant between condition contrast (Wilcoxon test, $p < 0.001$). This phenotype can then be passed to the Gene Hits module (Fig.~\ref{fig:Fig5_shiny_app}C) for LIN, which summarizes candidate loci after applying harmonized filters (dataset, trait source, significance threshold, lipid class/subclass). Under this view, \texttt{SORBI\_3008G142400} (Sobic.008G142400) is a candidate gene annotated as a terpene cyclase/mutase family member, consistent with triterpenoid biosynthesis. Prior biochemical work in sorghum shows that \texttt{Sobic.008G142400.1} encodes an oxidosqualene cyclase with simiarenol-synthase activity that produces most adult-leaf wax triterpenoids, which are enriched in the intracuticular wax layer underlying the cuticular water barrier and associated with reduced water loss at high temperatures \citep{Busta2021}. Thus, we can readily investigate highly specific individual lipids or entire lipid classes using the SoLD shiny application.

\section{Discussion}

In this study, we present the SoLD, a population-scale lipidomics resource for the SAP, and show that contrasting field environments drive pronounced, genome-wide shifts in lipid composition. Lipidomic differences between CTL and LIN conditions were not due to a few outlier genotypes but were widespread and converged on four main LIN-associated signatures: (i) depletion of SQDG, enrichment of TG, a PS-centered redistribution of phospholipids, and coordinated lysophospholipid remodeling reflected in an altered lysophosphatidylcholine/lysophosphatidylethanolamine balance. Multiple analytical layers, which include lipid class–level contrasts, lipid ontology enrichment, and dimensionality reduction of chemical space, consistently supported a model of coordinated redistribution among interconnected lipid pools rather than isolated changes in a few classes. Integration with GWAS indicated that sorghum lipid traits reflect broad aspects of plant physiology beyond core lipid metabolism. CTL-associated loci were enriched for genes involved in basal metabolism, chloroplast function, development, and cell-wall biology, whereas LIN-associated loci were linked to nutrient-status signaling, structural remodeling, defense responses, and cold acclimation. LINEX-guided network analyses placed these compositional shifts within a candidate biochemical network, emphasizing reorganization around DG–TG interconversion and phospholipid-associated branches. Since the CTL and LIN cohorts were sampled in different field years, the differences are interpreted conservatively as genotype-matched between-trial patterns rather than direct causal effects of the treatment alone.

\subsection{Cell-wall remodeling candidates under CTL and LIN link pectin turnover, wall sensing, wall biosynthesis, and wall plasticity}

Both CTL and LIN conditions yielded recurrent candidate loci related to cell-wall biology, indicating a shared wall-associated component of the genetic architecture across environments. However, they differed in the specific wall processes. CTL-associated loci were repeatedly linked to growth-coupled wall organization and maintenance, such as \texttt{SORBI\_3003G141800}, \texttt{SORBI\_3005G049600}, and \texttt{SORBI\_3002G373800} (\ref{tab:T1_control_recurrent_combined}). In contrast, LIN-associated candidates emphasized dynamic wall adjustment, including \texttt{SORBI\_3004G107800} and three expansin loci (\texttt{SORBI\_3007G018000}, \texttt{SORBI\_3001G238100}, \texttt{SORBI\_3001G238000}) \citep{Gladman2022SorghumBase} (Table \ref{tab:T2_lowinput_recurrent_combined}).

\texttt{SORBI\_3003G141800} has a homolog in rice, where \textit{PHOTO-SENSITIVE LEAF ROLLING 1} (\textit{PSL1}/\textit{OsPG1}) encodes a cell wall--localized polygalacturonase that alters wall composition and architecture, and \textit{psl1} loss-of-function increases drought tolerance with reduced water loss under drought and osmotic stress \citep{Zhang2021PSL1}. This provides a functional precedent for linking the \texttt{SORBI\_3003G141800} association to pectin remodeling and drought-related water-status traits. A second CTL-associated candidate, \texttt{SORBI\_3005G049600}, is supported by evidence from its homolog in rice showing that \textit{OsWAK11} encodes a pectin-sensing receptor-like kinase that connects pectin methylesterification status to brassinosteroid signaling through \textit{OsBRI1}, thereby tuning cell elongation \citep{Yue2022OsWAK11}. In addition, an \emph{Arabidopsis} homolog of \texttt{SORBI\_3002G373800} encodes for the translation elongation factor \textit{eEF-1B$\beta$1}, and loss-of-function reduces crystalline cellulose and lignin and causes dwarfism, whereas overexpression can rescue cell expansion defects in a cellulose-deficient background \citep{Hossain2012EF1Bb1}. Together, these CTL-associated loci are consistent with pectin turnover, wall sensing coupled to growth signaling, and wall biosynthetic capacity.

The LIN-associated cell-wall candidates point to a different axis of wall regulation, highlighted by \texttt{SORBI\_3004G107800}, annotated as a \textit{CALS8}/GSL-type callose synthase \emph{Arabidopsis} homolog, together with three expansin-family loci (\texttt{SORBI\_3007G018000}, \texttt{SORBI\_3001G238100}, and \texttt{SORBI\_3001G238000}) \citep{Gladman2022SorghumBase} (Table \ref{tab:T1_control_recurrent_combined}). Callose, a $\beta$-1,3-glucan polymer, is dynamically deposited at plasmodesmata and other cell-wall domains in response to biotic and abiotic cues, where it alters intercellular permeability and wall-associated stress responses \citep{CuiLee2016}. In Arabidopsis, \textit{CalS8} contributes to basal plasmodesmal permeability and is specifically required for ROS-dependent plasmodesmal regulation, whereas \textit{CalS1} functions in SA-dependent responses \citep{CuiLee2016}. These observations may support the allelic variation at a \textit{CALS8}-like locus may mark differences in stress-associated wall reinforcement. The expansin candidates provide a complementary route to altered wall mechanics. Expansins are conserved cell-wall proteins that promote wall loosening and irreversible extension under tensile stress without evidence of hydrolytic activity \citep{McQueenMason1992,Cosgrove2024}. Current models propose that expansins disrupt noncovalent interactions among aligned wall polysaccharides, particularly within cellulose-rich wall networks, thereby facilitating controlled wall yielding during growth and environmental response \citep{Cosgrove2024}. Across species, altered expansin expression has been linked to changes in organ growth, cell length, wall stiffness, and whole-plant architecture, and transgenic studies in rice show that increased expansin abundance can enhance cell extension while reducing wall stiffness \citep{Ma2013,Cosgrove2024}.

Together, these patterns indicate that the CTL and LIN environments recover distinct aspects of cell-wall-related regulation rather than the same wall program. The CTL GWAS highlights pectin turnover, wall-integrity sensing linked to growth signaling, and wall biosynthetic capacity, whereas the LIN GWAS highlights callose-based wall reinforcement and expansin-driven wall mechanics. Thus, these GWAS nominate genes as strong candidates for roles in cell wall organization and growth-related physiology in both conditions. Causal variants and downstream effects in sorghum still require functional validation.


\subsection{Growth and developmental regulation candidates under CTL and LIN indicate shared developmental control but distinct underlying processes}

CTL and LIN both yielded recurrent candidate loci linked to growth and development. However, the specific developmental pathways differed between conditions. Under CTL, recurrent loci were more enriched for vegetative growth, reproductive development, and organellar support, as illustrated by \texttt{SORBI\_3002G373700}, \texttt{SORBI\_3005G025400}, and \texttt{SORBI\_3003G141700} (\ref{tab:T1_control_recurrent_combined}). Under LIN, genes were more strongly associated with mitochondrial function, reproductive regulatory networks, and control of plant architecture, exemplified by \texttt{SORBI\_3001G256400}, \texttt{SORBI\_3002G153100}, and \texttt{SORBI\_3001G101700} (\ref{tab:T2_lowinput_recurrent_combined}).

Several recurrent CTL-associated loci provide a developmental framework for why specific lipid traits repeatedly colocalize with the same genomic regions. First, \texttt{OS07G0614400} (rice ortholog of \texttt{SORBI\_3002G373700}) encodes a \textit{DRO1}-family homolog (DRL1-like) involved in root gravitropism and root growth angle variation. It is specifically expressed in root-tip columella cells, is auxin regulated, and controls root growth angle and root system architecture \citep{Kitomi2020}. Second, \texttt{OsCDPK25} (rice homolog of \texttt{SORBI\_3005G025400}) encodes a calcium-dependent protein kinase. Perturbing \textit{OsCPK25/26} expression in rice alters stamen number, indicating a key role in reproductive organ development \citep{Zhang2014}. Third, Cen8.t00833/Os08g0310100 (homolog of \texttt{SORBI\_3003G141700}) encodes an ortholog of the mitochondrial large-subunit ribosomal protein \textit{MRPL15}. \textit{OsMRPL15} knockout causes male sterility with pollen defects linked to impaired mitochondrial function, consistent with an essential role for mitochondrial translation in pollen development \citep{Xie2023}. Together, these DRL1-, CDPK25-, and MRPL15-like loci define a CTL-specific theme of natural genetic variation in plant architecture, reproductive development, and organellar function.

The LIN candidate set also includes genes with established roles in organellar function and developmental regulation. \texttt{SORBI\_3001G256400} is most similar to \textit{SLO1} (\textit{SLOW GROWTH1}) of \textit{Arabidopsis}, which encodes a mitochondrion-localized pentatricopeptide repeat (PPR) protein required for RNA editing of mitochondrial \textit{nad4} and \textit{nad9} transcripts \citep{Sung2010}. Loss of \textit{SLO1} causes slow growth, delayed development, and broad changes in mitochondrial transcript accumulation, consistent with impaired organellar function \citep{Sung2010}. Two additional candidates point to developmental regulatory pathways. \texttt{SORBI\_3002G153100} is functionally similar to \textit{TGA10}, a bZIP transcription factor in \textit{Arabidopsis}. \textit{TGA9} and \textit{TGA10} function redundantly in anther development, are coexpressed with ROXY glutaredoxins during early anther ontogeny, and are required for normal tapetal function, pollen maturation, and anther dehiscence \citep{Murmu2010}. \texttt{SORBI\_3001G101700} is similar to maize \textit{GIF1} (\textit{GRF-interacting factor1}), a transcriptional coactivator that regulates shoot architecture and meristem determinacy. In maize, \textit{gif1} mutants exhibit narrow leaves, short internodes, fasciated inflorescence meristems, and loss of determinacy in axillary meristems, indicating that \textit{GIF1} coordinates growth across multiple meristematic contexts \citep{Zhang2018GIF1}.

Together, orthology-based evidence from CTL- and LIN-associated genes suggests that growth- and development-related signals are distributed across environments. CTL-associated loci were more often linked to functions involving root system architecture, reproductive organ development, and mitochondrial contributions to fertility and biomass. In contrast, LIN-associated loci were more often linked to mitochondrial function, reproductive regulatory networks, and the control of shoot architecture under stress. However, since they are based largely on functional information from homologs in other species, functional validation is needed in sorghum.


\subsection{Lipid oxidation, neutral-lipid turnover, and isoprenoid precursor supply in CTL}

Within the CTL condition, recurrent GWAS candidates included loci linked to lipid oxidation, neutral-lipid turnover, and isoprenoid precursor metabolism. One representative gene, \texttt{SORBI\_3006G248300}, belongs to a lipoxygenase-related clade implicated in oxylipin metabolism (Table \ref{tab:T1_control_recurrent_combined}). In maize, the related enzyme \textit{ZmLOX6} was characterized as a plastid-localized LOX-like protein that metabolizes 13-LOX-derived fatty acid hydroperoxides, and its transcript abundance is induced by jasmonic acid but repressed by abscisic acid, salicylic acid, and ethylene \citep{Gao2008ZmLOX6}. These observations support interpreting the sorghum LOX6-like association as variation in a lipid-oxidation that may influence oxylipin production.

A second candidate in the same locus group is \texttt{SORBI\_3006G248100}, orthologous to \textit{AtOBL1}. In Arabidopsis, \textit{AtOBL1} localizes to lipid droplets and acts as a lipase on TG, DG, and MG, consistent with a role in mobilizing acyl groups from neutral-lipid pools \citep{Muller2018AtOBL1}. The same study further proposed that TG can contribute directly to membrane-lipid synthesis and that OBL-family proteins participate more broadly in acyl-group flux \citep{Muller2018AtOBL1}. This candidate most conservatively highlights a candidate regulator of TG/DG/MG interconversion and acyl remobilization.

Finally, \texttt{SORBI\_3006G248000} encodes an FPS-like trans-prenyltransferase that is positioned upstream of multiple isoprenoid end-products. In rice, FPS-family trans-prenyltransferases are classified as predicted FPP synthases, and plant trans-prenyltransferases more broadly mediate sequential head-to-tail condensations of isopentenyl diphosphate with allylic diphosphate substrates to generate prenyl-diphosphate backbones \citep{Suratin2020RicePT}. These intermediates act as branch-point precursors for major terpenoid pathways, including sterols, carotenoids, chlorophyll-associated metabolites, and phytohormones \citep{Suratin2020RicePT}. Thus, the sorghum FPS-like candidate might be related to upstream isoprenoid precursor supply rather than a single terminal metabolite class.

Together, the LOX6-like, OBL1-like, and FPS-like candidates define a coherent CTL-associated theme centered on lipid oxidation, neutral-lipid turnover, and upstream isoprenoid metabolism with functional validation still needed to confirm the precise enzymatic roles.


\subsection{Nutrient sensing and recycling pathways provide upstream context for lipid variation under LIN}

Several LIN candidates converge on a common theme of nutrient stress and remobilization. Two adjacent loci, \texttt{SORBI\_3001G056400} and \texttt{SORBI\_3001G056500}, encode tryptophan synthase family members, placing amino acid metabolism upstream of the Trp–IAA (indole-3-acetic acid) regulatory module. Tryptophan is the main precursor of IAA. This links N-stress physiology, hormone-regulated development, and lipid phenotypes without implying direct enzymatic action on those lipids(\cite{Stepanova2008,Zhao2001,Fu2022,Liu2024,Wang2025}).

Several LIN candidates converge on a theme related to nutrient-responsive developmental regulation. Two adjacent loci, \texttt{SORBI\_3001G056400} and \texttt{SORBI\_3001G056500}, encode tryptophan synthase family members, linking this region to tryptophan biosynthesis. This is relevant to LIN because N metabolites, including tryptophan, have been implicated in auxin-regulated growth responses, and sorghum studies under low-N conditions further show that Trp metabolism, IAA-related pathways, root elongation, and carbon/N metabolic responses are coupled (\cite{Stepanova2008,Zhao2001,Fu2022,Liu2024,Wang2025}).

Another nutrient-associated candidate is \texttt{SORBI\_3001G384500}, whose maize ortholog, \texttt{Zm00001d029012}, is a strong candidate for N-uptake traits in seedling waxy maize (\cite{Gladman2022SorghumBase}). This ortholog was consistently detected across environments by integrating GWAS and co-expression network analyzes and was one of four genes repeatedly associated with N-uptake phenotypes \citep{Luo2025}. The cross-species concordance supports interpreting the LRR-RLK–like signal as part of nutrient-stress signaling, consistent with established roles in plant development and abiotic/biotic stress responses \citep{Dufayard2017}.

Moreover, the phosphate-starvation regulator \texttt{SORBI\_3001G384300} (a \textit{PHR1}-like MYB-CC transcription factor) supports a P-homeostasis axis implicated by the GWAS signals (\ref{tab:T1_control_recurrent_combined}). \textit{PHR1} coordinates transcriptional responses to phosphate limitation as a key upstream regulator of phosphate-starvation programs that coincide with phospholipid remodeling, making a PHR1-like association more consistent with regulatory variation in nutrient-stress signaling than with direct effects on lipid enzymatic activity (\cite{Pant2015,Rubio2001,Bustos2010,Nilsson2007,Rouached2011}).

Finally, another candidate gene \texttt{SORBI\_3002G117000} encodes an ATG8-interacting protein, consistent with roles in starvation-induced recycling pathways. In plants, ATG8-interacting proteins such as ATI1/ATI2 act as selective autophagy cargo receptors during carbon starvation and related stresses, and ATG8 itself is lipidated with PE during autophagosome biogenesis (\cite{Honig2012,Wu2021ATI,Michaeli2014}). Thus, an ATI-like candidate in our lipid GWAS supports genetic variation in nutrient-stress–induced recycling capacity that might modulate membrane and metabolite turnover under LIN conditions.

Thus, Trp biosynthesis, nitrate-linked signaling candidates, phosphate-starvation regulation, and ATG8-mediated recycling define a coherent nutrient-stress framework that may underlie the enrichment of nutrient-signaling candidates that need further functional validation.


\subsection{Cuticle barrier formation and ubiquitin-mediated proteostasis as cold-linked candidates under LIN}

Two LIN candidates are consistent with upstream processes that can influence cold and stress performance without directly encoding enzymes that modify the measured lipid species. \texttt{SORBI\_3001G222700}, a \textit{CER1}/\textit{WDA1}-like wax locus, is homologous to rice \textit{Wax-deficient anther1} (\textit{WDA1}), a gene required for very-long-chain fatty acid-derived wax production and cuticle formation \citep{Jung2006WDA1, Gladman2022SorghumBase}. Cuticular waxes are major components of the hydrophobic barrier covering aerial tissues and help limit non-stomatal water loss while also contributing to protection against environmental stress \citep{Jung2006WDA1,LeeSuh2022}. Wax deposition and composition are environmentally responsive, and low temperature can alter total wax load and wax composition across species, linking wax-associated genetic variation to cold-related field performance \citep{LeeSuh2022}.

Another candidate \texttt{SORBI\_3002G141600} is similar to a cullin-family protein. Cullins function as scaffold proteins in cullin--RING E3 ubiquitin ligase complexes that mediate selective protein turnover through the ubiquitin--proteasome system and thereby influence hormone signaling, development, and environmental stress responses \citep{WangXie2024Cullin}. In the context of cold stress, cullin-associated E3 ligase activity has been implicated in the regulation of key signaling components, including ICE1- and CBF-centered pathways, consistent with a role in tuning the abundance of proteins required for cold acclimation \citep{WangXie2024Cullin}.

Together, the \textit{WDA1-like} wax locus and the CUL1-like proteostasis candidate support a coherent cold-linked interpretation of the LIN associations, rather than remodeling the measured lipid pools. Further validation constitutes a promising direction.

\subsection{Defense-related candidates under LIN implicate receptor-like immune signaling and pathogenesis-related defense proteins}

Among the LIN candidates, a smaller subset was linked to defense-related processes, which may be consistent with the LIN management regime that did not use pesticides, herbicides, or insecticides. These loci point more specifically to immune perception and pathogenesis-related defense proteins. The strongest defense signal involved \texttt{SORBI\_3010G170150}, while additional support came from \texttt{SORBI\_3002G117300} as a pathogenesis-related thaumatin-like protein (\cite{Gladman2022SorghumBase}), and \texttt{SORBI\_3001G101900}, whose maize homolog is \textit{Zm00001eb056280} (\textit{prp22}, pathogenesis-related protein 22).

\texttt{SORBI\_3010G170150} is homologous to \textit{At5g10530}, which encodes the lectin receptor kinase \textit{LecRK-IX.1}. In \textit{Arabidopsis thaliana}, \textit{LecRK-IX.1} and its close paralog \textit{LecRK-IX.2} positively regulate resistance to \textit{Phytophthora brassicae} and \textit{Phytophthora capsici}. The loss-of-function mutants show reduced resistance, double mutants have additive susceptibility, and overexpression lines display enhanced resistance, although can also trigger cell death. These studies show that \textit{LecRK-IX.1} and \textit{LecRK-IX.2} directly mediate \textit{Phytophthora} resistance via receptor-like immune signaling. They further place these kinases in the broader cell-surface immune perception network, where receptor-like kinases activate downstream defenses after pathogen detection \citep{Wang2015LecRKIX}.

Another LIN candidate, \texttt{SORBI\_3002G117300} is annotated in SorghumBase (\cite{Gladman2022SorghumBase}) as a pathogenesis-related thaumatin-like protein, a class commonly linked to antifungal activity and defense. A third candidate, \texttt{SORBI\_3001G101900}, is homologous to the maize gene \textit{Zm00001eb056280} (\textit{prp22}), a member of the maize PR-1 family.A genome-wide analysis of the maize PR-1 family identified 17 \textit{ZmPR-1} genes, showed that maize PR-1 proteins are closely related to sorghum PR-1 proteins, and found promoter cis-elements associated with fungal elicitor response, defense and stress responses, hormone signaling, low-temperature response, and drought response \citep{Ma2022ZmPR1}. Several maize PR-1 genes are induced by multiple fungal pathogens and by SA, ABA, MeJA, and H\textsubscript{2}O\textsubscript{2}, supporting an important role for the \textit{ZmPR-1} family in disease resistance \citep{Ma2022ZmPR1}.

Taken together, these LIN-associated defense candidates indicate variation in both immune perception and downstream pathogenesis-related defenses, spanning a receptor-like kinase module and PR protein families. This helps explain why defense-linked loci recur under LIN management, while recognizing that further functional validation needs to be addressed.

\subsection*{Integrative lipidomics, reaction-network context, and GWAS reveals coordinated lipid redistribution}

SAP lipidome analysis shows that LIN is defined by coordinated shifts among glycolipids, sulfolipids, phospholipids, and TG pool, rather than isolated changes in single classes. Across conditions, 244 lipid species were quantified (153 shared, 49 CTL-specific, 42 LIN-specific), with glycerolipids and glycerophospholipids dominating diversity (Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s4_counts} A; Supplementary Table~S6 A,B,C). TG and PC were the most abundant species in both environments (TG: 71 CTL, 69 LIN; PC: 39 CTL, 38 LIN). By class (\%TIC), galactolipids and phospholipids dominated, with monogalactosyldiacylglycerol (MGDG) the largest contributor (34.5\% CTL; 32.1\% LIN). Against this background, LIN showed four main signatures: (i) reduced SQDG(SQDG; 2.9\% $\rightarrow$ 1.4\%), (ii) increased TG (2.7\% $\rightarrow$ 5.4\%), (iii) redistribution of phosphatidylserine (PS) within the phospholipid network, with PS strongly positively shifted in both CLR and ALR spaces, and (iv) lysophospholipid remodeling, reflected in altered LPC/LPE dynamics (Supplementary \ref{fig:suppfig_s3_comp}). LION-based enrichment independently supports this view, with enrichment in neutral-lipid and storage-related terms (TGs, lipid storage, lipid droplet), whereas CTL is enriched in membrane phospholipid and membrane-component categories (Figure \ref{fig:fig1_lipidomics_landscape}D).

Network- and reaction-level analyzes place these signatures in a biochemical framework in which DG links membrane remodeling to TG dynamics. LINEX-based reaction mapping (RHEA-annotated, rice reference) identifies four candidate branches connecting DG/monoacylglycerol (MG), TG, and lysophospholipid pools (PC+DG$\leftrightarrow$LPC+TG; TG$\leftrightarrow$DG; PE+DG$\leftrightarrow$TG+LPE; DG+MG$\leftrightarrow$TG) (Figure \ref{fig:Fig4_linex_rewiring_abc}A). These links are annotation-derived hypotheses rather than direct evidence of in vivo flux direction. Consistent with strong context dependence, 1709 of 3003 ratio--ratio edges (56.9\%) change classification between trials, indicating broad network reconfiguration rather than a uniform increase in connectivity (Supplementary Table~S12). Reaction-consistency scoring further supports coherent shifts toward TG- and lysophospholipid-associated product states (positive LIN--CTL effects for PNPLA3, LCAT*, and LRO1), whereas the TG$\rightarrow$DG branch shifts in the opposite direction. Within the LINEX DG--TG module, mapping individual reaction branches to GWAS candidates highlights strong LIN-associated support for LCAT-like and TG/DG-cycle genes, including SORBI\_3001G103800 (LCAT-like; 19 LIN traits, 14 reaction-linked), SORBI\_3001G041900 (PNPLA-domain TAG lipase-like; 8 LIN traits, all reaction-linked), and SORBI\_3010G170000 (DGAT1; 22 LIN traits, 7 reaction-linked), with top p-values from $3.16 \times 10^{-15}$ to $1.13 \times 10^{-9}$ (Supplementary Table~S13; Supplementary Table~S14). Taken together, these network layers support the interpretation that the four LIN-associated signatures arise from coordinated rebalancing across interconnected lipid pools rather than independent changes in individual metabolite abundances.

Together, lipid abundance and composition, functional ontology, network rewiring, reaction topology, and genetic associations support a model in which LIN exposure is associated with (i) reduced SQDG, (ii) expanded TG, (iii) phospholipid headgroup redistribution centered on PS, and (iv) altered lysophospholipid balance (LPC/LPE). However, causal directionality and mechanisms remain tentative and require targeted functional validation.


\section*{Conclusion}

This study establishes SoLD as a population-scale lipidomics resource for the SAP and shows that contrasting field conditions drive extensive lipidome remodeling. Across 244 annotated lipid species, the LIN context relative to CTL showed four consistent compositional signatures: (i) SQDG depletion, (ii) TG enrichment, (iii) PS-centered redistribution within the phospholipid pool, and (iv) coordinated lysophospholipid remodeling, reflected in an altered LPC/LPE balance. Abundance contrasts, compositional analyses, chemical-space summaries, and lipid ontology enrichment all support a coordinated redistribution across interconnected lipid pools in LIN, rather than isolated changes in a few lipid classes. These patterns are interpreted as genotype-matched, between-trial differences rather than direct causal effects of the LIN conditions. GWAS integration showed that under CTL, recurrent loci were enriched for genes linked to lipid and isoprenoid metabolism, chloroplast function, growth, and cell-wall processes. Under LIN, recurrent loci were enriched for genes involved in nutrient signaling and remobilization, cell-wall remodeling, defense, developmental regulation, and cold-associated barrier and proteostasis functions. Thus, lipid traits reflect both core metabolic variation and broader physiological programs for growth, acclimation, and stress-responsive regulation. LINEX network-enzyme analysis further situated the compositional patterns in a biochemical framework by highlighting DG–TG and phospholipid-associated branches, including LCAT-like, PNPLA-like, and DGAT1-associated reactions, with candidate GWAS loci mapping to these branches. Although these network-level interpretations are hypothesis-generating rather than direct evidence of in vivo flux, they define focused candidate mechanisms for future functional tests. SoLD therefore serves as a community-wide data resource and an integrative framework linking sorghum lipid diversity with environmental and genetic variation. The SoLD Shiny application makes these data accessible by supporting exploration and visualization of individual lipid species, lipid-class–level trends, and GWAS through an interactive interface. As shown by the triterpenoid case study, users can move seamlessly from lipid-class summaries to specific molecular species and their genetic associations. By integrating curated lipidomics, GWAS results, and interactive visualization, this work provides a foundation for future sorghum lipid research under CTL and LIN conditions.


\section*{Materials and methods}

\subsection*{Plant Material and Growth Conditions}

In our study, we used the Sorghum Association Panel (SAP), consisting of 400 accessions designed to cover extensive genetic and phenotypic diversity. This collection includes both temperate-adapted breeding lines and tropical landraces. The panel represents five botanical races, bicolor, caudatum, durra, guinea, and kafir, capturing a diverse range of domestication and adaptation processes.

SAP was first genotyped using simple sequence repeat markers, followed by low-coverage genotyping by sequencing (GBS). For a more comprehensive variation set, \cite{Boatwright2022} et al. resequenced all entries using whole genome sequencing (WGS) with an average depth of 38× (ranging from 25–72×). The variant data from WGS revealed approximately 43.98 million polymorphisms, including roughly 38 million SNPs with 5 million small insertions/deletions. While GBS variants were predominantly located in genic regions, the WGS data were more evenly distributed across genic and intergenic regions. Genome-wide linkage disequilibrium is approximately 20 kb, although there were deviations specific to each chromosome. The consequent high-density variant map establishes the resequenced SAP as a valuable tool for examining diversity and conducting genome wide association studies (GWAS).

We evaluated SAP accessions across two different field settings during two growing seasons (2019 and 2022) at the Pee Dee Research and Education Center, Clemson University, Florence, South Carolina. The "control" condition, herein denoted as CTL, involved standard agronomic inputs with sufficient levels of nitrogen (N) and phosphorus (P) along with a typical planting schedule. In contrast, the "low input" condition, herein denoted as LIN,  featured reduced N and P coupled with earlier planting to mimic a cold stress environment.

\subsection*{Lipidomics Analysis}

\paragraph{Sample Preparation and Extraction.} 
Samples were prepared following the standard extraction protocols explained in the paper by \cite{Barnes2022}.

\paragraph{Liquid Chromatography-Mass Spectrometry (LC-MS) Analysis.} Lipid extracts were subjected to high-resolution mass spectrometry employing both positive and negative ionization modes to achieve comprehensive lipid coverage. Chromatographic separation was executed utilizing either reversed-phase columns, such as C18, or hydrophilic interaction chromatography (HILIC) columns, contingent upon the polarity of the lipids. In the case of C18 methodologies, the gradient elution commenced at 1 minute and concluded at 8 minutes, succeeded by an isocratic elution phase from 8 to 9.5 minutes. Data preceding 1 minute and subsequent to 9.5 minutes were omitted to eliminate solvent front and late eluting artifacts. For HILIC methodologies, the gradient initiation occurred at 1 minute, terminating at 16.25 minutes, and was followed by an isocratic elution extending until 18.5 minutes; data before 1 minute and after 18.5 minutes were excluded in a similar manner.

\paragraph{Feature Detection and Data Processing.} Raw LC-MS data, in both positive and negative ion modes, were processed utilizing MZmine 2, an open-source software for the analysis of mass spectrometry data (\cite{Pluskal2010MZmine2}). The pipeline encompassed peak detection, chromatogram construction, deconvolution, and isotope filtering, producing a detailed feature table containing mass-to-charge ratio-retention time (mz-rt) pairs. Isotopic peaks were excluded to minimize redundancy, as a single metabolite can yield multiple co-eluting ions, such as adducts and in-source fragments. Therefore, mz-rt duplicates were handled with care, with potential de-adducting considered via MS-FLO when appropriate. We acknowledge that such degeneracy can lead to an inflated number of features compared to the actual number of metabolites present, which we considered during metabolite identification. 

\paragraph{Blank/extraction-control filtering, intensity thresholds, and sparsity pruning.}
To reduce background and carryover effects, an extraction control filter was implemented at the feature level. For each feature, the maximum intensity was determined across extraction controls (\(a\)) and biological samples (\(b\)). Features for which \(b < 10\times a\) were eliminated. To preclude the exclusion of borderline yet potentially biological signals, a feature was retained if at least one biological sample exceeded the extraction control maximum. Furthermore, a minimum average intensity threshold within the treatment groups of interest (\(\sim 10^{6}\) peak height) was imposed to ensure that downstream analyzes would emphasize robust signals. At the sample level, any sample exhibiting \(\geq 70\%\) features as zero (or missing) was excluded prior to normalization and statistical analysis. This pruning of sparsity is essential to prevent unstable scaling and spurious differential signals caused by ultra-sparse profiles. 


\paragraph{MS/MS spectral library matching and cross-referencing of IDs.}
For each feature analyzed by MS/MS, the most intense fragmentation spectrum was queried against the GNPS database. Library matches resulted in putative identifications (levels 2/3), potentially including isomers or near-mass analogs. Features without direct matches were eliminated. To enable quantification with identifications, tables were linked using the feature \emph{row ID} from the MZmine peak list and the corresponding \#Scan\# key in the GNPS results, ensuring a one-to-one correspondence between intensities and candidate identifications. In cases where a feature yielded multiple GNPS hits, a single primary annotation was designated by retaining the highest MQScore (cosine similarity). Ties in the values were resolved based on a greater number of shared fragment ions and a smaller precursor mass error (ppm). All other sub-threshold or lower-ranked candidates were retained for verification but were excluded from subsequent statistical analyzes.

\paragraph{Systematic Error Removal Using Random Forest (SERRF).}
Following the cleaning process, the data were then used for SERRF normalization (\cite{Fan2019}). We used the SERRF server (https://slfan2013.github.io/SERRF-online/\#) to obtain the normalized output. After applying SERRF, only biological samples were preserved. Any zeros were substituted with two-thirds of the minimum nonzero value for that feature to prevent potential infinite logarithmic transformations.

\paragraph{Spatial Correction.}
Finally, we conducted an additional quality control step specifically aimed at eliminating any spatial patterns across our experimental trials. This was achieved using the R package \texttt{SpATS} (\cite{RodriguezAlvarez2018}), which applies a two-dimensional P-spline ANOVA surface over the field coordinates. For every lipid feature, we characterized its intensity as
\begin{align}
  y_{ij} &= \mu + f_{\mathrm{row}}(i) + f_{\mathrm{col}}(j) + f_{\mathrm{row,col}}(i,j) + \varepsilon_{ij},
\end{align}

where 
\begin{itemize}
  \item \(f_{\mathrm{row}}\) and \(f_{\mathrm{col}}\) represent smooth functions that model systematic effects across rows and columns, respectively, 
  \item \(f_{\mathrm{row,col}}\) is a smooth interaction surface that handles more complex spatial gradients. 
\end{itemize}

We utilized the residuals, defined as the difference between observed intensity and the fitted spatial trend, as our analysis-ready spatially corrected intensity data which will be used for \%TIC, CLR, and log-ratio. We used SpATS-adjusted intensities (residuals + fitted mean) to preserve scale and avoid negative values. This methodology effectively corrects for positional artifacts, such as edge effects, that could interfere with subsequent analyzes. Detailed smoothing parameters, including the number of knots, penalty orders, and comprehensive model specifications, can be found in our GitHub repository.

\paragraph{Lipid Quantification}
The identified lipid species were organized into lipid classes and subclasses (see Supplementary Table\~1) based on Lipid Maps (\cite{Fahy2009LipidMapsClass}). In each sample, the total intensity for a class was obtained by summing the intensities of the species within that class. To manage variability in signal intensity due to different runs or injections, these class totals were normalized relative to the total ion current (TIC) of the sample. As a result, the relative abundances were presented as percentages of the TIC by adding up intensities across all lipid classes in the sample. For each class and its subclasses, we determined the TIC fraction for each sample and then averaged these percentages across samples for each condition (CTL, \$n=384\$; LIN, \$n=362\$). Lipids were categorized into glycerolipid, glycerophospholipid, sphingolipid, sterol, betaine lipid, fatty acid, ether lipid, and terpenoid. Refer to Supplementary Table 2 for the full list. 

\paragraph{Lipid ratio calculation}
For each sample $i$ and lipid class $c$, species intensities were aggregated to class totals,
\[
C_{i,c}=\sum_{k \in c} I_{i,k}.
\]
Because log-ratios are scale-invariant, TIC normalization is not required for ratio computation.
To handle zero class totals, we used a sample-specific pseudo-count,
\[
\varepsilon_i=0.5 \cdot \min\{C_{i,c} : C_{i,c}>0\},
\]
and computed
\[
\ell_{i,c}=\log_{10}(C_{i,c}+\varepsilon_i).
\]
Pairwise class log-ratios were then
\[
r_{i,c_1/c_2}=\ell_{i,c_1}-\ell_{i,c_2}.
\]
Positive LIN--CTL effects indicate the enrichment of the numerator class in LIN.


\paragraph{Statistical Tests.}
We used two complementary nonparametric approaches, reflecting that the dataset supports both population-level and matched-genotype analyzes. For class-level contrasts, lipid species were summed to class totals, and class log-ratios for sample \(i\) were defined as
\[
r_{i,c_1/c_2}=\log_{10}(C_{i,c_1}+\varepsilon_i)-\log_{10}(C_{i,c_2}+\varepsilon_i),
\]
where \(C_{i,c}\) is the total abundance of class \(c\) in sample \(i\), and \(\varepsilon_i\) is a sample-specific pseudocount to avoid undefined logarithms for zeros. For species-resolved analyzes, we used the centered log-ratio (CLR) transformation to account for compositionality:
\[
\mathrm{CLR}(x_i)=\ln\!\left(\frac{x_i}{g(\mathbf{x})}\right),
\]
where \(g(\mathbf{x})\) is the geometric mean of species-level abundances in a sample.

In the primary population-level analysis, CTL and LIN samples were treated as independent groups, using all available observations. Two-sided Wilcoxon rank-sum tests (unpaired) were applied separately to class-ratio features and species-level CLR features, allowing unequal group sizes. Effect sizes were summarized as median LIN\(-\)CTL differences. For class ratios, fold-changes were obtained by back-transforming median differences from the \(\log_{10}\) scale; for CLR features, fold-changes were obtained by exponentiating the median CLR difference, where applicable.

For a matched-genotype sensitivity analysis, we restricted to genotypes present in both conditions and applied two-sided paired Wilcoxon signed-rank tests to within-genotype LIN--CTL differences for selected class log-ratio features. This paired analysis primarily assessed the robustness of effect direction within shared genotypes, whereas the unpaired analysis provided the main population-level comparison.

To further assess directional robustness, we calculated a leave-one-out jackknife stability metric. For each feature, the median CTL--LIN contrast was recomputed after excluding each observation in turn, and jackknife stability was defined as the proportion of omissions for which the sign of the median effect was unchanged. A value of 1.0 indicates that no single omission altered the inferred effect direction. \(P\)-values were adjusted within each feature family using the BH procedure to control the false discovery rate. All CTL--LIN contrasts are interpreted as between-trial differences rather than strictly causal treatment effects.


\subsection*{Principal Component Analysis (PCA)}

Principal component analysis (PCA) was performed on individual lipid species within each experimental condition. Lipid intensities were first normalized to total ion current (TIC) to obtain relative abundances, then transformed using a centered log-ratio (CLR). The CLR-transformed data were mean-centered and scaled to unit variance, and PCA was conducted in R using the \emph{stats} package. The first two principal components were retained for visualization.


\subsection*{Genome-wide Association Studies (GWAS)} 

GWAS analyzes were carried out for each lipid trait under each condition using the mixed linear model (MLM) featured in GEMMA (v2.3) (\cite{Zhou2012}). To address population structure and relatedness, a centered relatedness matrix (kinship) was computed from SNP genotype data. We filtered the genotype data by MAF 0.05 and heterozygosity of 0.8. For each lipid trait, the MLM was applied using the kinship matrix to handle population stratification effects. Besides individual traits, GWAS was also applied to summed lipid classes and all possible ratios (refer to Supplementary Table 1). GWAS analysis was conducted on the first two PCs for each class. A significance threshold of \(-\log_{10}(p) \geq 7\) was employed in order to account for multiple comparisons. 

\subsection*{Gene Annotation}

SNPs were aligned with the Sorghum bicolor reference genome v3.1 (BTx623). For each marker, a 50 kb segment was designated, spanning 25 kb on either side, and all gene models within this area were retrieved. Functional annotations and homology were obtained from Phytozome (https://phytozome.jgi.doe.gov), SorghumBase (https://sorghumbase.com), and TAIR for corresponding Arabidopsis thaliana orthologs. Genes with known roles in N, P, cold tolerance, or lipid metabolism were specifically noted. We aggregated the frequency of each candidate gene within all lipid GWAS findings and marked those with the highest recurrence, showing -log10(p-values) of 7 or greater.

\subsubsection*{Lipid Metabolic Network Analysis (LINEX2)}

We used the Lipid Network Explorer (LINEX2; \url{https://exbio.wzw.tum.de/linex/}) to reconstruct lipid networks and identify condition enriched subgraphs for CTL vs. LIN, considering both the ratio and difference between them. Lipid names were standardized to align with species notation (class plus acyl composition). LINEX2 constructs a global species network based on curated reaction rules, including headgroup interconversions, (de)acylation/editing, elongation, desaturation, and overlays a quantitative association structure (Spearman correlations across samples) onto the reaction edges. For enrichment, LINEX2 calculates substrate–product change scores for each reaction regarding the CTL vs. LIN ratio and difference, employing a greedy local-search procedure to identify subgraphs that optimize the average substrate and product change. Since \textit{Sorghum bicolor} is not implemented as a default organism in LINEX2, we selected \textit{Oryza sativa} (OSA) as the reference species for network construction.


\subsubsection*{Lipid Ontology Enrichment and Hierarchical Classification (LION/web)}

We conducted a functional ontology for lipids using LION/web (Molenaar \emph{et al.}, 2019; \url{http://www.lipidontology.com}). Lipid nomenclature was standardized according to LIPID MAPS annotations. The default settings of LION/web were applied for enrichment statistics and multiple-testing correction. We retained terms for $q \leq 0.10$. A hierarchical classification analysis of individual lipids and functional ontologies was also performed using LION/web.

Data processing and statistical analyzes were performed in R (version 4.3.3). All the codes, figures, and pipeline are described in the GitHub repository: \url{https://www.github.com/nirwan1265/SoLD\_paper}.
