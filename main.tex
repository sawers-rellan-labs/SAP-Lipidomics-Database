\section{Results}

\subsection*{Quality control and normalization of LC-MS features}

[NOTE: Should this go in supplementary?]

We profiled early-stage sorghum lipid profiles from SAP grown under two contrasting field conditions: (i) Control site with sufficient nutrients and a usual planting date (C), and (ii) Low-input site, defined by reduced N and P fertilizers and an earlier planting date to mimic cold stress (LI). The overarching experimental workflow is outlined in Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1_lipidomics_landscape}A, including tissue collection and extraction, high-resolution LC-MS-based lipidomic profiling, GNPS-assisted lipid annotation, and post-acquisition data correction procedures through SERRF and SpATS implemented prior to downstream statistical and genetic analyses (explained in detail in the Methods section), as well as dissemination via the SoLD Shiny application.

Run-order quality control indicated stable analytical performance in both experimental conditions (Supplementary Fig.~S1A,B). Throughout the injection sequence, sample TICs conformed to expectations based on system suitability checks, internal standards, and blanks. TIC variability among QC samples also remained low (Control QC TIC CV = 7.5\%; LI QC TIC CV = 6.0\%; Supplementary Fig.~S1A,B). SERRF normalization enhanced technical precision in both environments, reducing the median QC-RSD from 3.6\% to 2.0\% in the C, and from 1.6\% to 0.3\% in the LI condition (Supplementary Fig.~S1C,D). Consistently, the proportion of features satisfying a QC-RSD $<30\%$ threshold increased from 75.7\% to 94.1\% in C and from 77.3\% to 89.3\% in LI (Supplementary Fig.~S1C,D). Furthermore, PCA of normalized data revealed a more compact clustering of samples relative to the unprocessed signal (Supplementary Fig.~S1E,F). Given that samples were derived from field-grown material, we further accounted for field-position effects using spatial modeling (SpATS) in R. Residual maps indicated that broad-scale row and column structures were largely mitigated following correction (Supplementary Fig.~S1G,H). All together, these procedures generated a normalized, spatially corrected lipid trait matrix that was used for all downstream analyses.

\subsection*{Lipid variation across genotypes}

[NOTE: Should this go in supplementary too?]

To determine whether the observed lipid differences between C and LI conditions reflected systematic, population-wide metabolic reprogramming rather than artifacts driven by a small number of outlier genotypes, we conducted two-sample Wilcoxon rank-sum tests coupled with a leave-one-out jackknife stability analysis. For lipid class–level comparisons, we analyzed log-ratios of class-wise abundance sums, thereby explicitly accounting for the compositional structure of the data. All 33 evaluated class log-ratios exhibited statistically significant differences between conditions (Benjamini–Hochberg–adjusted $p < 0.05$), each with maximal jackknife stability (1.0; Supplementary Table~S1). At the individual lipid species level, we applied a centered log-ratio (CLR) transformation prior to inferential testing to appropriately handle compositional constraints. Among the 172 lipid species shared between conditions, 170 (99\%) differed significantly (Supplementary Table S2). Notably, all 172 species displayed perfect jackknife stability (1.0), demonstrating that the direction of each species-level change was invariant to the exclusion of any single genotype. This pattern was consistent across all lipid classes (Supplementary Table~S3), indicating that the lipidomic remodeling observed under LI conditions represents a robust and uniform response across the 363–394 SAP genotypes examined, rather than being driven by a limited subset of outlier accessions. 

Given this robustness, we next evaluated which lipids exhibited the highest genetic variance. MGDG(18:3/18:3) was the highest-variance lipid in both environments (Supplementary Fig.~S2A,B; Supplementary Table~S4). Seven of the ten highest-variance lipids were shared between C and LI (Supplementary Fig. S2C), suggesting that a conserved subset of lipid traits exhibits strong       
genotype sensitivity across environments. Within this high-variance subset, PC species were the most frequent class in both conditions (C: 7/10; LI: 6/10; Supplementary Fig. S2D; Supplementary Table S4), supporting their prioritization for subsequent genetic mapping and predictive modeling analyses. 

Across both environments, we quantified 244 distinct lipid molecular species, with substantial overlap between conditions (153 shared species) and smaller environment-specific subsets (49 Control-exclusive; 42 LowInput-exclusive; Supplementary Fig.~S4A; Supplementary Table~S6). TG and PC represented the most species-rich classes in each environment (TG: 71 in Control, 69 in LowInput; PC: 39 in Control, 38 in LowInput; Supplementary Table~S6), and broader class-level summaries indicated that glycerolipids and glycerophospholipids constituted the majority of identified species (Supplementary Fig.~S4B,D). PCA of lipid species further revealed that the multivariate structure of the dataset was strongly driven by lipid class, with TGs separating from membrane lipid classes and galactolipids forming distinct clusters under both environmental conditions (Supplementary Fig.~S5).

At the lipid class level, the lipidome was predominantly composed of galactolipids and   
phospholipids, enriched with DG and TG (Supplementary Fig.~S3A). Under C conditions, MGDG accounted for 34.5\% of the mean TIC and declined to 32.1\% under LI, whereas PC remained comparatively stable (25.6\% in C versus 24.8\% in LI). In contrast, TG increased from 2.7\% (C) to 5.4\% (LI), and SQDG decreased from 2.9\% (C) to 1.4\% (LI) (Supplementary Fig.~S3A). Compositional contrast analyses corroborated these trends and further revealed a coordinated headgroup rebalancing within the phospholipid network. CLR contrasts also highlighted PS as one of the strongest positively shifted classes (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1_lipidomics_landscape}B; Supplementary Table~S5). Consistently, TG-referenced ALR contrasts and ratio-based comparisons showed that PS increased relative to multiple phospholipid pools (e.g., PC, PE, and PG), even when absolute \%TIC changes in those classes were modest (Supplementary Fig.~S3B). Moreover, lysophospholipids exhibited a reproducible compositional remodeling signal, with LPC/LPE behavior shifting under LI in a manner consistent with stress-responsive phospholipid turnover rather than uniform scaling of total lyso-lipid abundance (Supplementary Fig.~S3B; Fig.~2 and Supplementary Fig.~S7) (ref). Together, these results support four LI-associated lipid signatures: (i) SQDG depletion, (ii) TG enrichment, (iii) PS enrichment relative to other phospholipid headgroups, and (iv) lysophospholipid remodeling (LPC/LPE), consistent with coordinated membrane restructuring and increased partitioning into storage lipids.

To characterize structural remodeling beyond total class abundance, we embedded each lipid class into a reduced chemical space defined by the weighted mean total carbon number and weighted mean double-bond count (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1_lipidomics_landscape}C). TG occupied the high‑carbon region of this space and exhibited a pronounced shift toward higher total carbon under the LI, with a modest change in average unsaturation (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1_lipidomics_landscape}C). The remaining classes maintained distinct, class‑specific carbon/unsaturation signatures, indicating that the effects of LI manifested both as shifts in class abundance (described above) ((Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1_lipidomics_landscape}B) and as coordinated, class‑level changes in acyl‑chain composition (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1_lipidomics_landscape}C).

Consistent with these shifts, LION enrichment analysis highlighted ontology terms increased under LI that reflect neutral-lipid accumulation and storage biology, including \textit{triacylglycerols [GL0301]}, \textit{glycerolipids [GL]}, \textit{diacylglycerols [GL0201]}, \textit{lipid storage}, and \textit{lipid droplet} (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1_lipidomics_landscape}D). In contrast, ontology terms enriched in C were dominated by membrane phospholipid categories and membrane descriptors, including \textit{glycerophospholipids [GP]}, \textit{glycerophosphocholines [GP01]/diacylglycerophosphocholines [GP0101]}, \textit{glycerophosphoethanolamines [GP02]/diacylglycerophosphoethanolamines [GP0201]}, and \textit{membrane component} (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1_lipidomics_landscape}D). Together, LION corroborates that LI is associated with a coordinated shift toward TG alongside phospholipid/membrane remodeling captured by both abundance contrasts and acyl-chain chemical-space movement.   



\subsection*{Ratio-based multivariate analysis reveals coordinated lipid class transitions under Low-input}

To resolve relative reallocations among major lipid pools, rather than relying on absolute abundance differences, we aggregated individual molecular species into lipid classes (galactolipids, phospholipids, lysophospholipids, and neutral glycerolipids) and computed all pairwise class ratios. This ratio framework serves three purposes. First, because LC–MS signal variation is often multiplicative (e.g., injection volume, ionization efficiency, instrument performance), ratios reduce shared scaling factors among features within a sample. This improves robustness beyond standard normalization, especially for different field samples. Second, ratios directly capture compositional trade-offs among lipid pools (e.g., increased TG vs. membrane lipids, PS enrichment within phospholipids, SQDG depletion relative to other plastid lipids) that may be missed when classes are analyzed separately. Third, many lipidomic responses reflect competitive conversions and pathway-level regulation (e.g., DG→TG storage, PC/PE/PS headgroup rebalancing, shifts between phospholipids under nutrient stress), for which class ratios provide an interpretable readout. Ratios were calculated on a log\(_{10}\) scale (see Methods).              
                                                                                             
An OPLS-DA model constructed from these ratios clearly discriminated C from LI samples along the predictive component (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig2_opls_da}A), indicating that the LI lipidome is characterized by systematic rebalancing among lipid classes. Model diagnostics indicated robust classification performance (1 predictive and 1 orthogonal component; $Q^2_{\mathrm{cum}}=0.977$, $R^2Y_{\mathrm{cum}}=0.977$, $R^2X_{\mathrm{cum}}=0.643$; Fig.~\ref{fig:fig2_opls_da}D), and a label-permutation test (n=200) located the observed model performance at the extreme tail of the corresponding null distributions (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig2_opls_da}C), consistent with a stable, non-overfitted multivariate pattern. VIP-ranked ratios (VIP $>$ 1) revealed a highly structured pattern delineating which lipid pools most strongly discriminate C from LI samples (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig2_opls_da}B; Supplementary Fig.~\ref{fig:suppfig_s7_ratios}A; Supplementary Table~S7). First, many of the highest-ranking ratios were anchored on SQDG (e.g., MG/SQDG, PS/SQDG, PG/SQDG, PE/SQDG, PC/SQDG, LPE/SQDG), and these ratios were consistently elevated in LowInput (Supplementary Fig.~\ref{fig:suppfig_s7_ratios}A), indicating a depletion of SQDG \emph{relative to multiple membrane-associated and neutral lipid pools}. This observation is concordant with the class-level profile, in which SQDG abundance decreases under LI conditions, whereas several other lipid classes (such as TG and, to a lesser extent, PS) exhibit relative enrichment (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1_lipidomics_landscape}B; Supplementary Fig.~\ref{fig:suppfig_s3_composition}). Second, PS emerged as a central node in the remodeling landscape as multiple ratios with PS in the denominator (PC/PS, PE/PS, PG/PS, PA/PS, DG/PS, DGDG/PS, LPC/PS) were elevated in C (Supplementary Fig.~\ref{fig:suppfig_s7_ratios}A), implying that the LI condition is characterized by increased PS abundance relative to a broad spectrum of other lipid classes. Collectively, the SQDG- and PS-centered ratios describe a LI-associated remodeling axis defined by (i) reduced representation of sulfolipids and (ii) a coordinated redistribution of PS across the membrane lipid headgroup.

Beyond these two anchors, the VIP set also highlighted interpretable ``transition-like'' relationships among neutral and membrane pools. Several MG-centered ratios increased in LI (MG/MGDG, MG/SQDG, MG/PG), while DG/MG and DGDG/MG were higher in C (Supplementary Fig.~\ref{fig:suppfig_s7_ratios}A), indicating that LI shifts the balance toward higher MG relative to both DG and galactolipids. In parallel, SQDG/TG was higher in C (Supplementary Fig.~\ref{fig:suppfig_s7_ratios}A), consistent with the expansion of TG observed in class composition summaries and the chemical-space displacement of TG under LI (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1_lipidomics_landscape}C; Supplementary Fig.~\ref{fig:suppfig_s3_composition}). While ratio data do not directly measure metabolic flux, the direction-consistent behavior of these ratios supports a coherent LI program in which plastid-associated glycolipids/sulfolipids are reduced relative to neutral storage and selected membrane pools.                                   
                                                                                             
To further motivate the use of ratios that capture condition-dependent rewiring among lipid classes, we quantified partial correlations (controlling for total TIC) within each environment (Supplementary Fig.~\ref{fig:suppfig_s6_partial_corr}). Numerous lipid-class pairs exhibited sign reversals in their partial correlations between C and LI, indicating altered coordination among lipid pools under LI-associated stress. Ratios constructed from these sign-flip relationships displayed pronounced and directionally consistent shifts across conditions (Supplementary Fig.~\ref{fig:suppfig_s7_ratios}B). Most significantly, TG increased in relative abundance compared with PE, MGDG, DG, and DGDG under LI (TG/PE, TG/MGDG, TG/DG; Supplementary Fig.~\ref{fig:suppfig_s7_ratios}B), supporting a redistribution in which DG likely functions as a biosynthetic intermediate toward TG accumulation. In contrast, the TG/MG ratio was elevated in C (Supplementary Fig.~\ref{fig:suppfig_s7_ratios}B), indicating that MG increased disproportionately relative to TG under LI, consistent with the MG-centered VIP ratios described above (Supplementary Fig.~\ref{fig:suppfig_s7_ratios}A). PS-centered partial-correlation ratios additionally supported a redistribution of headgroup classes. For example, PS/PC, PS/DG, and PS/MGDG were elevated in LowInput (Supplementary Fig.~\ref{fig:suppfig_s7_ratios}B), paralleling the reciprocal pattern observed in the VIP analysis (PC/PS and DG/PS enriched in C; Supplementary Fig.~\ref{fig:suppfig_s7_ratios}A). Finally, lysophospholipid ratios indicated a reproducible shift in lyso-lipid balance as shown by LPC/LPE and LPC/PS being higher in C, whereas LI favored LPE relative to multiple denominators (e.g., LPE/SQDG; LPE/MGDG; Supplementary Fig.~\ref{fig:suppfig_s7_ratios}A). These patterns are consistent with stress-associated remodeling of phospholipid turnover pathways (e.g., PC$\rightarrow$LPC and PE$\rightarrow$LPE branches) rather than a uniform, condition-wide change in total lysolipid abundance.                                   

Several ratio-derived signatures were further substantiated by independent abundance-, composition-, and ontology-based analyses. TG enrichment was corroborated by increased \%TIC and a shift in TG chemical space toward higher carbon, and was additionally supported by LION ontology enrichment for neutral glycerolipid and storage-related categories (e.g., TG and lipid storage/lipid droplet). SQDG depletion was validated by a reduction in \%TIC in conjunction with widespread changes in SQDG-denominator ratios, whereas PS enrichment was supported by its pronounced positive centered log-ratio (CLR) contrast together with PS-centered ratios. In addition, lysophospholipid remodeling emerged as a reproducible ratio signature, with LPE increasing relative to multiple membrane pools and a reduced LPC/LPE balance, consistent with stress-responsive phospholipid turnover (PC\,$\to$\,LPC and PE\,$\to$\,LPE branches) rather than uniform scaling of total lyso-lipid abundance. By contrast, MG, MGDG, and DGDG patterns were predominantly driven by ratio rebalancing, with only modest alterations in class-level composition (e.g., a slight decrease in MGDG \%TIC). This emphasizes that some LI effects are expressed mainly as redistribution among biochemically interconnected lipid pools rather than large changes in any single lipid class.  


\subsection*{Genome-wide Association Studies of Lipid Traits Under LowInput}

We performed GWAS using three representations of the same lipidomics dataset: (i) individual lipid species, (ii) summed abundances at the level of lipid classes, and (iii) all possible pairwise ratios among these aggregated classes. Statistical significance was defined as $p \le 10^{-7}$ (equivalently, $-\log_{10}(p) \ge 7$), and SNPs were mapped to genes using a symmetric 25~kb flanking window around each gene. To prioritize genes exhibiting robust pleiotropic effects across multiple lipid-related traits, we implemented additional filters based on the number of associated phenotypes. Only Genes associated with $\ge 4$ individual lipid species or $\ge 23$ class-level sum/ratio phenotypes (corresponding to upper-quartile cutoffs) were retained for downstream functional annotation.  

Under LowInput conditions, we detected 10,884 distinct genes significantly associated with at least one individual lipid species and 6,516 genes significantly associated with at least one summed-class or ratio lipid phenotype at the genome-wide level. To situate these associations within established stress response pathways, we categorized the candidate genes into four functional groups based on Gene Ontology (GO) annotations, gene family membership, gene name, and known function from literature: P homeostasis, N metabolism, cold stress response, and lipid metabolism.  Comprehensive GWAS summary statistics and corresponding gene annotations are presented in Supplementary Tables~S8--S10, as well as within the accompanying interactive Shiny application.

\paragraph{Lipid metabolism genes.}                                                                
One of the most highly connected genes across all GWAS was \texttt{SORBI\_3007G223801}, encoding a glycerol-3-phosphate dehydrogenase (GPDH), which was associated with 48 distinct sum/ratio phenotypes ($p_{\min} = 1.2 \times 10^{-18}$). GPDH catalyzes the reduction of dihydroxyacetone phosphate to glycerol-3-phosphate, a key precursor for glycerolipid biosynthesis, positioning this gene at a critical metabolic branch point. The Kennedy pathway enzyme diacylglycerol O-acyltransferase 1 (\texttt{SORBI\_3010G170000}, DGAT1) showed associations with 5 individual lipid species ($p_{\min} = 3.2 \times 10^{-15}$) and 17 sum/ratio phenotypes ($p = 2.8 \times 10^{-10}$), consistent with its established role in TG biosynthesis. Additionally, \texttt{SORBI\_3001G283700}, encoding a chloroplastic glycerol-3-phosphate acyltransferase (GPAT), is associated with 47 sum/ratio phenotypes ($p = 2.1 \times 10^{-13}$), implicating plastidial lipid biosynthesis in the LI response. Two lecithin-cholesterol acyltransferase-like genes were also identified: \texttt{SORBI\_3001G103800} (LCAT-like 1) was associated with 4 individual lipids and 15 sum/ratio phenotypes ($p = 3.1 \times 10^{-12}$), while \texttt{SORBI\_3006G214500} (LCAT like 4) showed associations with 4 individual lipids ($p = 4.1 \times 10^{-10}$).          

\paragraph{Nitrogen metabolism genes.}    

Among the nitrogen-associated candidate genes, \texttt{SORBI\_3001G541900}, which encodes a NRT1/PTR FAMILY 6.3–type transporter homolog, was significantly associated with 37 sum and ratio phenotypes ($p = 3.8 \times 10^{-11}$). NRT1/PTR transporters are known to mediate nitrate uptake and redistribution, thereby coupling nitrogen acquisition to downstream metabolic reprogramming (ref). In addition, three tandemly arranged tryptophan synthase genes (\texttt{SORBI\_3001G056400}, \texttt{SORBI\_3001G056500}, \texttt{SORBI\_3001G056601}) were each associated with 5–7 individual lipid species ($p_{\min} = 1.6 \times 10^{-10}$) and 16–17 sum/ratio phenotypes, indicative of coordinated regulation of amino acid and lipid metabolism under nutrient limitation. The amino acid transporter gene \texttt{SORBI\_3010G116400} exhibited robust associations with both individual lipid traits ($p = 2.5 \times 10^{-12}$, 3 traits) and ratio phenotypes ($p = 1.9 \times 10^{-12}$, 26 traits).  

\paragraph{Phosphorus homeostasis genes.}

The transcription factor PHOSPHATE STARVATION RESPONSE 1 (\texttt{SORBI\_3001G384300}, PHR1) exhibited significant associations with nine individual lipid species ($p_{\min} = 6.6 \times 10^{-11}$), consistent with its established function in coordinating phospholipid remodeling under phosphate deficiency. Purple acid phosphatase 18 (\texttt{SORBI\_3001G186800}, PAP18) was associated with five individual lipid species ($p = 5.7 \times 10^{-9}$); PAP enzymes catalyze the mobilization of organic phosphorus and are transcriptionally upregulated in response to phosphate deprivation.  Additionally, the MYB-CC transcription factor PHOSPHATE STARVATION RESPONSE 1-LIKE (\texttt{SORBI\_3002G161700}, PHL) was associated with 29 sum/ratio phenotypes ($p = 1.4 \times 10^{-9}$), reinforcing the central role of the PHR1/PHL regulatory network in lipid remodeling under nutrient limitation.

\paragraph{Cold stress response genes.}    

Two genes with previously characterized functions in cold acclimation were significantly associated with variation in lipid-related phenotypes. The bZIP transcription factor \texttt{SORBI\_3003G363400}, a homolog of ABA-INSENSITIVE 5 (ABI5), was associated with 28 aggregate or ratio-based lipid phenotypes ($p = 6.4 \times 10^{-10}$); ABI5 is known to integrate abscisic acid (ABA) and low-temperature signaling to modulate membrane lipid composition. The AP2/ERF family member \texttt{SORBI\_3003G324400} (AP37) was associated with five individual lipid species ($p = 1.3 \times 10^{-8}$) and has been reported to confer enhanced drought and cold tolerance in cereal crops.

\subsection*{Lipid modules predict plant height with high accuracy and cross-dataset reproducibility}

To complement the GWAS-based gene discovery, we applied a machine learning framework to evaluate the predictive power of lipid profiles for agronomic traits. We applied XGBoost regression with SHAP-based interpretation. Given the high correlation among lipid species within biochemical classes, we first grouped the 226 individual lipids into 27 correlation-derived modules using hierarchical clustering based on Spearman correlation distances (Methods). This module-level strategy mitigates the  limitation whereby SHAP importance values become attenuated and unstable in the presence of highly collinear predictors (ref).


\paragraph{Plant height prediction.}                                                              
For plant height, the XGBoost model exhibited robust predictive performance on our dataset ($R^2 = 0.752 \pm 0.027$, 5-fold CV; $n = 388$; Fig.~5A). Module-level SHAP analysis indicated that a single TG-dominated module (M1; 22 lipids) contributed the majority of the predictive signal, with a mean absolute module SHAP approximately 10-fold higher than the second-ranked module (Fig.~5B). Lipids assigned to M1 consisted primarily of short- and medium-chain TG, including TG(10:0/10:0/10:0), TG(12:0/12:0/14:0), and TG(10:0/12:0/16:0).

To further substantiate these findings, we replicated the analysis using plant height phenotypes obtained from an independent study (Boyles et al.). Although the overall predictive performance was reduced in this dataset ($R^2 = 0.36 \pm 0.154$; $n = 264$), the ranking of module importance was highly concordant, i.e., 8 of the top 10 modules were shared between datasets, and the correlation of module importance scores was effectively perfect ($r = 0.99$; Fig.~5C,D). Notably, M1 (TG-enriched) remained the top-ranked module in both datasets, indicating that short- and medium-chain TG represent robust and reproducible biomarkers of plant height across independent studies.

\paragraph{Flowering time shows no lipid-based predictive signal but reveals consistent module patterns.}                                                                                         
In contrast to plant height, lipid modules exhibited negligible predictive power for flowering time(days to anthesis). Across all five environments evaluated, including our DTA measurements and the four environments reported in Wei et al.\ (MI20, NE20, IA21\_1, IA21\_2), the cross-validated $R^2$ values were negative or close to zero (range: $-0.39$ to $-0.05$; Fig.~S10B), indicating that lipid profiles do not explain appreciable variation in flowering time.

\subsubsection{Ratio-, ontology-, and network- based evidence supports coordinated redistribution across lipid pools under Low-input}

Class ratios provide a direct readout of relative lipid reallocation within each sample, enabling us to distinguish compositional tradeoffs from uniform scaling of total signal. Across LowInput, ratio signatures consistently indicated (i) depletion of SQDG relative to multiple pools, (ii) enrichment of PS within the phospholipid network, (iii) increased TG relative to membrane pools and glycerolipid intermediates such as DG, and (iv) lysophospholipid remodeling characterized by increased LPE relative to multiple membrane pools and a reduced LPC/LPE balance, consistent with stress-responsive phospholipid turnover rather than a uniform increase in total lyso-lipid abundance. A fifth, MG-centered signature was also reproducible, with LI favoring higher MG relative to MGDG and SQDG and Control favoring higher DG/MG and DGDG/MG, indicating condition-dependent rebalancing among monoacyl, diacyl, and (glyco)diacyl pools.

LION enrichment analysis provided an orthogonal corroboration of these ratio-defined patterns by identifying coherent ontology shifts aligned with neutral-lipid accumulation and storage biology (e.g., enrichment for triacylglycerols and lipid storage/lipid droplet terms) and concurrent remodeling within membrane-associated lipid categories. Together, the ratio and LION results support the interpretation that LowInput lipid remodeling is dominated by coordinated redistribution across interconnected lipid pools, rather than isolated changes in a single class.


\subsection{Phosphorus homeostasis regulators implicate Pi-starvation control of phospholipid remodeling.}

A prominent signal from the lipid GWAS was the enrichment of canonical phosphate (Pi) starvation regulators among phospholipid-associated loci. In plants, the MYB-CC transcription factor \textit{PHR1} is widely recognized as a central coordinator of Pi deprivation responses (Rubio et al., 2001). Mechanistically, PHR1 binds the P1BS cis-element (GNATATNC) present in promoters of Pi starvation–induced (PSI) genes and activates transcriptional programs spanning phosphate transport, signaling, and broader metabolic adaptation to Pi scarcity (Bustos et al., 2010). Genetic perturbations underscore its system-wide role: \textit{phr1} loss-of-function compromises PSI gene induction and alters hallmark Pi-starvation outputs, whereas PHR1 overexpression enhances Pi uptake and improves Pi-starvation responses (Rubio et al., 2001; Bustos et al., 2010; Nilsson et al., 2007). Importantly, PHR1 also connects phosphate status to other nutrient systems; under phosphate deficiency it promotes expression of the sulfate transporter \textit{SULTR1;3} and influences shoot-to-root sulfate redistribution, supporting a broader role in coordinating anion homeostasis during nutrient stress (Rouached et al., 2011). 

In this context, the repeated identification of the sorghum MYB-like DNA-binding gene \texttt{SORBI\_3001G384300} (PHR1-homolog) across phospholipid traits is consistent with natural variation in Pi-starvation regulation contributing to lipidomic variation. This locus was associated with multiple PC and PE molecular species (including PC(16:0/20:3), PC(16:0/22:5), PC(16:0/22:6), PC(18:1/20:1), PE(16:0/18:1), PC(18:1/24:1), PC(18:2/20:0), and PC(18:3/0:0); $p_{\min}=6.6\times10^{-11}$), suggesting that phospholipid composition may be a sensitive downstream readout of the Pi-starvation regulatory state (Supp.~Table). 

PHR1 functions within a multi-layer MYB-CC regulatory network. In Arabidopsis, PHR1 and its close homolog PHL1 are central regulators of Pi starvation transcriptional responses, and they can form homo- and heterodimers, yet even combined disruption only partially attenuates PSI gene expression (Rubio et al., 2001; Bustos et al., 2010). This incomplete suppression implies substantial functional redundancy within the MYB-CC family, with additional regulators contributing to full Pi-starvation transcriptional output (Sun et al., this work). Indeed, PHL2/PHL3 have been proposed to fill part of this gap: they are induced under Pi starvation, bind P1BS-like elements (including within promoters of key PSI genes such as \textit{AtPAP10}), and genetic evidence supports redundancy with PHR1 at the transcriptome scale (Sun et al., this work; Zhou et al., 2008). Consistent with this layered architecture, we also detected a broad sum/ratio association signal at the PHR1-like locus \texttt{SORBI\_3002G161700} (PHL; $p=1.4\times10^{-9}$; 29 sum/ratio phenotypes), supporting the interpretation that variation in upstream Pi-starvation regulators can propagate to coordinated shifts in lipid ratios rather than isolated changes in single species.

In addition to transcriptional regulators, our GWAS identified an enzymatic phosphorus-mobilization component through \texttt{SORBI\_3001G186800} (PAP18; $p=5.7\times10^{-9}$; five individual lipid species). Purple acid phosphatases (PAPs; EC 3.1.3.2) are a large family of non-specific acid phosphatases that release orthophosphate from diverse P-monoesters and anhydrides and are especially important under Pi deprivation (Del Vecchio et al., 2014; Mehra et al., 2017). PAPs include intracellular forms implicated in remobilizing internal Pi reserves and extracellular/secreted forms that scavenge Pi from organic-P pools in the apoplast and rhizosphere; because organic-P can constitute a major fraction of total soil P, extracellular PAP activity is considered a key adaptive strategy under low-P conditions (Del Vecchio et al., 2014; Mehra et al., 2017). Work in Arabidopsis highlights functional specialization among Pi starvation–inducible PAPs (e.g., surface-associated PAP10; secreted PAP12/PAP26; cell-wall PAP25 with potential signaling roles), and rice studies show that secretory PAPs (e.g., OsPAP21b) can improve utilization of external organic P, biomass accumulation, and P content when organic-P is limiting (Del Vecchio et al., 2014; Mehra et al., 2017). Together, the co-occurrence of PHR/PHL regulatory signals and a PAP locus in our lipid GWAS supports a model in which genetic variation in phosphate-starvation sensing and phosphate acquisition/remobilization capacity is coupled to phospholipid remodeling phenotypes under nutrient limitation, while recognizing that GWAS alone cannot establish direct causality or define the immediate biochemical substrates linking these pathways.

\subsection{Glycerolipid buffering and DG--TG control under low-input stress.}

A consistent theme from the lipid-GWAS candidates is that several loci map onto the DG--TG decision point and the upstream supply of glycerol backbones, which together control whether acyl flux is retained in membrane lipids or diverted into neutral-lipid storage. This is the same biochemical junction that multiple studies have identified as stress-relevant in vegetative tissues, where TG can function as a transient “buffer” during nutrient limitation or temperature stress rather than solely as a seed storage product \cite{Zhang2009,Yang2011,Tan2018}. In our dataset, the strongest and most direct evidence for DG$\rightarrow$TG commitment is the DGAT1 locus \texttt{SORBI\_3010G170000}, which was repeatedly identified across five TG molecular species (TG(18:1/18:3/22:0), TG(18:1/20:3/22:0), TG(18:2/18:2/18:4), TG(18:2/20:3/22:0), TG(18:3/18:3/18:3)). DGAT1 catalyzes the final acyl-CoA--dependent conversion of DG into TG, and prior work provides a mechanistic basis for why standing variation at DGAT1 could translate into lipidomic differences under stress: under nitrogen limitation, Arabidopsis leaves accumulate TG alongside increased \textit{DGAT1} and \textit{OLEOSIN1}, and ABA signaling can directly activate \textit{DGAT1} transcription through ABI4 binding to CE1 elements (CACCG) in the DGAT1 promoter; \emph{abi4} mutants show reduced DGAT1 induction and reduced TG accumulation under low N \cite{Yang2011}. Cold stress provides an additional, independent rationale for DGAT1 as a protective valve: DGAT1 is cold responsive (4$^\circ$C), and \emph{dgat1} mutants show chlorosis and increased cell death under cold with decreased TG but increased DG and phosphatidic acid (PA) \cite{Tan2018}. In that model, elevated PA promotes RbohD-dependent ROS formation, and increased DG kinase activity can further channel DG into PA; notably, removing specific \emph{dgk} genes restores cold tolerance, supporting the idea that DGAT1 competes for DG to limit PA/ROS escalation during cold \cite{Tan2018}. Seed phenotypes further emphasize that DGAT1 sits at a quantitatively important flux-control step: \emph{dgat1} reduces seed oil content, and combined disruption with \emph{pdat1} or strong knockdown causes severe TG loss and developmental defects \cite{Zhang2009}. Within this established framework, our DGAT1--TG associations are consistent with natural variation affecting how strongly DG is pulled into TG during low-input/cold conditions, without requiring that DGAT1 be the only driver of the phenotype.

Equally important, our GWAS signals do not point to one-way TG accumulation; instead they implicate TG turnover, which is consistent with a dynamic buffering model in which TG synthesis and degradation are both regulated depending on cellular demand. We identify a TG lipase locus \texttt{SORBI\_3001G041900} associated with multiple TG species (minimum $p = 1.13 \times 10^{-9}$), supporting genetically variable TG mobilization (TG$\rightarrow$DG + FA). In parallel, \texttt{SORBI\_3010G270700} is annotated in pathway resources as participating in triacylglycerol catabolism and appears as a significant locus in the sum/ratio GWAS (minimum $p = 3.07 \times 10^{-8}$), including ratios involving LPC/TG (e.g., Sum\_LPC\_over\_TG). Rather than interpreting these signals as redundant with DGAT1, the combination is biologically coherent: DGAT1-mediated TG formation can buffer acyl flux and constrain DG$\rightarrow$PA diversion under stress, while lipase/catabolic capacity can re-mobilize DG and fatty acids for membrane repair, signaling, or energy demands once conditions change. Importantly, this interpretation stays within what genetics can support—GWAS indicates loci linked to TG pools/ratios, but the direction and timing of flux (net storage vs net mobilization) would require expression, enzyme activity, or perturbation experiments.

Upstream of DG--TG interconversion, the presence of a strong signal at a glycerol-3-phosphate dehydrogenase locus (\texttt{SORBI\_3007G223801}; GPDH annotation in our summaries) provides a plausible “throughput” explanation for why many ratio traits shift together. Plastid-localized glycerol-3-phosphate production can limit the glycerol backbone supply for bulk membrane lipid synthesis: Arabidopsis SFD1/GLY1 encodes a plastid-localized G3pDH required for normal plastid lipid composition, and SFD1 overexpression in rice produces a gain-of-function phenotype with 30--40\% higher total fatty acids and increased plastidic membrane lipids (MGDG, DGDG, PG), accompanied by increased carbon assimilation \cite{Singh2015}. Mechanistic work in rice (an 18:3 plant) further suggests that increased plastidic G3P can be exported to the ER and enhance the eukaryotic pathway for lipid synthesis, shifting the distribution of galactolipid species in a manner consistent with altered DAG supply \cite{Singh2015}. In our sorghum data, a broad ratio-level association at a GPDH/G3P-supply locus is therefore consistent with a model in which variation in glycerol-backbone availability scales lipid synthesis capacity and propagates into many class ratios, including DG-centered ratios, without implying a specific headgroup enzyme as the sole causal step.

Finally, plastid pathway initiation and phospholipid remodeling candidates can be interpreted in ways that remain conservative but mechanistically meaningful. The plastid GPAT/ATS1 enzyme initiates the prokaryotic pathway of thylakoid lipid biosynthesis; classical ats1 mutants show reduced GPAT activity and altered galactolipid fatty-acid profiles, and deeper analyses indicate strong repression leads to severe developmental defects, emphasizing that plastid lipid initiation is tightly coordinated with growth \cite{Nishida1993,Kunst1988}. Thus, a GPAT/ATS1-like association touching anionic/chloroplast-linked lipid ratios is compatible with upstream variation in plastid lipid initiation influencing downstream membrane lipid balance. For the LCAT-like loci (\texttt{SORBI\_3001G103800}, \texttt{SORBI\_3006G214500}), prior work cautions against assuming a single enzymatic function from family membership alone: while some Arabidopsis LCAT-family members include characterized acyltransferases (e.g., PDAT), AT4G19860 (AtLCAT-PLA) is instead a calcium-independent phospholipase A with strong preference for PC headgroups and does not show LCAT/PDAT activity; expression in yeast inhibits growth, plausibly due to toxic LPC accumulation \cite{AtLCATPLAstudy}. Therefore, our LCAT-like GWAS signals are consistent with PC remodeling that can influence lysophospholipid intermediates and DG availability, but discriminating transacylation versus phospholipase-driven remodeling will require orthogonal functional evidence.

Together, these loci support a coherent biochemical axis centered on glycerol-backbone supply (G3P), plastid membrane lipid initiation, and DG--TG partitioning and turnover. This axis provides a parsimonious explanation for why DG- and TG-related species and ratios are repeatedly implicated in the GWAS, while keeping the interpretation appropriately conservative: genetics prioritizes candidate regulators of flux and remodeling, whereas net directionality and stress-stage specificity remain testable hypotheses for follow-up.


\subsection{Nitrogen sensing, amino-acid metabolism, and hormone signaling as upstream drivers of lipid variation.}

Beyond lipid-enzymology candidates, our lipid GWAS also prioritizes loci with clear links to nitrogen (N) acquisition and N-stress physiology, suggesting that standing variation in N signaling and amino-acid homeostasis can propagate into membrane and neutral-lipid phenotypes. One prominent theme is the tryptophan (Trp)–auxin axis. Trp is not only a proteinogenic amino acid but also the major precursor for indole-3-acetic acid (IAA), and Trp-dependent IAA biosynthesis is essential for root and shoot growth programs that determine nutrient capture and stress acclimation \citep{Fu2022,Liu2024,Wang2025}. Under N limitation specifically, Trp metabolism is reconfigured in ways that appear adaptive: low-N tolerant sorghum genotypes show higher endogenous Trp than sensitive genotypes, and exogenous Trp can elevate both Trp and IAA in sorghum roots under low N while promoting root development and improving physiological indices tied to C/N metabolism \citep{Liu2024}. In parallel, the Trp-dependent IAA pathway enzyme TAA1/SAV3 has been implicated as a regulator of chlorophyll accumulation and N assimilation, reinforcing the idea that Trp–IAA metabolism participates in coordinating N status with photosynthetic capacity and growth \citep{Fu2022}. These prior observations provide a biologically grounded context for interpreting GWAS signals at Trp/IAA-related loci: altered auxin-mediated root architecture, together with changes in photosynthetic performance and N assimilation, could plausibly shift carbon allocation and membrane turnover rates, thereby manifesting as changes in lipid species and lipid ratios. Importantly, this is a directional hypothesis consistent with known physiology rather than a claim of direct biochemical catalysis on the associated lipids.

A second, complementary theme centers on nitrate sensing through NRT1.1/CHL1/NPF6.3, which functions as both a dual-affinity nitrate transporter and a nitrate sensor that helps implement the primary nitrate response \citep{Tsay1993,Liu1999,Krouk2010,Sakuraba2021}. Under fluctuating nitrate availability, NRT1.1 mediates a well-studied regulatory switch: in roots it represses components of the high-affinity transport system (NRT2.1/NRT3.1) when nitrate is abundant, but this repression is relieved at low external nitrate (e.g., below \(\sim\)0.5 mM), enabling efficient nitrate scavenging and exploitation of nitrate-rich patches \citep{Krouk2006,Remans2006}. Notably, recent work indicates a divergent shoot role during N deficiency, where NRT1.1 expression can increase in shoots and promoter polymorphisms correlate with improved growth and reduced leaf yellowing, implying that aerial NRT1.1 activity may contribute to maintaining leaf function during N stress \citep{Sakuraba2021}. NRT1.1 also connects nutrient and hormone pathways: it can facilitate auxin uptake in a nitrate-inhibited manner, and nitrate-dependent modulation of auxin movement alters lateral root development (low nitrate promotes basipetal auxin transport out of lateral root tips and represses their growth; high nitrate inhibits this efflux, allowing auxin accumulation and stimulating lateral root growth) \citep{Krouk2010}. In addition, NRT1.1 has nitrate-independent roles in ammonium tolerance and broader metabolic outcomes \citep{Hachiya2011}. Critically for our study design, NRT1.1 is also positioned at N–P crosstalk: it participates in pathways through which N controls the phosphate starvation response and interacts with core PSR regulators including PHR1 and PHO2 \citep{Medici2019}. This offers a mechanistic bridge between the nitrogen-annotated GWAS candidates and the phosphorus-responsive regulatory module we identify elsewhere: genetic variation at NRT1.1-like loci could influence lipid remodeling indirectly by altering N status, root system architecture, auxin signaling, and the integrated N–P stress program that reshapes membrane lipid composition under low-input conditions.

Finally, amino-acid partitioning and glutamine homeostasis provide an additional route by which N status can influence lipid traits. OsATL6 (a tonoplast-localized AAAP-family transporter) is induced by glutamine and ammonium supply and functions primarily to export glutamine from the cytosol into root vacuoles for temporary storage; functional assays (e.g., \textit{Xenopus} oocytes) support unidirectional glutamine efflux, and OsATL6 knockdown reduces glutamine in roots but increases glutamine in xylem sap, indicating that impaired vacuolar storage diverts glutamine toward root-to-shoot allocation \citep{Ogasawara2021}. The spatial and temporal dynamics of OsATL6 accumulation (in cell types near Casparian strips and preceding peaks of ammonium assimilation enzymes) suggest a buffering mechanism that stabilizes glutamine pools during rapid assimilation and regulates shoot supply \citep{Ogasawara2021}. In the context of lipid GWAS, an ATL6-like signal is therefore interpretable as variation in N assimilation/transport buffering that could reshape carbon–nitrogen balance and energy demand, with downstream consequences for lipid synthesis and turnover. Together, the Trp–IAA module, NRT1.1 nitrate sensing (including hormone and N–P integration), and glutamine homeostasis form a coherent nitrogen-stress framework that can explain why nitrogen-related loci appear among genetic predictors of lipid variation under low-input and cold conditions, while remaining appropriately conservative about causality.



\subsection{Cold-associated lipid GWAS prioritizes ABI5 and AP37 regulatory modules}

A key outcome of the cold-associated GWAS candidates is the enrichment of transcriptional regulators that sit at well-established junctions between ABA signaling, canonical cold-response pathways (CBF/COR), and oxidative-stress protection—processes that collectively determine whether membranes remain functional during chilling/freezing. In particular, the bZIP transcription factor \textit{ABI5} is repeatedly positioned across species as a mechanistic link between ABA and cold tolerance, but with context-dependent directionality. In apple, MdABI5 was shown to enhance cold tolerance by directly activating a cold-positive regulator (MdCBF3) while repressing cold-negative regulators (MdJAZ1/3), consistent with a model in which ABI5 can reweight the balance between CBF-driven cold acclimation and jasmonate-associated growth restraint; MdABI5 overexpression improved cold resistance and reduced oxidative damage with increased antioxidant enzyme activities, even though MdABI5 transcript abundance itself was initially suppressed at early cold time points (Deng et al., 2026). :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0} This is important for interpretation because it illustrates a common cold-response pattern: early transcriptional dynamics can be suppressive even when the factor ultimately acts positively at later regulatory stages (e.g., via protein stability, partner interactions, or delayed transcriptional activation). Parallel evidence supports ABI5 participation in ABA-dependent chilling responses in other crops. In banana, MaABI5 contributes to ABA-induced cold tolerance through interaction with a RING-type E3 ubiquitin ligase (MaC3HC4-1), emphasizing that ABI5 function can be modulated post-translationally by ubiquitin-mediated turnover or stabilization mechanisms (Chen et al., 2018). :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1} In cucumber, the WRKY regulator CsWRKY46 is induced by cold and ABA, and transgenic experiments support an ABA-dependent cold-resistance program that includes ABI5-linked regulation (Zhang et al., 2016). :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2} In rice, OsNAC5 fine-tunes cold tolerance by enhancing OsABI5 stability and enabling activation of downstream cold-responsive (COR) genes, and promoter variation in OsABI5 has been implicated in cold tolerance, reinforcing the idea that regulatory variation at the ABI5 node can have phenotypic consequences (Li et al., 2024). :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3} At the same time, rice also highlights that ABI5 can behave as a cost–benefit regulator: OsABI5 is induced by ABA and salt but reported to be suppressed by drought/cold in seedlings, and knockdown/repression can increase general stress tolerance while reducing fertility, indicating that ABI5 can act as a constraint in certain stress contexts or developmental stages (Zou et al., 2008). :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4} Taken together, prior work argues that an ABI5-associated GWAS signal is biologically interpretable as variation in ABA–cold transcriptional wiring (including CBF/COR outputs and ROS management), while cautioning that the \emph{direction} of effect (beneficial vs. constraining) may depend on developmental stage, tissue, and interaction partners.

A second cold-relevant transcriptional module in our candidate set is represented by the AP2-domain factor \textit{AP37}, which has unusually clear functional evidence for low-temperature tolerance. In rice, AP37 transcript abundance responds rapidly to low temperature (reported as induction within \(\sim\)30 minutes, comparable to canonical early cold TF kinetics such as OsDREB1A), indicating that AP37 behaves like an early cold-responsive regulator rather than a late downstream marker (Oh et al., 2009; Dubouzet et al., 2003). :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5} Functional characterization further shows specificity within the AP2 family: constitutive overexpression of AP37 (OsCc1:AP37 plants) increased tolerance to low temperature at the vegetative stage, while the closely related AP59 improved drought/salinity tolerance but did \emph{not} improve low-temperature tolerance. Importantly, this cold tolerance was quantified physiologically—under low-temperature conditions, OsCc1:AP37 plants exhibited \(\sim\)15\% higher maximum photochemical efficiency of PSII (F\(_v\)/F\(_m\)) than non-transgenic controls, whereas OsCc1:AP59 plants did not differ from controls—supporting a genuine protection of photosynthetic machinery under chilling rather than only delayed symptom development (Oh et al., 2009). :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6} The AP37/AP59 divergence also implies that “AP2 domain” annotation alone is insufficient: closely related TFs can separate into stress programs with distinct outputs (cold vs. drought/salinity), which matters for interpreting GWAS candidates.

Crucially, these transcriptional regulators connect naturally to lipid biology without requiring over-interpretation. Plant membranes are frontline targets of cold stress, and cold acclimation is tightly coupled to membrane lipid remodeling (e.g., shifts in fatty-acid unsaturation, phospholipid and plastid-lipid remodeling, and lipid-signaling intermediates) that maintain fluidity and integrity (Barrero-Sicilia et al., 2017). :contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7} Cold also rapidly activates phospholipid signaling, including phosphatidic acid (PA) accumulation through PLD- and PLC/DGK-linked routes, which can feed into downstream stress signaling and ROS-associated outcomes (Arisz et al., 2013; Wu et al., 2022). :contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8} In parallel, multiple stresses—including cold—can drive transient TAG accumulation and lipid-droplet dynamics as a buffering mechanism to sequester potentially toxic lipid intermediates generated during membrane remodeling (Lu et al., 2020). :contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9} Within this framework, cold-associated GWAS signals at ABI5- and AP37-like regulators are consistent with genetic control of upstream stress signaling and transcriptional programs that ultimately shape membrane protection and lipid remodeling states under chilling. However, because GWAS does not establish directionality or direct molecular targets, we interpret these loci conservatively as candidates for regulatory control of cold acclimation pathways that are known to intersect with lipid homeostasis, rather than as direct lipid enzymes.

\subsection*{Triglyceride-based lipid predictors capture growth-related metabolic state and improve XGBoost prediction of plant height}

The predominance of triglyceride modules in plant height prediction is biologically well substantiated. Triglycerides constitute the principal form of energy storage in seeds and vegetative tissues, and their mobilization supplies both carbon skeletons and metabolic energy required for growth processes. In particular, short-chain triglycerides may serve as indicators of metabolic flux through fatty acid biosynthetic pathways that support rapid biomass accumulation. The finding that these associations are consistently observed across independent datasets strengthens confidence that they represent true underlying biological relationships rather than artifacts of model overfitting.

The absence of robust lipid-based predictors for flowering time is itself informative. Floral transition is predominantly regulated by genetic determinants and environmental cues (e.g., photoperiod, vernalization) acting through well-characterized signaling networks. Although lipid metabolic processes intersect with these regulatory pathways, our findings indicate that steady-state lipid profiles obtained at a single developmental stage are insufficient to capture the underlying regulatory complexity governing flowering time specially because the lipid profiles were samples at the XXX stage. 



\section*{Conclusion}

Our multi-omics analysis reveals that the maize phosphorus starvation response is influenced by the leaf developmental stage during the vegetative phase, with older leaves positioned below the collar exhibiting enhanced stress responses characteristic of developmental senescence, which integrate nutrient limitation with natural developmental progression.
The bifurcation of molecular responses into light-harvesting shutdown and senescence acceleration shows coordinated regulation of functionally distinct pathways.
Lipidomic patterns parallel transcriptomic responses, with age-dependent amplification of phospholipid degradation, galactolipid accumulation, and triacylglycerol synthesis.
The divergence of lysophosphatidylethanolamine patterns from 16:3 plant models highlights the importance of lipid metabolism architecture differences between 16:3 and 18:3 plant species.
Despite this strong developmental dependency, the \invfour chromosomal inversion does not substantially modulate phosphorus starvation responses, indicating that its contribution to highland adaptation operates through constitutive effects on developmental timing rather than enhanced nutrient stress tolerance.


\section*{Materials and methods}

\subsection*{Plant Material and Growth Conditions}

In our study, we used the Sorghum Association Panel (SAP), consisting of 400 accessions designed to cover extensive genetic and phenotypic diversity. This collection includes both temperate-adapted breeding lines and tropical landraces. The panel represents five botanical races, bicolor, caudatum, durra, guinea, and kafir, capturing a diverse range of domestication and adaptation processes.

SAP was first genotyped using simple sequence repeat markers, followed by low-coverage genotyping by sequencing (GBS). For a more comprehensive variation set, Boatwright et al. (ref) resequenced all entries using whole genome sequencing (WGS) with an average depth of 38× (ranging from 25–72×). The variant data from WGS revealed approximately 43.98 million polymorphisms, including roughly 38 million SNPs with 5 million small insertions/deletions. While GBS variants were predominantly located in genic regions, the WGS data were more evenly distributed across genic and intergenic regions. Genome-wide linkage disequilibrium is approximately 20 kb, although there were deviations specific to each chromosome. The consequent high-density variant map establishes the resequenced SAP as a valuable tool for examining diversity and conducting genome wide association studies (GWAS).

We evaluated SAP accessions across two different field settings during two growing seasons (2019 and 2022) at the Pee Dee Research and Education Center, Clemson University, Florence, South Carolina. The "control" condition, herein denoted as C, involved standard agronomic inputs with sufficient levels of nitrogen (N) and phosphorus (P) along with a typical planting schedule. In contrast, the "low input" condition, herein denoted as LI,  featured reduced N and P coupled with earlier planting to mimic a cold stress environment.

\subsection*{Lipidomics Analysis}

\paragraph{Sample Preparation and Extraction.} 
Samples were prepared following the standard extraction protocols explained in the hpc1 paper (ref).


\paragraph{Liquid Chromatography-Mass Spectrometry (LC-MS) Analysis.} Lipid extracts were subjected to high-resolution mass spectrometry employing both positive and negative ionization modes to achieve comprehensive lipid coverage. Chromatographic separation was executed utilizing either reversed-phase columns, such as C18, or hydrophilic interaction chromatography (HILIC) columns, contingent upon the polarity of the lipids. In the case of C18 methodologies, the gradient elution commenced at 1 minute and concluded at 8 minutes, succeeded by an isocratic elution phase from 8 to 9.5 minutes. Data preceding 1 minute and subsequent to 9.5 minutes were omitted to eliminate solvent front and late eluting artifacts. For HILIC methodologies, the gradient initiation occurred at 1 minute, terminating at 16.25 minutes, and was followed by an isocratic elution extending until 18.5 minutes; data before 1 minute and after 18.5 minutes were excluded in a similar manner.

\paragraph{Feature Detection and Data Processing.} Raw LC-MS data, in both positive and negative ion modes, were processed utilizing MZmine 2, an open-source software for the analysis of mass spectrometry data (ref). The pipeline encompassed peak detection, chromatogram construction, deconvolution, and isotope filtering, producing a detailed feature table containing mass-to-charge ratio-retention time (mz-rt) pairs. Isotopic peaks were excluded to minimize redundancy, as a single metabolite can yield multiple co-eluting ions, such as adducts and in-source fragments. Therefore, mz-rt duplicates were handled with care, with potential de-adducting considered via MS-FLO when appropriate. We acknowledge that such degeneracy can lead to an inflated number of features compared to the actual number of metabolites present which is we considered during metabolite identification. 


\paragraph{Blank/extraction-control filtering, intensity thresholds, and sparsity pruning.}
To reduce the background and carryover effects, an extraction control filter was implemented at the feature level. For each feature, the maximum intensity was determined across extraction controls (\(a\)) and biological samples (\(b\)). Features for which \(b < 10\times a\) were eliminated. To preclude the exclusion of borderline yet potentially biological signals, a feature was retained should at least one biological sample exceed the extraction control maximum. Furthermore, a minimum average intensity threshold within the treatment groups of interest (\(\sim 10^{6}\) peak height) was imposed to ensure that downstream analyses would emphasize robust signals. At the sample level, any sample exhibiting \(\geq 70\%\) features as zero (or missing) was excluded prior to normalization and statistical analysis. This pruning of sparsity is essential to prevent unstable scaling and spurious differential signals caused by ultra-sparse profiles. 


\paragraph{MS/MS spectral library matching and cross-referencing of IDs.}
For each feature analyzed by MS/MS, the most intense fragmentation spectrum was queried against the GNPS database. Library matches resulted in putative identifications (levels 2/3), potentially including isomers or near-mass analogs. Features without direct matches were eliminated. To enable quantification with identifications, tables were linked using the feature \emph{row ID} from the MZmine peak list and the corresponding \#Scan\# key in the GNPS results, ensuring a one-to-one correspondence between intensities and candidate identifications. In cases where a feature yielded multiple GNPS hits, a single primary annotation was designated by retaining the highest MQScore (cosine similarity). Ties in the values were resolved based on a greater number of shared fragment ions and a smaller precursor mass error (ppm). All other sub-threshold or lower-ranked candidates were retained for verification but were excluded from subsequent statistical analyses.

\paragraph{Systematic Error Removal Using Random Forest (SERRF).}
Following the cleaning process, the data were then used for SERRF normalization (ref). We used the SERRF server (https://slfan2013.github.io/SERRF-online/\#) to obtain the normalized output. After applying SERRF, only biological samples were preserved. Any zeros were substituted with two-thirds of the minimum nonzero value for that feature to prevent potential infinite logarithmic transformations.

\paragraph{Spatial Correction.}
Finally, we conducted an additional quality control step specifically aimed at eliminating any spatial patterns across our experimental trials. This was achieved using the R package \texttt{SpATS} (ref), which applies a two-dimensional P-spline ANOVA surface over the field coordinates. For every lipid feature, we characterized its intensity as
\begin{align}
  y_{ij} &= \mu + f_{\mathrm{row}}(i) + f_{\mathrm{col}}(j) + f_{\mathrm{row,col}}(i,j) + \varepsilon_{ij},
\end{align}

where 
\begin{itemize}
  \item \(f_{\mathrm{row}}\) and \(f_{\mathrm{col}}\) represent smooth functions that model systematic effects across rows and columns, respectively, 
  \item \(f_{\mathrm{row,col}}\) is a smooth interaction surface that handles more complex spatial gradients. 
}  

We utilized the residuals, defined as the difference between observed intensity and the fitted spatial trend, as our final intensity data. This methodology effectively corrects for positional artifacts, such as edge effects, that could interfere with subsequent analyses. Detailed smoothing parameters, including the number of knots, penalty orders, and comprehensive model specifications, can be found in our GitHub repository at \texttt{scripts/spats\_qc.R}.


\paragraph{Lipid Quantification.}
The identified lipid species were organized into lipid classes and subclasses (see Supplementary Table~1) based on Lipid Maps (ref). In each sample, the total intensity for a class was obtained by summing the intensities of the species within that class. To manage variability in signal intensity due to different runs or injections, these class totals were normalized relative to the total ion current (TIC) of the sample. As a result, the relative abundances were presented as percentages of the TIC by adding up intensities across all lipid classes in the sample. For each class and its subclasses, we determined the TIC fraction for each sample and then averaged these percentages across samples for each condition (Control, $n=384$; LowInput, $n=362$). Lipids were categorized into glycerolipid, glycerophospholipid, sphingolipid, sterol, betaine lipid, fatty acid, ether lipid, and terpenoid. Refer to Supplementary Table 2 for the full list. 

\paragraph{Lipid Ratio Identification.}
To determine the key lipid ratios most significantly influenced by the shift from C to LI, we utilized the cumulative class-level abundances of lipids and calculated all possible pairwise ratios. These calculations were then analyzed through orthogonal partial least squares–discriminant analysis (OPLS-DA) employing the \emph{ropls} package in R (v3.3.2). To identify the most distinguishing ratios, we analyzed the Variable Importance in Projection (VIP) scores generated by OPLS-DA. A threshold of 1 for VIP was used. These high-ranking ratios enhanced the multivariate differentiation between C and LI samples. The model is explained in detail below. 

\paragraph{Lipid Ratio Calculation.}
To quantify condition specific shifts between lipid classes, we used  log\textsubscript{10} ratios of class‐level relative abundances. Normalization proceeded as follows:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Per‐sample TIC normalization.}  
For each sample, preprocessed peak intensities were summed (total ion current, TIC) and each species intensity was divided by the sample TIC to yield a relative abundance:
\[
\text{Relative Abundance} = \frac{\text{Intensity}}{\text{TIC}}.
\]

\item \textbf{Log\textsubscript{10} transformation with a pseudo‐count.}  
Because many relative abundances are very small or zero, we added half of the smallest nonzero value in that sample ($\varepsilon$) and computed 
\[
\log_{10}(x + \varepsilon),
\]
to stabilize variance.

\item \textbf{Class‐level aggregation (mean log\textsubscript{10}).}  
Species were grouped into lipid classes (e.g., PC, PE, DGDG, MGDG, TG, DG; see Supp.\ Table~2).  
For each sample $i$ and class $c$, we averaged the species‐level logs:
\[
\text{class\_log}_{i,c} = \frac{1}{n_c} \sum_{k \in c} \log_{10}\!\left(\frac{\text{Intensity}_{i,k}}{\text{TIC}_i} + \varepsilon_i\right).
\]
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Ratios (log scale).}
Pairwise \emph{log‐ratios} were computed as differences of class logs, e.g.
\[
\text{DGDG/PC} = \text{class\_log}_{\text{DGDG}} - \text{class\_log}_{\text{PC}} 
= \log_{10}\!\left(\frac{\text{DGDG}}{\text{PC}}\right).
\]
Positive values indicate enrichment of the numerator class (LI $>$ C if the LI–C effect is positive), and negative values indicate the reverse.

\paragraph{Statistical Tests.}
For class-level comparisons, we computed log-ratios of class-wise abundances (e.g., $\log_{10}(\text{PS}/\text{SQDG})$), which inherently address the compositional structure of the data. For each of the 33 class ratios, we compared LI and C using the two-sample Wilcoxon rank-sum (Mann–Whitney) test. To ascertain that the observed effects reflected population-level patterns rather than being driven by individual outlier genotypes, we quantified leave-one-out jackknife stability, defined as the proportion of single-sample omissions for which the sign of $\text{median}_{LI} - \text{median}_{C}$ was preserved. A stability value of 1.0 indicates that the direction of the effect remains unchanged upon removal of any single sample. For each ratio, we report: 
\begin{itemize}                                                                                    
\item \texttt{n\_C}, \texttt{n\_LI} = sample sizes.                                                
\item \texttt{median\_C}, \texttt{median\_LI} = group medians of the log-ratio.                    
\item \texttt{effect\_log10} = median difference on the log$_{10}$ scale ($\text{median}_{LI} -        
\text{median}_{C}$).                                                                               
\item \texttt{effect\_fc} = fold-change ($10^{\texttt{effect\_log10}}$); values $>1$ indicate      
higher ratios in LI.                                                                               
\item \texttt{p\_adj\_BH} = Benjamini–Hochberg adjusted $p$-value (FDR, $m=33$).
\item \texttt{jackknife\_stability} = leave-one-out sign stability (1.0 = population-wide effect). 
\end{itemize}                                                                                         

For individual species, we applied the centered log-ratio (CLR) transformation to address compositional constraints as $\text{CLR}(x_i) = \ln(x_i / g(\mathbf{x}))$, where $g(\mathbf{x})$ is the geometric mean across all species within a sample. For each of the 172 common species, we report:                                       
\begin{itemize}                                                                                    
\item \texttt{median\_CLR\_C}, \texttt{median\_CLR\_LI} = group medians of CLR values.             
\item \texttt{effect\_CLR} = median CLR difference; \texttt{effect\_fc} = $e^{\texttt{effect\_CLR}}$.
\item \texttt{p\_adj\_BH} = BH-adjusted $p$-value.
\item \texttt{jackknife\_stability} = leave-one-out sign stability.    
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Principal Component Analysis (PCA)}  

We performed three complementary PCA workflows in R using \emph{stats} package (ref).  First, we ran PCA on the individual lipid species abundances.  Second, we summed abundances by lipid class (SuppTable1) (e.g. TG, DG, PC, MGDG, SQDG) and repeated PCA to highlight broader shifts in lipids. Third, we computed key log ratios metrics using OPLS-DA (explained below) and carried out PCA on these as well. In all cases, data were mean–centered and scaled prior to analysis. For each PCA, we retained the first two principal components for visualization. 


\subsection*{Genome-wide Association Studies (GWAS)} 

GWAS analyses were carried out for each lipid trait under each condition using the mixed linear model (MLM) featured in GEMMA (v2.3) (ref). To address population structure and relatedness, a centered relatedness matrix (kinship) was computed from SNP genotype data. For each lipid trait, the MLM was applied using the kinship matrix to handle population stratification effects. Besides individual traits, GWAS was also applied to summed lipid classes and all possible ratios (refer to Supplementary Table 1), and the first two PCs of the summed classes. GWAS analysis was conducted on the first two PCs for each class. A significance threshold of \(-\log_{10}(p) \geq 7\) was employed in order to account for multiple comparisons. 
%In contrast, a more lenient threshold of \(-\log_{10}(p) \geq 5\) was applied to all other analyses.

\subsection*{Gene Annotation}

SNPs were aligned with the Sorghum bicolor reference genome v3.1 (BTx623). For each marker, a 50 kb segment was designated, spanning 25 kb on either side, and all gene models within this area were retrieved. Functional annotations and homology were obtained from Phytozome (https://phytozome.jgi.doe.gov), SorghumBase (https://sorghumbase.com), and TAIR for corresponding Arabidopsis thaliana orthologs. Genes with known roles in N, P, cold tolerance, or lipid metabolism were specifically noted. We aggregated the frequency of each candidate gene within all lipid GWAS findings and marked those with the highest recurrence showing -log10(p-values) of 7 or greater.

\subsection*{Orthogonal Projections to Latent Structures–Discriminant Analysis (OPLS–DA)}

OPLS–DA was employed to identify the lipid class ratios that most effectively distinguish between C and LI while reducing unrelated variance. The analysis was confined to ratios from glycero- and glycerophospholipid classes, specifically TG, DG, MG, DGDG, MGDG, PC, LPC, PE, LPE, PA, PS, and PG. All possible pairwise ratios between the mean log relative abundances of classes were computed as explained above. OPLS–DA was conducted using the \texttt{ropls} R package (v1.34.0), wherein the ratio matrix was denoted as $\mathbf{X}$ and the response $Y$ was coded as lipid class. A single predictive component (\texttt{predI = 1}) was defined, whereas the number of orthogonal components was determined through cross-validation (\texttt{orthoI = NA}). The decomposition is:
\[
  \mathbf{X} = T_{p} P_{p}^{T} \;+\; T_{o} P_{o}^{T} \;+\; E,
\]
where,
\begin{itemize}
  \item \(T_{p}\) and \(P_{p}\) are the predictive score and loading matrices capturing variation correlated with \(Y\),
  \item \(T_{o}\) and \(P_{o}\) are the orthogonal score and loading matrices capturing structured variation orthogonal to \(Y\),
  \item \(E\) is the residual matrix representing unexplained variation.
\end{itemize}

To address the issue of overfitting and adjust for differing sample sizes (C: $n=394$, LI: $n=363$), we employed a stratified seven-fold cross-validation approach with folds that are balanced to estimate $R^{2}_{Y}$ (the variance in $Y$ explained) and $Q^{2}$ (cross-validated predictivity). The significance of the model was evaluated using 500 label permutations. One-sided exact $p$-values were derived as $(\#\{\text{perm} \geq \text{obs}\} + 1)/(N_{\text{perm}} + 1)$. Furthermore, we present $R^{2}_{X}$ (the fraction of $X$ variance captured on the predictive axis) to enhance the interpretability of the score plots.

Ratios that discriminate between C and LI were prioritized based on Variable Importance in Projection (VIP) scores, with VIP $> 1$ as the threshold. Since VIP signifies contribution rather than direction, the effect size direction was separately summarized through the calculation of group medians of the log ratios. 

\subsubsection*{Lipid Metabolic Network Analysis (LINEX2)}

We used the Lipid Network Explorer (LINEX2; \url{https://exbio.wzw.tum.de/linex/}) to reconstruct lipid networks and to identify condition enriched subgraphs for C vs. LI, considering both the ratio and difference between them. Lipid names were standardized to align with species notation (class plus acyl composition). LINEX2 constructs a global species network based on curated reaction rules, including headgroup interconversions, (de)acylation/editing, elongation, desaturation, and overlays a quantitative association structure (Spearman correlations across samples) onto reaction edges. For enrichment, LINEX2 calculates substrate–product change scores for each reaction reagrading the C vs. LI ratio and difference, employing a greedy local-search procedure to identify subgraphs that optimize the average substrate and product change. Since, \textit{Sorghum bicolor} is not implemented as a default organism in LINEX2, we selected \textit{Oryza sativa} (OSA) as the reference species for network construction.


\subsubsection*{Lipid Ontology Enrichment and Hierarchical Classification (LION/web)}

We conducted functional ontology for lipids using LION/web (Molenaar \emph{et al.}, 2019; \url{http://www.lipidontology.com}). Lipid nomenclature was standardized according to LIPID MAPS annotations. The default settings of LION/web were applied for enrichment statistics and multiple-testing correction. We retained terms for $q \leq 0.10$. A hierarchical classification analysis of individual lipids and functional ontologies was also performed using the LION/web.


\subsection*{Gradient boosting modeling and module-based SHAP interpretation}

\paragraph{Lipid data processing.}
To achieve variance stabilization and address zero values, each lipid intensity was log\textsubscript{10}-transformed with a pseudocount of 1:
\[
L_{ij}^{\prime} = \log_{10}(L_{ij} + 1),
\]
where $L_{ij}$ is the raw intensity of lipid $j$ in sample $i$. Following transformation, lipid columns were median-centered across samples through feature-wise subtraction of the column median, effectively removing global offsets while maintaining inter-sample variability.

\paragraph{Phenotype and population structure covariates.}
The phenotypic traits for SAP include plant height (PH) and days to anthesis (DTA) (Supp.\ Table~10), which served as response variables. To reduce the likelihood of the model capturing population structure rather than biological associations, both response variables and lipid predictors underwent adjustment through residualization with respect to the first three principal components (PCs), employing ordinary least squares (OLS) regression.

Let $y$ denote the phenotype vector and $X_{\mathrm{PC}}$ the PC design matrix (with intercept). We computed the residual phenotype as
\[
y_{\mathrm{adj}} = y - X_{\mathrm{PC}}(X_{\mathrm{PC}}^{\top}X_{\mathrm{PC}})^{-1}X_{\mathrm{PC}}^{\top}y,
\]
i.e., the residuals from the regression $y \sim X_{\mathrm{PC}}$.
For the lipid matrix $L$ (samples $\times$ features), we removed PC effects feature-wise via the same projection:
\[
L_{\mathrm{adj}} = L - X_{\mathrm{PC}}(X_{\mathrm{PC}}^{\top}X_{\mathrm{PC}})^{-1}X_{\mathrm{PC}}^{\top}L.
\]
The adjusted lipid matrix $L_{\mathrm{adj}}$ and the residual phenotype $y_{\mathrm{adj}}$ were used for all downstream modeling. Importantly, residualization was performed separately within each cross-validation fold to prevent data leakage: coefficients were estimated on training data only and applied to both training and test sets.

\paragraph{Lipid module construction via correlation clustering.}
Due to high correlation among lipid species within the same biochemical class, interpreting SHAP values for individual lipids can be misleading---importance tends to be distributed across correlated features, making it difficult to identify which lipids (or lipid programs) are truly predictive. To address this, we grouped lipids into correlation-based modules prior to interpretation.

We computed the pairwise Spearman correlation matrix across all lipid features and converted it to a distance matrix:
\[
D_{jk} = 1 - |r_{jk}|,
\]
where $r_{jk}$ is the Spearman correlation between lipids $j$ and $k$. Hierarchical clustering (average linkage) was performed on this distance matrix, and modules were identified using the dynamic tree cut algorithm (\texttt{dynamicTreeCut} in R) with \texttt{deepSplit = 2} and a minimum cluster size of 3. Lipids not assigned to any cluster were treated as singleton modules. This procedure yielded 27 lipid modules, each characterized by its dominant lipid class (e.g., TG, PC, DG).

\paragraph{XGBoost model training and cross-validation.}
We employed gradient-boosted trees (XGBoost) to model the PC-residualized phenotype as a function of the adjusted lipid features. XGBoost was chosen over random forests for its typically superior performance on tabular data and its built-in L1/L2 regularization, which encourages sparsity when features are correlated.

The model was trained with the following hyperparameters: learning rate $\eta = 0.1$, maximum tree depth = 6, row subsampling = 0.8, column subsampling = 0.8, L2 regularization $\lambda = 1$, and L1 regularization $\alpha = 0.5$. Models were trained for 150--200 boosting rounds.

To assess generalization performance, we implemented 5-fold cross-validation with PC residualization performed within each fold. For each fold, we computed:
\[
R^2 = 1 - \frac{\sum_{i}(y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2}{\sum_{i}(y_i - \bar{y})^2}, \quad
\mathrm{RMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i}(\hat{y}_i - y_i)^2}, \quad
\mathrm{MAE} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i}|\hat{y}_i - y_i|.
\]
We report the mean $\pm$ standard deviation of $R^2$ across folds.

\paragraph{SHAP computation and module-level aggregation.}
For model interpretation, a final XGBoost model was trained on all samples (after PC residualization). SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) values were computed using the \texttt{shapviz} package in R, which provides exact TreeSHAP values for gradient-boosted tree ensembles. This yielded an $n \times p$ matrix of SHAP values $\phi_{ij}$, representing the contribution of lipid $j$ to the prediction for sample $i$.

To obtain module-level importance, we aggregated SHAP values within each module. For module $m$ containing lipids $\mathcal{L}_m$, we computed the module SHAP for each sample as:
\[
\Phi_{im} = \sum_{j \in \mathcal{L}_m} \phi_{ij},
\]
and the global module importance as:
\[
\overline{|\Phi|}_m = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}|\Phi_{im}|.
\]
Modules were ranked by $\overline{|\Phi|}_m$ in descending order.

\paragraph{Bootstrap stability analysis.}
To assess the stability of module importance rankings, we performed bootstrap resampling (50--100 iterations). In each bootstrap iteration, we: (1) resampled $n$ observations with replacement, (2) trained an XGBoost model on the bootstrap sample, (3) computed SHAP values and module-level aggregations, and (4) recorded the mean absolute module SHAP. From the bootstrap distribution, we computed 95\% confidence intervals for each module's importance. This procedure identifies modules whose importance is robust to sampling variability, as opposed to those whose rankings are unstable.

\paragraph{Cross-dataset and cross-environment validation.}
For plant height, we validated our findings using an independent phenotype dataset from Boyles et al. We computed module SHAP importance separately for each dataset and assessed concordance by: (1) counting the overlap of top-10 modules between datasets, and (2) computing the Pearson correlation of module importance scores across all modules.

For flowering time, we extended the analysis across five environments (our DTA data plus four environments from Wei et al.: MI20, NE20, IA21\_1, IA21\_2). For each environment, we computed module SHAP importance and identified modules that consistently appeared in the top 10 across multiple environments. This cross-environment consistency analysis reveals lipid programs with reproducible associations, even when overall predictive power is limited.
